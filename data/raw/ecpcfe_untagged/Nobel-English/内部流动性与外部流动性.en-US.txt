<seg id="1"> Prologue</seg>
<seg id="2"> Why do financial institutions, industrial companies and households hold low-yielding money balances, Treasury bills, and other short-term assets? The standard answer to this question, dating back at least to Keynes (1936), Hicks (1967), and Gurley and Shaw (1960), is that these assets are ḂḞliquidḂḟ as they allow their owners to better weather income shortages.</seg>
<seg id="3"> It is unclear, though, why an economic agentḂŸs ability to withstand shocks would not be better served by the broader concept of net wealth, including stocks and long-term bonds.</seg>
<seg id="4"> While some forms of equity, such as private equity, may not be readily sold at a ḂḞfair price,Ḃḟ many long-term securities are traded on active organized exchanges; for example, liquidating oneḂŸs position in an open-ended S&amp;P 500 index fund can be performed quickly and at low transaction costs.</seg>
<seg id="5"> For some reason a big part of the agentsḂŸ net wealth is not liquid and cannot be used as a substitute for liquid assets.</seg>
<seg id="6"> This explains why the yield on liquid assets is lower than could be expected from standard economic models (the ḂḞrisk-free rate puzzleḂḟ).</seg>
<seg id="7"> The standard theory of general equilibrium offers no explanation for this phenomenon.</seg>
<seg id="8"> In the ArrowẀCDebreu model, and its variants, economic agents are subject to a single budget constraint, implying that the consumersḂŸ feasible consumption sets and the firmsḂŸ feasible production sets only depend on their wealth.</seg>
<seg id="9"> Similarly financial institutions and industrial companies pay a lot of attention to risk management.</seg>
<seg id="10"> They hedge against liquidity risks using short-term securities, credit facilities, currency swaps, and similar instruments, adjusting their positions to meet future liquidity needs in the most efficient way. These activities cost billions of dollars.</seg>
<seg id="11"> Yet, received theory is not of much help in explaining all the resources and attention spent on them.</seg>
<seg id="12"> In an ArrowẀCDebreu world it does not mat-ter whether economic agents make their consumption and production plans at the initial date or make these decisions later on provided that they can contract on transfers of numẀḊraire from one period to the next (Arrow 1970).</seg>
<seg id="13"> In particular, the ModiglianiẀCMiller (1958) irrelevance results imply that the hoarding of liquidity or the hedging of liquidity risk (through choice of leverage, dividend payments, etc.) do not affect a firmḂŸs value.</seg>
<seg id="14"> The purpose of this book is to offer an explanation of the demand for and supply of liquid assets.</seg>
<seg id="15"> We use insights from modern corporate finance to study how such a theory can explain the pricing of assets, the role of liquidity management, decisions on real investments, and also how this theory relates to some classic themes in macroeconomics and in international finance.</seg>
<seg id="16"> Macroeconomic policy rests on the presumption that the government can do things that the market cannot.Foremost among these is the provision of liquidity.The government provides liquidity in a variety of ways: through industry and banking bailouts, deposit insurance, the discount window, open-market operations, implicit insurance against major accidents or epidemics, unemployment insurance, social security, debt management, and so forth.</seg>
<seg id="17"> The result is often a redistribution of income from consumers to producers or from future generations to current ones.</seg>
<seg id="18"> The Ricardian equivalence theorem (Barro 1974), the macro-economic counterpart of the ModiglianiẀCMiller theorem, suggests that these activities are useless, since economic agents can replicate privately optimal outcomes by undoing whatever the government does.</seg>
<seg id="19"> In this book we depart from the ArrowẀCDebreu paradigm in a small but important way: we assume that some part of a firmḂŸs income stream cannot be promised or pledged to investors. Outside investors must share the firmḂŸs income with insiders (large shareholder, managers, employees) either because the latter enjoy perks, or can divert resources, or because insiders may exert insufficient effort and so must be given a share of the proceeds to refrain from engaging in moral hazard.</seg>
<seg id="20"> Adverse selection also limits the extent to which firms can pledge their future income to investors.</seg>
<seg id="21"> Except for part IV of the book, we assume that arbitrary state-contingent financial claims can be written on the pledgeable part of firm income.</seg>
<seg id="22"> But given partial nonpledgeability, the income base on which various kinds of financial claims can be built is smaller than in the ArrowẀCDebreu world.</seg>
<seg id="23"> We also assume that consumers cannot pledge any of their future income. There are several reasons why this is by and large reality.</seg>
<seg id="24"> One reason is that some agents may not participate in markets; they may be too young or not yet born. This type of market incompleteness has been thoroughly investigated in the overlapping-generations literature, starting with Allais (1947), Samuelson (1958), and Diamond (1965).</seg>
<seg id="25"> Second, consumers can pledge only a small share of their future labor income for institutional reasons (limited liability, limited slavery, priority of tax claims), verifiability problems (a liability-ridden individual may move abroad), and incentive considerations (future income is endogenous).</seg>
<seg id="26"> The key implication of nonpledgeability is that firms (as well as consumers) can count on liquidating or using as collateral only part of their wealth whenever they need funds.</seg>
<seg id="27"> Consequently they must prepare themselves for adverse financial shocks by hoarding liquid assets or by contracting in other ways for the provision of liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="28"> Firms are willing to pay a premium for liquidity services.</seg>
<seg id="29"> We show that in general, they have to do so because limited pledgeability also constrains the supply of liquid assets. It reduces the amount of wealth in the economy, which in turn limits the ability of investors to promise, in a credible manner, future financing of firms.</seg>
<seg id="30"> This observation gives rise to a demand for liquidity (stores of value) that can transfer wealth from today to tomorrow as well as across states of nature tomorrow.</seg>
<seg id="31"> We make a distinction between inside (aggregate) liquidity and outside (aggregate) liquidity, depending on the source of the pledgeable income.2 When the pledgeable income is generated by the corporate sector, the claims on it constitute inside liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="32"> All claims on goods and services outside the corporate sector constitute outside liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="33"> A basic question we will address concerns the adequacy of inside liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="34"> When the corporate sector cannot generate enough inside liquidity, or when it is too expensive to do so, it will make use of outside liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="35"> There are three sources of outside liquidity: (1) consumers, who can securitize their assets, notably the houses they own; (2) the government, which can issue claims backed by its exclusive right to tax consumers and producers; and (3) international financial markets, which can offer liquidity in the form of claims on international goods and services.3 We will pay special attention to how the government supplies liquidity alone and in conjunction with international investors.</seg>
<seg id="36"> The book is organized as follows.</seg>
<seg id="37"> Part I builds the foundations for the corporate demand for liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="38"> Chapter 1 introduces a simple model of credit rationing with constant returns to scale.</seg>
<seg id="39"> Credit rationing of some kind is essential for corporate liquidity demand.</seg>
<seg id="40"> Chapter 2 introduces the workhorse model of liquidity demand that we will use throughout the book.</seg>
<seg id="41"> In this model, firms with limited pledgeability must plan their liquidity in advance.</seg>
<seg id="42"> Firms demand liquidity because they want to insure themselves against credit rationing.</seg>
<seg id="43"> Through mechanisms such as credit lines or credit default swaps, investors commit themselves to supplying funds in states of nature in which they would not naturally have done so, namely in states in which liquidity needs exceed the future income that can be pledged to investors.</seg>
<seg id="44"> We also examine the provision of liquidity by investors who cannot perfectly monitor the firmsḂŸ use of funding.</seg>
<seg id="45"> Chapter 2 also compares our model of corporate liquidity demand with the celebrated model of consumer liquidity demand by Bryant (1980) and Diamond and Dybvig (1983).</seg>
<seg id="46"> While the two types of models differ in many respects, we show that there is a close formal relationship between them as well.</seg>
<seg id="47"> Furthermore the two can readily be merged into a single framework in which corporations and consumers compete for liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="48"> Part II of the book (chapters 3 and 4) develops our main analysis of liquidity supply and liquidity pricing.</seg>
<seg id="49"> Chapter 3 asks whether the private sector provides enough aggregate liquidity on its own. That is, do the firms in the aggregate create enough pledgeable incomeḂẂinside liquidityḂẂto support the financial claims necessary for implementing a second-best, state-contingent production plan?</seg>
<seg id="50"> The answer is yes if the corporate sector is a net borrower and the firmsḂŸ liquidity shocks are idiosyncratic.</seg>
<seg id="51"> In that case the second-best plan can be implemented by each firm holding a share of the market index.</seg>
<seg id="52"> However, if the corporate sector is a net lender, there will always be a shortage of aggregate liquidity. The same is true if all firms are hit by the same (macroeconomic) shock and this shock is sufficiently large.</seg>
<seg id="53"> A shortage of liquidity induces the private sector to try and create more stores of value, albeit at a cost.</seg>
<seg id="54"> This may involve investing in projects that deliver a safe income or making changes in governance structures that raise the corporationsḂŸ pledgeable incomeḂẂsuch as going public, improving monitoring systems, or employing financial innovations that enable a more efficient use of collateral.</seg>
<seg id="55"> Examples of the latter include bilateral and triparty repos (legal innovations that free posting of collateral from the vagaries of bankruptcy processes), and securitization, which transforms illiquid, low-grade loans into publicly traded assets of higher quality.</seg>
<seg id="56"> Such efforts fit well with de SotoḂŸs (2003) view that a major role of a financial system is to transform ḂḞdead capitalḂḟ into ḂḞlive capital.Ḃḟ He thought of the opportunities to create collateral in developing economies.</seg>
<seg id="57"> What seems to have take place at the beginning of the new century is that excesss savings from China, and other emerging economies with underdeveloped financial markets, flowed to developed countries, and especially the United States which could meet the increased demand for stores of value at a lower cost.</seg>
<seg id="58"> Chapter 3 also offers a preliminary analysis of the pricing of outside liquidity and assets, which in broad terms fits the empirical evidence on liquidity premia of Treasury bonds in Krishnamurthy and Vissing-Jorgenson (2010).</seg>
<seg id="59"> Chapter 4 goes on to develop a general equilibrium model in which only pledgeable income can be used as the basis for contingent claims.</seg>
<seg id="60"> While the basic logic of our Liquidity Asset Pricing Model (LAPM) is identical to that of the ArrowẀCDebreu model, LAPM prices do not merely reflect the yield of the assets, but also the value that they bring as collateral, which helps firms withstand liquidity shocks.</seg>
<seg id="61"> The chapter also illustrates how one can use state prices to derive an optimal policy for risk management at the firm level.</seg>
<seg id="62"> If the corporate sector cannot produce enough inside liquidity it may obtain insurance from providers of outside liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="63"> More generally, firms will turn to outside liquidity whenever it is less expensive than inside liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="64"> In part III (chapters 5 and 6) we study the supply of outside liquidity by the government as well as international investors.</seg>
<seg id="65"> Our main interest is in understanding how the government should manage public liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="66"> In chapter 5 the government acts without the help of international investors.</seg>
<seg id="67"> Given that consumers cannot directly pledge their future income to firms, at least in states of nature where the latter are short of liquidity, we argue that the state, through its regalian taxation power, can increase the pledgeability of consumersḂŸ future income and thereby create liquidity for the corporate sector.</seg>
<seg id="68"> Consumers and firms can be made better off by having the government act as an insurance broker, transferring funds from consumers to firms when the latter are hit by aggregate liquidity shocks.</seg>
<seg id="69"> We study government policy assuming that an explicit insurance contract can be drawn up ex ante, but we argue that a number of ex post policy interventions emulate the patterns of optimal government insurance. In fact the ability of the government to provide liquidity ex post gives it a potential advantage over privately supplied liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="70"> Privately supplied liquidity often requires ex ante investments in short-term assets, incurring an opportunity cost whether or not liquidity is needed. In contrast, an ex post government policy that does not waste liquidity can be much cheaper, especially when covering liquidity shortages that occur rarely.</seg>
<seg id="71"> Chapter 6 pursues the analysis of public provision of liquidity by asking whether the presence of efficient international financial markets could eliminate all potential liquidity shortages. The answer is no, in general.</seg>
<seg id="72"> A countryḂŸs access to international financial markets is limited by its ability to generate pledgeable income that is tradable.</seg>
<seg id="73"> We study the relationship between international and domestic liquidity in an open economy with both tradable and nontradable goods and conclude that when there is a shortage of international liquidity, the insights about the value of domestic liquidity continue to hold.</seg>
<seg id="74"> Part IV (chapters 7 and 8) departs from the assumption of perfect coordination of liquidity within the corporate sector and look at situations where each firm individually arranges its own supply of liquidity without any ex ante coordination with the other firms. The only type of coordination occurs ex post in spot markets that can reallocate liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="75"> One could ask how close this kind of self-provided liquidity arrangement comes to the second best. We show that it will, in general, not replicate the second-best optimum because firms may hoard either too much or too little liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="76"> Yet the government may be unable to improve on the situation, in contrast to the large literature on incomplete insurance markets.</seg>

<seg id="80"> I Basics of Leverage and Liquidity</seg>
<seg id="81"> In standard microeconomic theory a firm that confronts financial needs can meet these needs as they arise by taking out loans or by issuing new securities whose repayments and returns are secured by the cash flows that the firm generates.</seg>
<seg id="82"> As long as the net present value of a re-investment is positive, investors will agree to supply the needed funds.</seg>
<seg id="83"> Reality is very different.</seg>
<seg id="84"> Firms keep a close watch on their current and forecasted cash positions to ensure that their essential liquidity needs can be met at all times. They do not wait until the cash register is empty.</seg>
<seg id="85"> To guard against liquidity shortages, firms arrange financing in advance using both the asset and the liability sides of their balance sheets.</seg>
<seg id="86"> On the asset side, they may hoard liquidity by buying Treasury bills and other safe assets that can be easily sold when necessary.</seg>
<seg id="87"> On the liability side, they may take out credit lines or issue securities that give them flexibility in their management of cash, such as long-term debt, preferred equity, and straight equity.</seg>
<seg id="88"> The recent subprime crisis is a cogent demonstration of how costlyḂẂand in some cases impossibleḂẂit can be to face a maturity mismatch and to seek financing in times of distress.</seg>
<seg id="89"> Financial institutions have been struggling to replace with alternative sources of funding the short-term, market-based financing that they had grown used to in boom times.</seg>
<seg id="90"> At the same time they have been forced to deliver significantly by selling assets at distressed prices.</seg>
<seg id="91"> The troubles in the financial sector in turn have shut off normal credit channels for the nonfinancial sector, causing bankruptcies and distress throughout the economy.</seg>
<seg id="92"> A crisis of this magnitude is obviously rare, but it certainly is a stark reminder of how important it is to think about oneḂŸs liquidity needs in advance rather than wait until the need materializes.</seg>
<seg id="93"> By now there are several related theories in modern corporate finance that can explain why firms demand liquidity. Each theory provides a rationale for why a firm wants to buy insurance against higher credit costs or outright credit rationing stemming from information problems.</seg>
<seg id="94"> In von Thadden (1995) a firm that waits may be unable to get access to funding because of adverse selection problems. Adverse selection can make it very costly for a firm to obtain funding and in the worst case, asset and credit markets may dry up entirely.</seg>
<seg id="95"> The cost of ḂḞsignal-jammingḂḟ is an alternative reason for advanced funding.</seg>
<seg id="96"> If financiers base tomorrowḂŸs refinancing decision on a firmḂŸs current performance, competitors have an incentive to prey on each other by choosing hidden actions, such as secret price cuts, that hurt rivals and make them look financially weak.</seg>
<seg id="97"> In equilibrium, the market can see through this, but nevertheless the wasteful signaling behavior is rational.</seg>
<seg id="98"> To prevent such predation, a firm has an incentive to secure its funds in advance (in a publicly observable fashion).</seg>
<seg id="99"> Throughout this book we employ a very simple information-based model as the driver of liquidity demand.</seg>
<seg id="100"> The key assumption is that firms are unable to pledge all of the returns from their investments to the investors.</seg>
<seg id="101"> InsidersḂẂfrom control shareholders to managers to ordinary employeesḂẂenjoy private benefits of various kinds that create a wedge between total returns and pledgeable returns.</seg>
<seg id="102"> The insiders may consume employment rents, enjoy perks, engage in empire building, or receive inducements to perform that give them an extra share of the firmḂŸs payoff. To the extent that these benefits cannot be transferred or paid for up front, a part of the total surplus will not be pledgeable to outside investors.</seg>
<seg id="103"> This partial nonpledgeability of investment returns can make it more costly or impossible to finance a project.</seg>
<seg id="104"> And even when the project can get off the ground, private benefits can make future reinvestments difficult.</seg>
<seg id="105"> In general, we show that firms will face credit rationing not just at the start but also in the future.</seg>
<seg id="106"> There is, however, a key difference between credit rationing at the initial financing stage and the refinancing stages: in the latter case, credit rationing can be anticipated and therefore measures can be taken to insure against it.</seg>
<seg id="107"> We begin by showing (in chapter 1) how a simple moral-hazard model with limited liability can give rise to a wedge between total and pledgeable income.</seg>
<seg id="108"> Chapter 2 introduces a ḂḞliquidity shockḂḟ that may hit the firm after it has sunk its initial investment. This gives rise to a demand for liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="109"> We show that if the potential liquidity shock is severe enough, the firm needs to arrange financing in advance to avoid facing costly credit rationing.</seg>
<seg id="110"> Firms face both a solvency concern (the need to be adequately capitalized in order to attract financing in the first place) and a liquidity concern (the risk of facing solvency concerns in the future).</seg>
<seg id="111"> The optimal design exhibits a trade-off between liquid and illiquid investments: the higher the insurance purchased by the firm in the form of a liquidity backup, the lower the investment in illiquid assets.</seg>
<seg id="112"> Put differently, the firm can opt for a large scale with a maturity mismatch (much long-term illiquid assets and little short-term liquidity), or for a smaller, but more secure balance sheet.</seg>
<seg id="113"> Appendix 2A shows how private information about the magnitude of the liquidity shock limits contracting possibilities and affects the optimal solution and the demand for liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="114"> Appendix 2B addresses an obvious question: how does our model of liquidity demand by firms compare with the extensively studied case of consumer liquidity demand?</seg>

<seg id="182"> At the margin, capital-constrained firms will therefore accept safer projects at the expense of lower expected returns.</seg>
<seg id="183"> Diversification</seg>
<seg id="184"> A variant on the theme above occurs when diversifica-tion helps reduce the need for own funds.</seg>
<seg id="185"> Suppose that a single project can be replaced by two identical, half-sized projects of the sort we have discussed. Assume that the projects are stochastically independent and that the entrepreneur chooses separately, but simultaneously, whether to be diligent in executing each project.</seg>
<seg id="186"> One can show that in this case the optimal incentive scheme pays the entrepreneur a positive amount only when both projects succeed.</seg>
<seg id="187"> The entrepreneur in effect pledges the rewards that accrue from a successful project as collateral for the other project. This maximizes the pledgeable income.</seg>
<seg id="188"> For diversification to be of value, it is important that the projects be independent.</seg>
<seg id="189"> If the projects are perfectly correlated (or the entrepreneur opportunistically choses them to be perfectly correlated), diversification does not raise the pledgeable income.</seg>
<seg id="190"> Intermediation</seg>
<seg id="191"> Another way of increasing the pledgeable income is to reduce the entrepreneurḂŸs opportunity cost of being diligent.</seg>
<seg id="192"> Some projects are more conducive to misbehavior than others, for instance, projects that are exceptional, that do not have tangible investments, or that involve poor accounting.</seg>
<seg id="193"> Acapital poor firm can sometimes increase its pledgeable income by turning to an intermediary that has monitor-ing expertise.</seg>
<seg id="194"> A simple way to model monitoring is to assume that the intermediary can reduce B to a lower level b (and perhaps simultane-ously reduce pL) because it can place constraints on what the firm can do.</seg>
<seg id="195"> Loan covenants serve this purpose: for instance, lending contracts frequently forbid the firm from paying dividends if certain financial conditions are violated. Covenants may also give the bank veto rights on the sale of strategic assets and spell out circumstances under which the bank can intervene even more aggressively by getting the right to nominate all or part of the board.</seg>
<seg id="196"> Another potential interpretation of the monitoring activity is that the bank acquires information that is relevant for decision-making and uses it to convince the board not to rubberstamp (what turns out to be) the managementḂŸs pet project.</seg>
<seg id="197"> In the model, and apparently in reality, giving the firm less attractive outside options reduces entrepreneurial rents, increases pledgeable income and thus lowers A.</seg>
<seg id="198"> The carrot can be smaller if the stick is bigger.</seg>
<seg id="199"> Of course, intermediation is not free.</seg>
<seg id="200"> To determine whether interme-diaries can really increase pledgeable income, monitoring costs must also be taken into account.</seg>

<seg id="238"> 2 A Simple Model of Liquidity Demand</seg>
<seg id="239"> Firms demand liquidity in anticipation of future financing needs either because it is cheaper to get financing now or because there is a risk that financing will not be available if the firm waits until the need for funding arises.</seg>
<seg id="240"> In this chapter we analyze the demand for liquidity in a simple extension of the two-period model from section 1.3. The basic idea is easy to understand.</seg>
<seg id="241"> Suppose that there is an intermediate period when additional funds have to be invested in order to continue the project and realize any payoffs.</seg>
<seg id="242"> We refer to this reinvestment need as a liquidity shock and denote it ḊÑ (per unit of investment).</seg>
<seg id="243"> If the liquidity shock ḊÑ turns out to be larger than the pledgeable amount ḊÑ0, the firm cannot get outside funding to continue the project unless it has arranged for such funding in advance.</seg>
<seg id="244"> This creates a demand for liquidity, as firms look to insure against shocks that have a positive total return (ḊÑ1 ? ḊÑ &gt; 0), but a negative net present value for investors (ḊÑ0 ? ḊÑ &lt; 0).</seg>
<seg id="245"> Note that the wedge ḊÑ1 ? ḊÑ0 &gt; 0 is crucial for the argument. If ḊÑ1 = ḊÑ0, the liquidity shock ḊÑ cannot fall strictly between the total and the pledgeable return.</seg>
<seg id="246"> The ex ante demand for liquidity will depend on the size of the liquid-ity shock.</seg>
<seg id="247"> Shocks that are high enough will not be insured (financed in advance).</seg>
<seg id="248"> The second-best policy trades off the scale of the initial invest-ment against the ability to withstand higher liquidity shocks.</seg>
<seg id="249"> In general, there will be credit rationing both at the initial period and in the inter-mediate period, since entrepreneurial capital is scarce and commands a premium relative to the market.</seg>
<seg id="250"> By assumption, no external claims can be issued on the private (illiq-uid) return ḊÑ1 ? ḊÑ0, while arbitrary external claims can be issued on the pledgeable (liquid) return ḊÑ0.</seg>
<seg id="251"> In particular, these claims can be made contingent on the liquidity shock ḊÑ. In effect we are assuming complete contracting on the liquid portion of the firmḂŸs return.</seg>
<seg id="252"> This is perhaps unrealistic, but it has the attraction that it is a minimal departure from the standard ArrowẀCDebreu world.</seg>
<seg id="253"> After studying optimal contingent contracts, we discuss how they can be implemented using common means such as credit lines, equity issues (involving dilution), and hold-ing liquid (marketable) assets in anticipation of future liquidity needs.</seg>
<seg id="254"> Relaxing the assumption that the liquidity shock is observed by in-vestors, we also show that the implementation of the second-best policy hinges crucially on the ability of investors to keep the firm from spending funds on unauthorized projects.</seg>

<seg id="303"> 2.3 Implementation of the Second-Best Contract</seg>
<seg id="304"> The implementation of the second-best contract presents two kinds of problems.</seg>
<seg id="305"> The first problem is that the entrepreneur may use the funds in a different way than the contract specifies (either at date 0 or at date 1).</seg>
<seg id="306"> The second problem is that investors may not be able to deliver on the promise to provide funds at date 1.</seg>
<seg id="307"> Such promises must be backed up by claims on real assets that the investor owns at date 1 and can use as collateral.</seg>
<seg id="308"> We will discuss collateral problems at length in the coming chapters.</seg>
<seg id="309"> The purpose of this section is to illustrate some of the problems that may arise on the firmḂŸs side.</seg>
<seg id="310"> One potential problem is that when the second-best optimum speci-fies that the firm should not continue when facing the high shock, the entrepreneur may still want to invest less than the agreed-upon amount I at date 0 in order to keep extra funds in store to meet the high liquidity shock.</seg>
<seg id="311"> Alternatively, when the second-best optimum recommends that the firm withstand even the high liquidity shock, the entrepreneur may not want to save funds for reinvestment in the high state and instead spend the funds on a higher initial investment I. This may be a tempt-ing possibility, since the entrepreneur knows that investors will always finance a low shock even if the scale is higher than initially intended.</seg>
<seg id="312"> The entrepreneur can rely on a soft-budget constraint for the low shock because investors face a fait accompli.</seg>
<seg id="313"> The downside to the entrepreneur of such a policy is that there will not be enough funds to fully finance the high shock, as no investor will be ready to make up the shortfall at date 1.</seg>
<seg id="314"> In order to implement the second-best policy, there must be a way to enforce the right levels and kinds of investment at date 0 as well as date 1.</seg>
<seg id="315"> Intermediaries, venture capitalists, large block holders, and oth-ers, monitor in varying degrees and in different ways a firmḂŸs use of funds.</seg>
<seg id="316"> A rich literature in corporate finance has investigated these issues in depth.</seg>
<seg id="317"> We will not study intermediation explicitly, even though it constitutes an integral part of the financing of firms.</seg>
<seg id="318"> We will assume instead that investors can directly monitor the firmḂŸs liquidity position but not the firmḂŸs liquidity shock and consider two illustrative cases: in the first, the firm has no alternative uses of funds and will there-fore behave; in the second, the entrepreneur can divert funds for some personal benefit.</seg>

<seg id="341"> 2.3.2 Costly Implementation: Firm Can Divert Funds at Date 1</seg>
<seg id="342"> The assumption that the firm has no alternative use for excess liquid-ity is extreme.</seg>
<seg id="343"> Firms always have alternative investments to consider, especially if the funds come at no extra charge as above.</seg>
<seg id="344"> In appendix 2A we illustrate with a simple example how the optimal contract responds to the presence of an alternative investment option.</seg>
<seg id="345"> Consider again the two-shock model in which (2.10) holds, so that the second-best contract prescribes continuation for both the low (ḊÑL) and the high (ḊÑH) liquidity shock. Suppose that the firm can divert funds at date 1 to a project that only generates private benefits.</seg>
<seg id="346"> Each unit diverted into the alternative project generates a benefit to the entrepreneur that is equal to ḊÎ units of consumption.</seg>
<seg id="347"> Investors can observe how much funds the firm uses in total at date 1 as well as the scale at which the project is continued, but they cannot see how much is used for reinvestments and how much for diversion, nor can they observe the realization of the date-1 liquidity shock.</seg>
<seg id="348"> The presence of the alternative project makes the design of liquidity provi-sion more challenging.</seg>
<seg id="349"> In general, the second-best solution from sec-tion 2.2 can no longer be implemented, since the optimal contract must ensure that the entrepreneur does not want to divert funds for private benefit.</seg>
<seg id="350"> Appendix 2A determines the optimal second-best contract with po-tential diversion, using a standard mechanism design approach.</seg>
<seg id="351"> To sum-marize our findings briefly, we find that diversion can be avoided most efficiently in one of two ways.</seg>
<seg id="352"> Either the entrepreneur is asked to hold more than the minimum stake at the beginning, which he for-goes if he claims that the high shock has occurred, or investors scale down the investment in response to a reported high shock.</seg>
<seg id="353"> Which of the two options are used depends on parameter values.</seg>
<seg id="354"> Both schemes add to the cost of financing and therefore reduce the social surplus.</seg>
<seg id="355"> The idea of discouraging false reporting (claiming high costs) by lowering the continuation scale of investment corresponds to standard distor-tions in adverse selection models.</seg>
<seg id="356"> The other solution, which forces the entrepreneur to hold some of the pledgeable income in addition to the nonpledgeable one, is more interesting.</seg>
<seg id="357"> The entrepreneurḂŸs incentives are made to align with the investors by having his extra shares diluted along with the initial investorsḂŸ shares when a high-liquidity shock occurs.</seg>
<seg id="358"> Both solutions resonate with practice.</seg>
<seg id="359"> Finally, we note that when ḊÎ = 1, namely when there is no dead-weight loss for the entrepreneur to consume unneeded funds, the option to divert destroys all outside insurance opportunities.</seg>
<seg id="360"> In that case the firm is left to take care of itself using market instruments that are not contingent on idiosyncratic shocks.</seg>

<seg id="367"> The purpose of looking into such an extension is twofold.</seg>
<seg id="368"> It gives a richer interpretation of the liquidity shock by pointing to a variety of sources that give rise to a demand for liquidity. The general model will also be the basis for dis-cussing risk management in section 4.3.</seg>
<seg id="369"> Our original focus on shocks to the cost of reinvestment ḊÑ was mainly for convenience.</seg>
<seg id="370"> For empirical work, it is important to note that these ḂḞcomparative staticsḂḟ results pertain to variations across realized states of nature. Changes in the (random) parameters of the model would in general affect ḊË and that way investments as well as the cutoff.</seg>
<seg id="371"> This should be contrasted with comparative statics performed on the parameters of the basic model. In that model, changes in the parameters ḊÑ0 and ḊÑ1 had no effect on continuation decisions (the cutoff rule implicit in (2.23) does not include these parameters) as long as the parameter values are such that I is strictly positive but finite.</seg>
<seg id="372"> Nevertheless, most of the qualita-tive features of the basic model are retained.</seg>
<seg id="373"> In particular, instead of taking ḊÑ to be random, we can let the liquidation value w or the pledge-able income ḊÑ0 be random (keeping b(ḊØ) fixed) without changing our main conclusions (e.g., the optimality of a cutoff rule and the incom-plete insurance result).</seg>
<seg id="374"> In this sense, the liquidity shock can be given a broader interpretation (also for many of the qualitative results that follow up to part IV).</seg>
<seg id="375"> 2.6 Summary and Concluding Comments</seg>
<seg id="376"> In this chapter we have introduced our basic model of liquidity demand.</seg>
<seg id="377"> Firms demand liquidity, because they want to insure themselves against credit rationing.</seg>
<seg id="378"> Credit rationing is caused by the wedge between the pledgeable income to investors and the total income to the firm. When the cost of continuing a project falls between the pledgeable income and the total income, the project can continue only if its funding has been arranged in advance.</seg>
<seg id="379"> This is the sense in which firms demand liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="380"> In traditional financial models, firms never demand liquidity.12</seg>
<seg id="381"> As in many corporate finance models, investments have, at least at the margin, a negative net present value based on pledgeable income, so the entrepreneur has to put in some of his own capital to get the project off the ground.</seg>
<seg id="382"> Because entrepreneurial capital has a higher rate of return than investor capital, it is optimal for the entrepreneur to commit all of the firmḂŸs pledgeable income to the investors and only hold the illiquid claims for himself.</seg>
<seg id="383"> The amount of entrepreneurial capital determines the scale of operations.</seg>
<seg id="384"> Adding a liquidity demand to this standard solvency-requirement story, we have shown how the firm must also plan for its liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="385"> The model is one of assetẀCliability management. Not hoarding liquidity is akin to accepting a maturity mismatch.</seg>
<seg id="386"> Liquidity hoarding is akin to pur-chasing insurance.</seg>
<seg id="387"> In general (and always with a variable investment scale and a continuum of liquidity shocks), the firm is credit rationed both ex ante (initial investment) and ex post (continuation investment).</seg>
<seg id="388"> All strictly positive net present value investments and reinvestments will not be undertaken.</seg>
<seg id="389"> It may at first be surprising that investors knowingly fund projects with a negative continuation value, yet this is an essential ingredient of the theory of liquidity shortages.</seg>
<seg id="390"> If all liquidity shocks were associated with a positive continuation value for the investors, there would be no need to seek funding in advance.13 The demand for insurance is always associated with one side winning and the other side losing ex post.</seg>
<seg id="391"> In that sense, our model is conceptually no different than Diamond and DybvigḂŸs (1983) seminal model of consumer liquidity demand.</seg>
<seg id="392"> Indeed the two models are formally similar, though liquidity shocks to the pro-ductive sector have quite different implications for the economy as we will see later.</seg>
<seg id="393"> The extent to which investors can commit additional funds to projects that have a negative continuation value depends on their ability to mon-itor the contingencies.</seg>
<seg id="394"> If investors cannot observe the firmḂŸs liquidity shock, a contingent contract has to rely on information reported by the firm.</seg>
<seg id="395"> This is not a problem if the firm has no alternative use for funds.</seg>
<seg id="396"> However, if the firm can divert excess funds for private benefit, the con-tract needs to be adjusted as shown in appendix 2A.</seg>
<seg id="397"> Diversion can be avoided in one of two ways. Either the entrepreneur is asked to hold more than the minimum stake at the beginning, which he forgoes if he claims that a high shock has occurred, or investors scale down the invest-ment in response to a reported high shock.</seg>
<seg id="398"> In either case the solution is less efficient than when the shock is contractible.</seg>
<seg id="399"> In appendix 2B we develop in more detail the formal relationship between consumer liquidity demand and producer liquidity demand by reinterpreting our firms as DiamondẀCDybvig consumers.</seg>
<seg id="400"> Here is a brief summary of the key points.</seg>
<seg id="401"> The basic model of consumer liquid-ity demand has a three-period structure like the model with producer liquidity demand.</seg>
<seg id="402"> Consumers hoard liquidity to smooth consumption and meet unexpected liquidity needs.</seg>
<seg id="403"> Modeling this has tradition-ally assumed that consumers save at date 0 because they face uncer-tainty about the timing of their consumption needs, that is, they are unsure about their marginal rate of substitution between consumption at date 1 and date 2.</seg>
<seg id="404"> If one is to compare the models of producer and consumer liquidity demand, this suggests considering firms that have net savings at date 0 (I &lt; A) and, as discussed in this chapter, a ran-dom liquidity need that accrues either at date 1 or at date 2 (and income accruing at date 3).</seg>
<seg id="405"> With firms as net lenders, we are able to show the precise sense in which the two models are isomorphic.</seg>
<seg id="406"> The central optimal insurance result in the DiamondẀCDybvig analysis, whereby patient consumers end up subsidizing impatient ones (provided relative risk aversion is greater than 1), has an exact counterpart in the producer liquidity context as long as one interprets the entrepreneurḂŸs ḂḞconsumptionḂḟ as the ḂḞaver-age liquidity withdrawals.Ḃḟ</seg>
<seg id="407"> We also discuss JacklinḂŸs (1987) critique of a breakdown in insurance (cross-subsidization) between patient and impatient consumers when consumers have access to financial mar-kets. JacklinḂŸs critique in our context comes down to whether firms have alternative uses for excess funds withdrawn at date 1.</seg>
<seg id="408"> Finally, it is worth noting that our model can be used to support some degree of cross-subsidization within a firm.</seg>
<seg id="409"> Conglomerates are often criticized for subsidizing divisional investments, though the empi-rical evidence by now is rather mixed (for a survey of the evidence, see Stein 2003).</seg>
<seg id="410"> In light of the model presented here, subsidies can be viewed as optimal insurance policies for the human capital invested in the firm or alternatively as efficient ways to motivate the management and employees of divisions.</seg>
<seg id="411"> Conglomerates have specific information about divisional circumstances as well as the right to reallocate capital based on this information.</seg>
<seg id="412"> Reputation and culture can provide the nec-essary commitment power to implement such policies (see Shleifer and Summers 1988 for a related argument).</seg>
<seg id="413"> To sum up, whether diversion is avoided by asking the entrepreneur to hold more than the minimum stake at the beginning (which he for-goes if he claims that the high shock has occurred) or by scaling down investment in response to a reported high shock, the solution is less efficient than when the shock is contractible.</seg>
<seg id="414"> Sometimes the option to divert destroys all outside insurance opportunities and the firm is left to take care of itself, using market instruments that are not contingent on idiosyncratic shocks.</seg>
<seg id="415"> Appendix 2B: Relationship with Consumer Liquidity Demand</seg>
<seg id="416"> The DiamondẀCDybvig model</seg>
<seg id="417"> To focus on corporate liquidity demand, our analysis has assumed that consumers are indifferent about the date of consumption (but see section 5.3.1).</seg>
<seg id="418"> In reality, of course, consumers hoard substantial amounts of liquid assets in order to insure themselves against liquidity shocks.</seg>
<seg id="419"> They are willing to sacrifice returns to ensure that they will have enough money for various expenditures such as buying a house or a car when the opportunity arises, to send their children to college, or to protect themselves against illness or unemployment.</seg>
<seg id="420"> Thus consumers compete with corporations for the available stock of liquidity.</seg>
<seg id="421"> Consumer liquidity demand has been the focus of a large and inter-esting literature, starting with the seminal papers of Bryant (1980) and Diamond and Dybvig (1983).</seg>
<seg id="422"> The purpose of this appendix is to com-pare the implications of the DiamondẀCDybvig approach to consumer demand for liquidity (CDL) with our approach to producer demand for liquidity (PDL).</seg>
<seg id="423"> To this end, we will first go over the DiamondẀCDybvig model with our notation.</seg>
<seg id="424"> Timing Like our model, the DiamondẀCDybvig model has three peri-ods, t = 0, 1, 2.</seg>
<seg id="425"> Consumer Preferences</seg>
<seg id="426"> Consumers are ex ante identical.</seg>
<seg id="427"> For nota-tional simplicity, let us assume that they have no demand for consump-tion at date 0, and therefore invest their entire date-0 endowment a (per consumer).</seg>
<seg id="428"> They receive no additional endowments at dates 1 and 2.</seg>
<seg id="429"> At date 1 consumers are revealed to be one of two types.</seg>
<seg id="430"> A fraction 1 ? ḊÁ are impatient: they only care about date-1 consumption. The remaining fraction ḊÁ of consumers are patient: they are indifferent about date-1 and date-2 consumption.</seg>
<seg id="431"> There are two ways for the consumer to insure against liquidity risk: self-insurance and risk pooling.</seg>
<seg id="432"> There are two well-known issues raised by the DiamondẀCDybvig analysis worth highlighting.</seg>
<seg id="433"> The first concerns bank runs and their prevention.</seg>
<seg id="434"> When the consumersḂŸ type cannot be verified at date 1, patient con-sumers may pretend that they are impatient and withdraw early.</seg>
<seg id="435"> They have no incentive to do so as long as the other patient consumers do not withdraw early, since c2 &gt; c1.</seg>
<seg id="436"> However, there is also a bad equilibrium where patient consumers withdraw their money at date 1.</seg>
<seg id="437"> Suppose that a fraction of consumers exceeding (1 ? ḊÁ) come to the liquidity pool to withdraw their money.</seg>
<seg id="438"> Provided that liquidating long-term assets at date 1 generates a bit of income, the financial intermediary may liquidate these assets early in order to (partly) serve the withdrawing customers.</seg>
<seg id="439"> But this induces other patient consumers to run since their claim to date-2 income is depreciated.</seg>
<seg id="440"> Indeed, if all run, then it is in the interest of each to run.</seg>
<seg id="441"> Diamond and Dybvig have offered several ways out of such bad equi-libria.</seg>
<seg id="442"> The financial intermediary (perhaps with the assent of a pruden-tial supervisor) can suspend convertibility of demand deposits when the fraction of withdrawers reaches (1 ? ḊÁ).</seg>
<seg id="443"> Knowing that their long-term claim will not be jeopardized by early liquidation, patient consumers are better off waiting until date 2.</seg>
<seg id="444"> Similarly the presence of a central bank acting as a lender of last resort, or international markets extending suf-ficient credit lines to intermediaries, can destroy the incentive for a run (we have more to say on the limits on international credit in chapter 6).</seg>
<seg id="445"> The second well-known point is that trading in financial markets can have an adverse impact on efficient liquidity provision.</seg>
<seg id="446"> Jacklin (1987) showed that it is necessary to prevent consumer trading at date 1 in order for financial intermediaries to be able to provide efficient insurance.</seg>
<seg id="447"> To see this, suppose that an entrepreneur uses his date-0 endowment a to set up a mutual fund that invests only in long-term assets at date 0.</seg>
<seg id="448"> The entrepreneur does not issue shares in the mutual fund until date 1.</seg>
<seg id="449"> Patient consumers are better off withdrawing their deposits c1 and re-investing the money in the mutual fund as long as the price of a share in the mutual fund does not exceed (c1/c2)r2.</seg>
<seg id="450"> Let the entrepreneur keep the shares of the mutual fund for himself if he turns out to be patient and sell them at price c1r2/c2 if he turns out to be impatient.</seg>
<seg id="451"> This way the entrepreneur secures r2a &gt; c2 when patient and (c1r2/c2)a &gt; c1 when impatient.</seg>
<seg id="452"> In summary, consumer insurance against liquidity shocks involves a cross-subsidy that can be arbitraged by financial markets.</seg>
<seg id="453"> The arbi-trageurs free-ride on the liquidity (the investments in short-term assets) provided by the financial intermediaries. Trading must be prevented to maintain the optimal subsidy.</seg>
<seg id="454"> More generally, Diamond (1997) shows that when some consumers do not have access to financial markets, there is still scope for insurance, but the social cost of financial mar-kets increases as the number of consumers who have access to them increases.</seg>
<seg id="455"> Farhi et al. (2009) consider general preferences u (c1, c2, ḊÈ), where ḊÈ is a one-dimensional preference parameter learned by the consumer at date 1, and provide a general treatment of the Jacklin critique.</seg>
<seg id="456"> They show that the inefficiency created by self-provision of liquidity paired with trading at date 1 can be entirely attributed to a failure to control the interest rate between dates 1 and 2.</seg>
<seg id="457"> The interest rate here is tech-nologically driven and, in general, does not coincide with the opti-mal interest rate, which involves cross-subsidies between patient and impatient consumers.</seg>
<seg id="458"> Farhi et al. demonstrate that the state can implement the efficient allocation by imposing on all financial intermediaries a liquidity requirement: a liquidity floor if the optimal cross-subsidy runs from patient to impatient consumers (the case where the coefficient of rela-tive risk aversion exceeds 1) and a liquidity ceiling if the cross-subsidy runs the other way.</seg>
