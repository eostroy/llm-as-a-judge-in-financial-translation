<seg id="97"> The Keynesian multiplier, taught for generations to millions of undergraduates, works as follows.</seg>
<seg id="98"> Any initial government stimulus, say a program of increased government expenditure, puts money into people's hands, which they then spend.</seg>
<seg id="99"> The initial government stimulus is the first round.</seg>

<seg id="109"> But the sum may be much larger than the original government stimulus.</seg>
<seg id="110"> If the MPC is, say, 0.5, the Keynesian multiplier is 2.</seg>

<seg id="271"> The most basic economics is a theory of exchange:</seg>
<seg id="272"> it describes who trades what to whom in which markets.</seg>
<seg id="273"> But there is also a sociological theory of exchange.</seg>
<seg id="274"> This theory differs from the economic theory primarily in the central role it accords to fairness.</seg>
<seg id="275"> It depends upon notions of what is fair and what is unfair.</seg>
<seg id="276"> Sociology needs a different theory since sociologists have a more general notion of exchange than economists.</seg>
<seg id="277"> Not only do they want to explain who trades what in which markets, they also want to explain nonmarket exchanges, within the firm, between friends and acquaintances, and within the family.</seg>
<seg id="278"> The sociologists say that, when transactions are not fair, the person on the short end of the transaction will be angry.</seg>
<seg id="279"> The impulses released by that anger force exchanges to be fair.</seg>
<seg id="280"> The social psychological theory of exchange is called equity theory.</seg>
<seg id="281"> It holds that on either side of an exchange the inputs should equal the outputs.</seg>
<seg id="282"> This of course sounds like what happens in any market situation.</seg>
<seg id="283"> For example, at the supermarket the store gives you the groceries; you give them the value of those goods.</seg>
<seg id="284"> Sociologists thus say that their theory is motivated by economists (and it is perhaps a bit tainted in their eyes for that reason).</seg>
<seg id="285"> But there is a world of difference between the two theories, because what an economist would put into the valuation of the input on either side of the exchangeḂẂwhich is only the monetary value of the exchange ḂẂis very different from what sociologists put into the valuation of the inputs and the outputs.</seg>
<seg id="286"> Those inputs include subjective evaluations, such as whether the person on either side of the transaction is of high status or low status.</seg>
<seg id="287"> An early version of exchange theory comes from Peter Blau's study of government agents involved in complex litigation.</seg>
<seg id="288"> The official rules said that they could seek assistance only from their supervisors.</seg>
<seg id="289"> Of course the agents did not want to go constantly to their supervisors for help.</seg>
<seg id="290"> Not only would they be a nuisance, but they would also be admitting their own lack of knowledge and independence.</seg>
<seg id="291"> So they systematically violated the rules; they consulted among themselves.</seg>
<seg id="292"> Blau observed the pattern of these consultations and explained it in terms of equity theory.</seg>
<seg id="293"> He noticed that the agents had different levels of expertise.</seg>
<seg id="294"> It rarely happened, as one might suppose, that low- expertise agents sought advice from those with high expertise.</seg>

<seg id="296"> And similarly, high-expertise agents gave and received advice from others with high expertise.</seg>

<seg id="302"> It also becomes wearing to give.</seg>
<seg id="303"> So low-expertise agents might have initially sought the advice of more knowledgeable agents, but they rarely returned.</seg>
<seg id="304"> In contrast, with their peers, the exchange took place with trades of comparable value.</seg>
<seg id="305"> When subjective elements enter this evaluation, such as the values of ingratiation and thanks, this becomes a theory of fair exchange.</seg>
<seg id="306"> The gratitude expressed by the low-expertise agents for their trades with the high-expertise agents, when they occur, makes the transactions fair.</seg>
<seg id="307"> The inputs on the one side of the exchange are equal in value to the outputs on the other side.</seg>
<seg id="308"> The theory explains why those of low status (for example, blacks and women in traditional societies) are subservient.</seg>
<seg id="309"> To equalize the subjective and objective inputs and outputs in the exchange, they have to give more than those of higher status.</seg>
