<seg id="1"> EQUILIBRIUM</seg>
<seg id="2"> THIS CHAPTER SHOWS how equilibrium can be reached in a capital market and describes the characteristics of such an equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="3"> We present a series of cases, each of which assumes agreement among investors concerning the chances of alternative future outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="4"> More complex (and realistic) cases are covered in later chapters.</seg>
<seg id="5"> Trading and Equilibrium</seg>
<seg id="6"> A standard definition of equilibrium is:</seg>
<seg id="7"> A condition in which all acting influences are canceled by others, resulting in a stable, balanced or unchanging system.</seg>
<seg id="8"> We will use a much simpler definition: a financial economy is in equilibrium when no further trades can be made.</seg>
<seg id="9"> But of course in the real world trading seldom stops, and when it does stop, it is typically because low-cost markets are temporarily closed.</seg>
<seg id="10"> The implication is that financial markets never really reach a state of equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="11"> Conditions change, there is new information, and people begin to act on the new information before they have fully acted on the old information.</seg>
<seg id="12"> In actuality, people make trades to move toward an equilibrium target but the target is constantly changing.</seg>
<seg id="13"> Despite this completely valid observation, we need to understand the properties of a condition of equilibrium in financial markets, because markets will usually be headed toward such a position.</seg>
<seg id="14"> And the more efficient the financial system, the smaller will be the discrepancies between market conditions and those of full equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="15"> Moreover, we will see that for many purposes the most important aspects of equilibrium for portfolio choice concern the levels of broad market indices, overall consumption, and other macroeconomic variables, which are likely to be closest to their equilibrium levels.</seg>
<seg id="16"> Understanding the nature of a financial market in equilibrium is a crucial step toward understanding real financial markets.</seg>
<seg id="17"> The goal of this book is to explore the relationships between investors¡¯ characteristics and investment opportunities and the key aspects of the situation that would be obtained if trading continued until equilibrium were reached.</seg>
<seg id="18"> Determinants and Results</seg>
<seg id="19"> Economics is a social science, dealing with the behavior of individuals and the results of their interactions where money is concerned.</seg>
<seg id="20"> When trying to under-stand equilibrium relationships the focus is descriptive, concentrating on what actually happens.</seg>
<seg id="21"> But much of financial economics is prescriptive, attempting to help people make better financial decisions.</seg>
<seg id="22"> Such decisions involve buying and selling financial assets at prices determined in markets.</seg>
<seg id="23"> Good financial decisions require an understanding of the forces that determine such prices.</seg>
<seg id="24"> More specifically, optimal portfolio choice requires an understanding of equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="25"> Figure 2-1 provides a simplified version of the operation of an exchange economy with two investors.</seg>
<seg id="26"> Production is taken as given, with productive outputs represented by a set of securities.</seg>
<seg id="27"> Individuals start with initial security portfolios, then trade securities in financial markets until no further voluntary trades can be made.</seg>
<seg id="28"> When this point is reached, each person has a final security portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="29"> The terms on which trades were made or, in some circumstances, the terms on which additional trades might be made, constitute security prices (more broadly, asset prices).</seg>
<seg id="30"> After equilibrium has been established, time passes and outcomes are determined.</seg>
<seg id="31"> Typically, some people do better than others, depending on their portfolio holdings and the nature of events.</seg>
<seg id="32"> Then the process begins anew.</seg>
<seg id="33"> In a real economy, and in many of the cases discussed in this book, there will be more than two investors but the essential elements shown in Figure 2-1 remain the same.</seg>
<seg id="34"> The trades made by an individual in this process will of course depend on his or her initial holdings of securities (or, more broadly, level of wealth). But other factors will play important roles.</seg>
<seg id="35"> Investors differ in geographic location, home ownership, profession, and so forth. We term these aspects an individual¡¯s position.</seg>
<seg id="36"> If two people have different positions they may wish to hold different portfolios.</seg>
<seg id="37"> Similarly, people may have different feelings about risk, present versus future gratification, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="38"> We term these an individual¡¯s preferences.</seg>
<seg id="39"> Differences in preferences will lead investors to choose different portfolios.</seg>
<seg id="40"> Finally, investors often assess the chances of alternative future outcomes differently. One investor¡¯s predictions may differ from those of another, leading to choices of different portfolios.</seg>
<seg id="41"> Taking production as given, the future will provide one of many alternative outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="42"> Financial markets allow people to share those outcomes in ways that can take into account their different positions, preferences, and predictions.</seg>
<seg id="43"> When thinking about portfolio choice it is important to keep in mind that if one person chooses less than his or her share of some security, someone else must choose more than his or her share.</seg>
<seg id="44"> This implies that individuals should be able to justify their investment decisions on the basis of differences among their positions, preferences, and/or predictions.</seg>
<seg id="45"> Prices are not determined by random number machines.</seg>
<seg id="46"> They come from recent or prospective trades by real people.</seg>
<seg id="47"> Investors who fail to fully take this into account do so at their peril.</seg>
<seg id="48"> In this book, we analyze exchange economies in which investors build portfolios from existing securities.</seg>
<seg id="49"> But those who create such securities to finance productive opportunities base their decisions in part on the prices of current securities.</seg>
<seg id="50"> Asset prices, like other prices, are determined by the joint forces of supply and demand.</seg>
<seg id="51"> In the short run, supply and demand come from individuals and institutions trading existing securities or new purely financial instruments.</seg>
<seg id="52"> In the long run, however, some old securities will expire or become worthless as firms cease production; moreover, new securities will be created to finance new production.</seg>
<seg id="53"> We concentrate here on the determination of asset prices and portfolio choice in the short run¡ªa key part of the long-run picture.</seg>
<seg id="54"> Figure 2-1 allows for financial intermediaries that can help markets function.</seg>
<seg id="55"> But it includes no financial planners, banks, mutual funds, or other institutional investors, even though such financial services firms are an essential part of any modern financial system.</seg>
<seg id="56"> In our examples, such firms will be at best shadowy, helping individuals make better decisions, providing some types of new securities, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="57"> We do this primarily to keep things simple.</seg>
<seg id="58"> But there is another reason.</seg>
<seg id="59"> There are typically many ways that financial institutions can facilitate efficient sharing of investment outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="60"> Ultimately, the particular institutional structure found in an economy will depend on relative costs, skill in marketing, and a certain amount of chance.</seg>
<seg id="61"> It is extremely difficult to predict the precise nature of financial services; hence we concentrate on the more fundamental aspects of asset prices and the ultimate payoffs from chosen portfolios.</seg>
<seg id="62"> Time, Outcomes, Securities, and Predictions</seg>
<seg id="63"> Financial economics deals with time, risk, options, and information.</seg>
<seg id="64"> Individuals allocate resources over time by borrowing, lending, investing in stocks, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="65"> Many of these investments have risk¡ªtheir future values are uncertain.</seg>
<seg id="66"> Some allow the option of taking an action or not¡ªfor example, one may purchase a contract giving the right but not the obligation to buy 100 shares of Hewlett Packard stock a year hence for a price fixed in advance.</seg>
<seg id="67"> Finally, information is used by investors to make predictions that affect both asset prices and portfolio choices.</seg>
<seg id="68"> We use a simple but powerful structure to incorporate these aspects.</seg>
<seg id="69"> First, we divide time into discrete dates and intervals.</seg>
<seg id="70"> We examine cases in which there are two dates (¡°now¡± and ¡°later¡±) and one time period between them.</seg>
<seg id="71"> More realistic cases could involve many dates and periods.</seg>
<seg id="72"> We will not deal with such situations explicitly but will indicate some of the ways that long-run considerations can affect short-term asset pricing.</seg>
<seg id="73"> Second, we assume that at each future date there are two or more alternative outcomes, or states of the world (states for short).</seg>
<seg id="74"> One and only one of these states will actually occur, and there is generally uncertainty about the actual outcome.</seg>
<seg id="75"> Uncertainty is expressed by assigning probabilities to the alternative states.</seg>
<seg id="76"> Thus if the possible states are that (1) it will rain tomorrow or (2) the sun will shine, investors will agree on the definition of the states, but may have different views about the chances of those states.</seg>
<seg id="77"> One investor may think there is a 40 percent chance of rain, while another may think that there is a 60 percent chance.</seg>
<seg id="78"> An investor¡¯s predictions are stated in the form of a set of probabilities for the different states.</seg>
<seg id="79"> The vehicles that people use for trades are assets or securities. We will use both terms more or less interchangeably but favor the latter for our cases.</seg>
<seg id="80"> A security provides payoffs in different states. Thus the stock of an umbrella com pany might pay $5 if it rains and $3 if it shines.</seg>
<seg id="81"> We assume that everyone agrees on the set of payoffs for a given security (here: $5 if rain, $3 if shine).</seg>
<seg id="82"> As indicated earlier, however, there may be substantial disagreement about the probabilities of different states.</seg>
<seg id="83"> This approach is sometimes termed state/preference theory.</seg>
<seg id="84"> Importantly, it allows for considerable generality without making excessive demands on one¡¯s mathematical skills.</seg>
<seg id="85"> The key objection (often made) is that to reflect even an approximation to reality one must consider cases with thousands, millions, or billions of states and time periods.</seg>
<seg id="86"> Some argue that for this reason alternative approaches that rely on smooth probability distributions and/or continuous concepts of time are superior.</seg>
<seg id="87"> This argument has some merit, but many key economic relationships can best be understood using a discrete-time, discrete-state approach with a limited number of states and time periods.</seg>
<seg id="88"> The resulting qualitative conclusions can then form the basis for building other types of systems for empirical applications.</seg>
<seg id="89"> One of the goals of this book is to show that the state/preference approach offers an excellent basis for thinking about asset prices and portfolio choice.</seg>
<seg id="90"> The Market Risk/Reward Theorem and Corollary</seg>
<seg id="91"> A staple of textbook discussions of asset pricing and portfolio choice is the market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="92"> By definition, it includes all securities available in a market.</seg>
<seg id="93"> An individual with a budget equal to x percent of the value of the overall market portfolio can choose a portfolio with the same composition by holding x percent of the outstanding units of each available security.</seg>
<seg id="94"> The simplest mean/variance asset pricing theory (the original Capital Asset Pricing Model [CAPM]) concluded that in equilibrium, investors will choose combinations of the market portfolio and borrowing or lending, with the proportions determined by their willingness to bear risk to obtain higher expected return.</seg>
<seg id="95"> Such investors face only one source of uncertainty¡ªthe performance of the market as a whole; that is, they bear only market risk.</seg>
<seg id="96"> An investor who chooses a less diversified portfolio will generally bear both market risk and non-market risk¡ªuncertainty that would remain even if the market outcome were known.</seg>
<seg id="97"> In the setting of the original CAPM the expected return of a security or portfolio is greater, the greater its market risk.</seg>
<seg id="98"> Non-market risk is not rewarded with higher expected return.</seg>
<seg id="99"> This principle can be stated more generally in a form that we will call the Market Risk/Reward Theorem (MRRT):¡£</seg>
<seg id="100"> This purports to describe actual capital markets, and is hence part of a positive economic theory.</seg>
<seg id="101"> Under some circumstances there may follow normative advice that we will call the Market Risk/Reward Corollary (MRRC):</seg>
<seg id="102"> Don¡¯t take non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="103"> The original CAPM leads to an interesting and perhaps correct statement about expected returns and risks (the MRRT) but also to the prediction that all investors will use a market-like index fund for all but their riskless investments.</seg>
<seg id="104"> In fact, only a minority of investors do this, so this implication of the model is inconsistent with observed behavior.</seg>
<seg id="105"> Nonetheless, the standard presentation of the CAPM concludes that most investors should put most (if not all) of their at-risk money in a broadly diversified market-like portfolio¡ªthat is, obey the MRRC.</seg>
<seg id="106"> Cases</seg>
<seg id="107"> In this book we consider a number of possible cases, each of which describes a miniature version of a stylized capital market.</seg>
<seg id="108"> In some cases the MRRT holds exactly; in others it holds only approximately (i.e., market risk is a source of expected return but not the only one).</seg>
<seg id="109"> In some cases the MRRC represents sound investment advice; in others it must be modified (i.e., some investors should take non-market risk).</seg>
<seg id="110"> In some cases market risk can be measured in the manner specified in the original CAPM; in others a different measure is appropriate. And so on.</seg>
<seg id="111"> In an important sense, each case represents a view of the nature of the capital market and the best approach to adopt when determining the most appropriate saving and investment strategy for an investor.</seg>
<seg id="112"> As indicated earlier, anyone making such decisions or advising others concerning such decisions should have some such view.</seg>
<seg id="113"> Those unfamiliar with the theoretical and empirical work in finance might expect there to be solid evidence in favor of one of these views.</seg>
<seg id="114"> Unfortunately this is not the case.</seg>
<seg id="115"> Investment decisions are about the future. The relevant aspects are the probabilities of possible future events.</seg>
<seg id="116"> Investment theory and practice are concerned with expected future returns and the associated future risks.</seg>
<seg id="117"> Information on the frequencies of past events, historic average returns, and variabilities of historic returns may be useful in assessing future prospects, but conditions change and with them future prospects, security prices, and capital market opportunities.</seg>
<seg id="118"> At the very least, empirical evidence needs to be combined with the results of experiments in which human beings make decisions concerning uncertain future prospects before a particular view about the capital markets is adopted.</seg>
<seg id="119"> Most of the figures and tables in the book are taken directly from workbooks prepared for cases processed using the APSIM program. This makes it possible for others to replicate the experiments.</seg>
<seg id="120"> Agreement</seg>
<seg id="121"> This chapter follows in the tradition of the original CAPM by assuming that investors agree on the probabilities of alternative future outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="122"> To obtain more general results, however, we do not use standard mean/variance assumptions for the cases in this chapter.</seg>
<seg id="123"> In later chapters we show that the specific conclusions of the CAPM can be obtained if special assumptions are made.</seg>
<seg id="124"> Case 1: Mario, Hue, and the Fish</seg>
<seg id="125"> We now turn to a case designed to include many of the factors in Figure 2-1 and yet be as simple as possible.</seg>
<seg id="126"> While economists often choose forestry metaphors (¡°trees¡±), we adopt a nautical setting to reflect the author¡¯s location on the California coast.</seg>
<seg id="127"> The protagonists are Mario, who lives in Monterey and works at the Monterey Fishing Company, and Hue (rhymes with ¡°whey¡±), who lives in Half Moon Bay and works at the Half Moon Bay Fishing Company.</seg>
<seg id="128"> The investors start out with shares of company stock, with ticker symbols MFC and HFC.</seg>
<seg id="129"> Only two dates are of interest¡ªnow and later.</seg>
<seg id="130"> Mario and Hue consume only fish, and the securities pay off in fish as well.</seg>
<seg id="131"> Both players have fish now and must rely on the payoffs from their portfolios for fish later.</seg>
<seg id="132"> The fishing companies will provide the owners of their securities with all the fish that will be caught at the future date.</seg>
<seg id="133"> However, the catch will depend on the whims of nature.</seg>
<seg id="134"> Two aspects are important.</seg>
<seg id="135"> First, how many fish come to the California coast? Second, do these fish favor the north (Half Moon Bay) or the south (Monterey)?</seg>
<seg id="136"> There are four different future states.</seg>
<seg id="137"> Mario and Hue agree on the sizes of the catches in each of these states and make predictions about the chances of the alternative outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="138"> Their goal is to trade with one another until the fish¡ªpresent and alternative future amounts¡ªare shared voluntarily in the best possible way.</seg>
<seg id="139"> In this case, Mario and Hue have the same opinions about the chances of next year¡¯s catch. We use the term agreement to connote such a situation.</seg>
<seg id="140"> Much of the literature on asset pricing explicitly or implicitly makes this assumption.</seg>
<seg id="141"> For example, many books and articles analyze the expected returns on assets.</seg>
<seg id="142"> But expected returns are computed using probabilities (as are standard deviations, correlations, and other such measures).</seg>
<seg id="143"> If individuals differ concerning probabilities, that is, if there is disagreement, whose probabilities are to be used?We devote considerable attention to this issue in Chapter 6.</seg>
<seg id="144"> For now, we follow common practice.</seg>
<seg id="145"> In this case, markets are incomplete. We use this term to mean that there are some trades that cannot be made using available securities.</seg>
<seg id="146"> Some of the cases in later chapters involve complete markets, which represent the ultimate in security availability, albeit at the cost of some lack of realism.</seg>
<seg id="147"> Here, as in subsequent cases, we show inputs and outputs from the APSIM program.</seg>
<seg id="148"> Securities provide payments at one or more dates and states.</seg>
<seg id="149"> For generality, we specify the amount each security pays in each state, including the present.</seg>
<seg id="150"> The payoffs provided by each of the securities in each possible state of the world are shown in a securities table (Figure 2-2).</seg>
<seg id="151"> For Case 1, each row in the table represents one of the five states.</seg>
<seg id="152"> The first is the present (¡°Now¡±).</seg>
<seg id="153"> The others are named to indicate the size of the total catch (¡°Bad¡± or ¡°Good¡±) and whether more fish go south (¡°S¡±) or north (¡°N¡±).</seg>
<seg id="154"> The last two columns show the pay-offs (fish per share) for each of the two stocks. Neither stock provides any fish today, as the entries in the first row indicate.</seg>
<seg id="155"> As we will see, each company has 10 shares outstanding.</seg>
<seg id="156"> Thus the total catch is 80 fish in states BadS and BadN, with Monterey doing better in the former and Half Moon Bay doing better in the latter.</seg>
<seg id="157"> The total catch is also the same (120) in both the GoodS and GoodN states, with the two areas dividing the catch differently, as before.</seg>
<seg id="158"> As will be seen, these features are highly relevant.</seg>
<seg id="159"> The first column, labeled ¡°Consume,¡± represents a security that pays one fish now and none at any other time or circumstance. It is included so that current consumption can be represented as being provided by securities in an individual¡¯s portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="160"> This makes it possible to represent decisions to consume less (or more) now in order to consume more (or less) in the future as matters of portfolio choice.</seg>
<seg id="161"> Crucial decisions concerning how much to save and invest are thus integrated with more traditional decisions about the allocation of savings among traditional securities.</seg>
<seg id="162"> Savings and investment decisions thus are simply parts of the overall portfolio choice decision.</seg>
<seg id="163"> There are no units of the second security (¡°Bond¡±) in existence when this story begins.</seg>
<seg id="164"> This security pays one fish at the future date, no matter what happens.</seg>
<seg id="165"> Whatever one thinks the probabilities of the four future states may be, one bond will pay one fish, so that it is truly riskless. It is included so that Mario and Hue can make deals in which one of them ¡°issues¡± a bond to the other, in return for a present payment.</seg>
<seg id="166"> Formally, the issuer will have a negative number of bonds while the buyer will have a positive number.</seg>
<seg id="167"> In more conventional terms, the issuer will have borrowed money and the other party will have lent money.</seg>
<seg id="168"> In practice, such activities are usually conducted through financial institutions.</seg>
<seg id="169"> Thus Hue might deposit money (fish) in a bank so that Mario could obtain a loan (take the current fish in return for promising to pay that amount back with interest).</seg>
<seg id="170"> Here such arrangements are made directly, with the bond payoffs providing a template.</seg>
<seg id="171"> In this case, and throughout this book, we consider only securities with pay-offs that are represented in the securities table by positive numbers or zeros. Traditional securities such as stocks, bonds, and options provide this type of limited liability.</seg>
<seg id="172"> More complex securities, such as swaps and futures contracts, require the holder to make payments in some states of the world and require frequent monitoring of credit and/or payments made prior to the future date.</seg>
<seg id="173"> We do allow negative holdings of limited liability securities, subject to a credit check, as will be seen.</seg>
<seg id="174"> In this case there are four securities and five states.</seg>
<seg id="175"> Formally, if there are fewer securities than states, a market is said to be incomplete.</seg>
<seg id="176"> If there are as many or more securities than states it can be complete in the sense that any desired combination of consumption across states can be obtained by choosing appropriate positions in the available securities.</seg>
<seg id="177"> In an incomplete market such as this, some ways of sharing outcomes between the two investors cannot be attained by trading available securities.</seg>
<seg id="178"> This may or may not preclude mutually desirable financial arrangements.</seg>
<seg id="179"> We explore this matter in detail in later chapters.</seg>
<seg id="180"> Initially Mario has 10 shares of MFC and Hue has 10 shares of HFC.</seg>
<seg id="181"> Neither has any bonds.</seg>
<seg id="182"> Finally, each has 49 fish at present.</seg>
<seg id="183"> The portfolios table for Case 1 is shown in Figure 2-3.</seg>
<seg id="184"> The probabilities of the states are shown in Figure 2-4. Each entry indicates the probability that the indicated state will occur.</seg>
<seg id="185"> The entries in a row that pertain to the same date sum to 1, because one and only one of the alternatives will occur. The probability that the present state will occur is, of course, 1.</seg>
<seg id="186"> In this example, good times are more likely than bad times.</seg>
<seg id="187"> Moreover, whether the overall catch is good or bad, the fish are more likely to go north than they are to go south.</seg>
<seg id="188"> In this case, the investors agree on the probabilities of the alternative states and their assessments of the probabilities are correct.</seg>
<seg id="189"> This is a characteristic of all the cases in this chapter.</seg>
<seg id="190"> In subsequent chapters we consider cases in which an investor can make predictions resulting in probability estimates that differ from those of other investors and/or from the actual probabilities shown in the probabilities table.</seg>
<seg id="191"> We come now to the matter of each player¡¯s preferences.</seg>
<seg id="192"> Two aspects are important.</seg>
<seg id="193"> The first concerns attitudes toward consumption at alternative times, other things equal.</seg>
<seg id="194"> We represent this by a time preference, or discount.</seg>
<seg id="195"> By convention, current consumption serves as a numeraire, with the time preference indicating the desirability of a unit of consumption at a future time, expressed relative to present consumption.</seg>
<seg id="196"> In this case, both Mario and Hue consider a unit of consumption in the future as good as 0.96 units now, as shown in the first column of Figure 2-5.</seg>
<seg id="197"> In some cases people will have different views about the desirability of consumption in alternative future states.</seg>

<seg id="199"> Later we explore the implications of such cases, which involve state-dependent preferences.</seg>
<seg id="200"> In the cases in this chapter, however, the desirability of consumption in a state depends only on the amount consumed in that state and the date (now or later).</seg>
<seg id="201"> The other aspect of preferences concerns attitudes toward risk.</seg>
<seg id="202"> Few people are comfortable with risk, especially when it can lead to a serious reduction in standard of living.</seg>
<seg id="203"> In the settings in this book a thoughtful investor will take on risk only in order to achieve higher expected return.</seg>
<seg id="204"> But investors differ in their willingness to accept risk in pursuit of higher expected return.</seg>

<seg id="210"> Trading</seg>
<seg id="211"> To improve their situations, Mario and Hue need to trade with one another.</seg>
<seg id="212"> They have to consider the terms on which they might be willing to make trades, then reach mutually agreeable arrangements.</seg>
<seg id="213"> Interpreting the setting literally, we might expect Mario and Hue to negotiate with one another using bluffing, feigned disinterest in desirable trades, concealed information about current holdings, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="214"> But our interest is not in small markets with personal hand-to-hand combat. Mario and Hue are simply vehicles for understanding larger economies.</seg>
<seg id="215"> Thus we will assume that they use a trading mechanism more appropriate for markets with large numbers of participants.</seg>
<seg id="216"> 2.8.1.</seg>
<seg id="217"> The Role of the Market Maker</seg>
<seg id="218"> In particular, we invoke the services of a market maker who gathers information and facilitates trades.</seg>
<seg id="219"> Because we are interested more in the properties of equilibrium in capital markets than in the manner in which equilibrium is established, we adopt a trading process that at best only approximates the ways in which actual financial markets operate.</seg>
<seg id="220"> Our market maker¡¯s job is to conduct markets for each of the securities, executing trades among the investors.</seg>
<seg id="221"> A set of such markets, one for each security, constitutes a round of trading.</seg>
<seg id="222"> If no trades are made in a round, the process is complete and equilibrium has been attained.</seg>
<seg id="223"> If some trades are made in a round, additional rounds are conducted, as needed.</seg>
<seg id="224"> Each security market is conducted in four phases.</seg>
<seg id="225"> First, the market maker polls investors to obtain information on the prices at which they would enter into trades and possibly on the amounts that they would trade at various prices.</seg>
<seg id="226"> This is generally termed the process of price discovery.</seg>
<seg id="227"> In the second phase, based on the information obtained in the first phase, the market maker announces a price at which orders for trades may be submitted.</seg>
<seg id="228"> In the third phase, given the announced price, each individual submits an offer to buy a number of shares, an offer to sell a number of shares, or no offer at all.</seg>
<seg id="229"> In the fourth and final phase, the market maker executes the orders.</seg>
<seg id="230"> If there is a disparity between the total number of shares offered for sale and the total amount investors would like to buy, some orders are only partially filled.</seg>
<seg id="231"> If the quantity demanded exceeds the quantity supplied, all sell orders are executed in full and each buyer is allocated a proportionate amount of his or her order, with the proportion given by the ratio of the total amount offered for sale divided by the total amount bid.</seg>
<seg id="232"> If the total number of shares buyers wish to purchase is less than the total amount sellers wish to sell, all purchase orders are executed in full, with the corresponding portion of each of the seller¡¯s orders filled.</seg>
<seg id="233"> Figure 2-6 summarizes the process.</seg>
<seg id="234"> A key function of a market is to establish a price at which substantial numbers of shares will be traded.</seg>
<seg id="235"> In the process of price discovery, a skilled market maker will gather enough information to obtain a good estimate of such a price, often by polling a representative subset of likely buyers and sellers.</seg>
<seg id="236"> In our simulations, the market maker gathers information from all the investors. A key aspect is each investor¡¯s reservation price for a security.</seg>
<seg id="237"> The concept is straight-forward: an investor will not buy shares at any price above his or her reservation price and will not sell shares for any price below the reservation price.</seg>
<seg id="238"> In our setting, the reservation price for an investor will be unique. Later we show why this is the case.</seg>
<seg id="239"> In the simplest version of our trading process, the market maker chooses a trade price based solely on investors¡¯ reservation prices.</seg>
<seg id="240"> More specifically, the market maker (1) averages the reservation prices for all potential buyers,(2) averages the reservation prices for all potential sellers, and then (3) sets the trade price halfway between the two amounts.</seg>
<seg id="241"> An investor is a potential buyer at a price if no constraints would preclude the purchase of at least some shares. Correspondingly, an investor is a potential seller at a price if no constraints would preclude the sale of at least some shares.</seg>
<seg id="242"> In the typical case in which no constraints are binding, the resulting trade price will simply be the average of the investors¡¯ reservation prices.</seg>
<seg id="243"> For the cases in this book, the market maker takes this relatively simple approach.</seg>
<seg id="244"> In cases with great disparities among investors¡¯ situations, however, this may result in markets in which there are considerable differences between the amounts demanded and those supplied at the announced price.</seg>
<seg id="245"> To better handle such cases the simulation program allows for a more information-intensive procedure.</seg>
<seg id="246"> In this approach the market maker polls investors to find out how many shares they would purchase or sell at various possible prices, then chooses the price that will maximize the number of shares actually traded (i.e., the smaller of the quantity demanded or supplied).</seg>
<seg id="247"> The first price tried is the same as in the simpler procedure.</seg>
<seg id="248"> If there is excess demand at that price, the next price chosen is halfway between it and the highest unconstrained reservation price (at which there is excess supply).</seg>
<seg id="249"> If there is excess supply at the first price, the next price chosen is halfway between it and the lowest unconstrained reservation price (at which there is excess demand).</seg>
<seg id="250"> The process can be repeated as many times as desired, always choosing a price halfway between the most recent price with excess demand and that with excess supply.</seg>
<seg id="251"> Real markets are far more complex than our simulations.</seg>
<seg id="252"> Market makers and/or traders work hard to estimate the levels of demand and supply at various prices but must eventually act on less than perfect information about investors¡¯ likely choices.</seg>
<seg id="253"> On the other hand, conditions rarely change radically overnight; thus recent prices convey substantial amounts of information concerning prices that will balance current supply and demand.</seg>
<seg id="254"> While the simulated market mechanisms are far from realistic, they lead to plausible equilibria and thus serve our purposes.</seg>
<seg id="255"> Investor Demand and Supply</seg>
<seg id="256"> How does an investor determine the amount to buy or sell at a given trade price?If the trade price is below the investor¡¯s reservation price for the security, he or she will wish to purchase shares.</seg>
<seg id="257"> In our simulations, investors¡¯ demand curves are downward-sloping¡ªthe larger the number of shares purchased, the lower is the resulting new reservation price.</seg>
<seg id="258"> Given this, it is best for the investor to purchase shares until the reservation price for an additional share equals the trade price.</seg>
<seg id="259"> If this is feasible, the investor will submit a purchase order for that number of shares.</seg>
<seg id="260"> If only a smaller amount may be purchased, the investor will submit an order for the largest quantity allowed.</seg>
<seg id="261"> If the trade price is above the investor¡¯s reservation price for the security, he or she will wish to sell shares.</seg>
<seg id="262"> In our simulations, investors¡¯ supply curves are upward-sloping¡ªthe larger the number of shares sold, the higher is the resulting new reservation price.</seg>
<seg id="263"> Given this, it is best for the investor to sell shares until the reservation price for an additional share equals the trade price.</seg>
<seg id="264"> If this is feasible, the investor will submit a sell order for that number of shares.</seg>
<seg id="265"> If only a smaller amount may be sold, the investor will submit an order for the largest quantity allowed.</seg>
<seg id="266"> If an investor¡¯s reservation price equals the trade price, he or she will submit neither a sell order nor a buy order.</seg>
<seg id="267"> When stating reservation prices and determining orders, it is assumed investors avoid ¡°playing games.¡±</seg>
<seg id="268"> In large markets, this is likely to be sensible behavior.</seg>
<seg id="269"> But in a market with few investors, an individual might well decide to engage in tactical behavior, providing less than truthful answers to the market maker¡¯s inquiries.</seg>
<seg id="270"> Thus Mario might claim that he would buy HFC shares only if the price were very low, hoping thereby to be able to buy shares at a low price.</seg>
<seg id="271"> Hue might alter her offer to sell HFC shares in one round in the hope that she could thereby obtain a better outcome in a subsequent round, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="272"> We rule out such behavior, assuming that each of the players provides truthful information to the market maker and does not try to take into account possible side effects on other players or on subsequent markets.</seg>
<seg id="273"> We do so because our goal is to simulate a relatively simple trading process that can mirror at least some of the characteristics of the larger markets for which our cases serve as proxies.</seg>
<seg id="274"> An Example of a Market for One Stock</seg>
<seg id="275"> Figure 2-7 illustrates the process of making a market for one security.</seg>
<seg id="276"> The graph shows the relationship between price per share (in present fish) and the number of shares demanded or supplied if the first security traded were HFC stock.</seg>
<seg id="277"> Rounding slightly, Mario¡¯s reservation price is 7, while Hue¡¯s is 5.</seg>
<seg id="278"> This reflects that fact that initially Mario has no HFC stock and Hue has only HFC stock.</seg>
<seg id="279"> In a sense, he wants it more than she does.</seg>
<seg id="280"> At prices above 5, Hue would like to sell HFC shares.</seg>
<seg id="281"> Her supply curve shows that the higher the price, the more shares she will be willing to sell.</seg>
<seg id="282"> Mario is willing to buy shares as long as the price is less than 7. His demand curve shows that the lower the price, the more shares he would like to buy.</seg>
<seg id="283"> At any price between 5 and 7 the two will be willing to trade HFC shares.</seg>
<seg id="284"> In this case, the market maker can concentrate on the range between the two investors¡¯ reservation prices (5 and 7).</seg>
<seg id="285"> The quantity demanded will equal the quantity supplied at a price close to 6, the average of Mario and Hue¡¯s reservation prices. At this price Mario would like to buy 0.68 shares and Hue would like to sell him 0.68 shares.</seg>
<seg id="286"> The market maker thus announces the trade price is 6, takes orders, and executes the trades.</seg>
<seg id="287"> Purchase and Sale Constraints</seg>
<seg id="288"> It might seem that there would be no reason to constrain investors from buying or selling any number of shares they might desire.</seg>
<seg id="289"> Neither Mario nor Hue would voluntarily choose to make a trade that would result in negative consumption in any state as starvation is not an attractive alternative.</seg>
<seg id="290"> But some investors might be happy to take positions that would require net payments to creditors in at least some states of the world.</seg>
<seg id="291"> Why?</seg>
<seg id="292"> Because most countries provide both welfare payments and the possibility of escaping debts by declaring bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="293"> To avoid this possibility the simulated market maker does not allow any investor to submit a bid or ask offer that would, if executed in full, result in consumption in any future state below a minimum level.</seg>
<seg id="294"> This is set at the larger of (1) a very low subsistence level and (2) the investor¡¯s salary in that state.</seg>
<seg id="295"> The first constraint is provided to reduce processing time, and the second to avoid the possibility that an investor will make a promise that can be abrogated by declaring bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="296"> Simulation Precision</seg>
<seg id="297"> In practice, markets and investors do not squeeze every last benefit from the trading process, in part because trading uses up resources.</seg>
<seg id="298"> In our simulations, no expenses are associated with the market making function, but we stop somewhat short of perfection in order to reduce processing time.</seg>
<seg id="299"> Our market maker will not open a market for a security if the highest bid price is only slightly above the lowest ask price.</seg>
<seg id="300"> Similarly, an investor may submit a buy or sell order that would still leave a small gap between his or her reservation price and the trade price.</seg>
<seg id="301"> The required degree of precision for a simulation can be altered, if desired.</seg>
<seg id="302"> For most of the cases in this book, the precision level was set at the APSIM program¡¯s default level to provide a balance between processing time and precision.</seg>
<seg id="303"> The Impact of Trading on Equilibrium Conditions</seg>
<seg id="304"> While our trading procedures enable investors to reach equilibrium, the final equilibrium conditions depend to an extent on both the initial conditions and the way in which trading is conducted.</seg>
<seg id="305"> This is undoubtedly true in the real world as well.</seg>
<seg id="306"> As indicated earlier, no claim is made that the way in which we simulate the trading process is either representative or superior to other possible approaches.</seg>
<seg id="307"> We are interested in the general properties of equilibrium in capital markets, not the specific manner in which such an equilibrium may be reached.</seg>
<seg id="308"> Equilibrium</seg>
<seg id="309"> Many aspects of the equilibrium reached by Mario and Hue are of interest, both in their own right and as illustrations of more general principles.</seg>
<seg id="310"> Portfolios</seg>
<seg id="311"> Figure 2-8 shows the equilibrium portfolios for Mario and Hue, along with the sum of all individuals¡¯ holdings, that is, the market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="312"> Since we assume no transactions costs, the market portfolio is always the same; trading only changes the division of its components among investors.</seg>
<seg id="313"> As can be seen, both Mario and Hue have diversified.</seg>
<seg id="314"> Each holds a replica of the market portfolio of stocks.</seg>
<seg id="315"> In aggregate, there are equal numbers of shares of MFC and HFC stock, and each of the investors chooses to hold equal numbers of shares of the two stocks.</seg>
<seg id="316"> To be sure, Mario has 62.4 percent of each company¡¯s shares (6.24/10.0) while Hue has 37.6 percent (3.76/10.0).</seg>
<seg id="317"> But as long as an investor holds the same percentage of the outstanding shares of available securities, we say that he or she ¡°holds a market portfolio.¡±</seg>
<seg id="318"> Consumption</seg>
<seg id="319"> It is straightforward to determine an individual¡¯s consumption in each state, given portfolio holdings and the security payoffs in the states.</seg>
<seg id="320"> In Figure 2-9 we show the amounts of consumption in each state for each of our actors and for the aggregate (which we term the market consumption).</seg>
<seg id="321"> Not surprisingly, the total amount available for consumption in each state is fixed; the trades only change the allocation among individuals.</seg>
<seg id="322"> Four aspects of the equilibrium situation deserve comment.</seg>
<seg id="323"> First, Mario has arranged to have the same consumption in each of the bad states, as has Hue.</seg>
<seg id="324"> This is also the case for the good states.</seg>
<seg id="325"> Each of them has thus diversified away any risk associated with the eventual division of the total catch between north and south.</seg>
<seg id="326"> Neither investor has chosen to take any non-market risk because such risk is diversifiable.</seg>
<seg id="327"> It is always possible for investors to allocate assets so that no one is subject to non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="328"> In this case, since our investors agreed on the probabilities of alternative future states, it was in their mutual interest to do so.</seg>
<seg id="329"> Second, both Mario and Hue remain vulnerable to the risk arising from uncertainty about the total catch, although to different extents.</seg>
<seg id="330"> Thus they both bear market risk that cannot be diversified away.</seg>
<seg id="331"> Ultimately someone must bear this fundamental societal risk. This is of great importance.</seg>
<seg id="332"> Asset pricing theory focuses on the distinction between the risk associated with the size of the economic pie (market risk) and the risk associated with the division of the pie among securities (non-market risk).</seg>
<seg id="333"> We will see that the two types of risk are associated with very different expected returns.</seg>
<seg id="334"> Third, Mario has chosen to take more market risk than has Hue.</seg>
<seg id="335"> This is not surprising because he is less averse to risk.</seg>
<seg id="336"> The equilibrium portfolios show how this happened.</seg>
<seg id="337"> Hue ended up holding 12.16 bonds, each of which will pay her one fish no matter what the future state may be.</seg>
<seg id="338"> Thus she knows with complete certainty that her bond holdings will provide her with 12.16 fish in the future.</seg>
<seg id="339"> Mario created those bonds for Hue, so he will have to pay her 12.16 fish in the future.</seg>
<seg id="340"> Of course, Hue paid Mario something to effect this transaction, which is why Mario ended up with 6.24 shares of both MFC and HFC stock and Hue with only 3.76 shares of each.</seg>
<seg id="341"> They chose to take different amounts of market risk.</seg>
<seg id="342"> Finally, the actual outcome will have a major impact on the welfare of the investors. This can be seen in the consumptions table.</seg>
<seg id="343"> If times are bad, Mario will be worse off than Hue, with 37.8 fish instead of her 42.2. On the other hand, if times are good, Mario will be better off, with 62.7 fish, compared to Hue¡¯s 57.3.</seg>
<seg id="344"> This highlights an important point about gains through trade under conditions of risk.</seg>
<seg id="345"> After each trade, all parties consider themselves better off.</seg>
<seg id="346"> But when the future arrives and the actual state of the world is determined, some investors will be worse off than if they had not traded at all.</seg>
<seg id="347"> For example, if the state is BadS, Mario will have 37.8 fish, compared with the 50 he would have had without trading. On the other hand, Hue will be better off, with 42.2 fish, compared to her initial amount of 30.</seg>
<seg id="348"> A portfolio choice that is desirable before the fact (ex ante) can turn out to be undesirable after the fact (ex post).</seg>
<seg id="349"> Such is the nature of risk.</seg>
<seg id="350"> Gains through Trade</seg>
<seg id="351"> In this case, two factors contributed to Mario and Hue¡¯s ex ante gains through trade¡ªdiversification and the division of market risk between them.</seg>
<seg id="352"> Here, as in many cases in the real world, large gains from sensible portfolio choice may come from achieving adequate diversification.</seg>
<seg id="353"> Among financial economists, a standard mantra is ¡°diversify, diversify, diversify.¡±</seg>
<seg id="354"> But many investors follow a different path.</seg>
<seg id="355"> For example, many people invest substantial portions of their retirement savings in stocks of the companies that employ them.</seg>
<seg id="356"> This may provide an incentive to work harder but it leaves much to be desired from an investment standpoint.</seg>
<seg id="357"> Concentration of a portfolio in the stock of a single company exposes the owner to substantial non-market risk, which can be avoided through diversification. Mario and Hue know this.</seg>
<seg id="358"> Another drawback associated with investing in company stock is not present in this case, since Mario and Hue do not rely on their employer for any income other than that from their stocks.</seg>
<seg id="359"> But many who hold company stock in retirement plans are subject to some risk that their employer will not provide raises or might lay off employees if profits decline or vanish.</seg>
<seg id="360"> An employee who holds company stock runs the risk of receiving two concurrent sets of bad news: (1) you are out of work and (2) your retirement savings have suffered a large decline.</seg>
<seg id="361"> Absent a compelling argument to the contrary, it is wise to avoid excessive holdings of stock in the company for which one works.</seg>
<seg id="362"> In this case, neither Mario nor Hue takes any non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="363"> But Mario takes more market risk than does Hue.</seg>
<seg id="364"> This is not surprising because Hue is more averse to risk than is Mario.</seg>
<seg id="365"> But why does Mario choose to take more than his share of market risk? The answer is that while his portfolio has more risk than the market portfolio (which is bad), it also has more expected return (which is good).</seg>
<seg id="366"> On the other hand, Hue¡¯s portfolio has less risk and expected return than the market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="367"> Both achieve ex ante gains through trade by taking different levels of market risk.</seg>
<seg id="368"> For some investors, the ex ante gains through trade may not be highly sensitive to small variations in the amount of market risk taken.</seg>
<seg id="369"> One sometimes sees aspects of this in the real world.</seg>
<seg id="370"> When presented with the consequences of alternative efficient investment strategies, some people find it difficult to make a choice, finding it hard to decide on the amount of risk to be taken in the pursuit of higher expected return.</seg>
<seg id="371"> Of course, while differences in ex ante gains through trade associated with different efficient portfolios may be small, differences in ex post results can be very large.</seg>
<seg id="372"> Asset Prices</seg>
<seg id="373"> Thus far we have focused on Mario and Hue¡¯s portfolio choices. It is time to turn to asset prices.</seg>
<seg id="374"> Security prices are used for many purposes, including portfolio valuation.</seg>
<seg id="375"> For example, a mutual fund may calculate its net asset value at the end of the day using reported closing prices for the component securities.</seg>
<seg id="376"> Typically, the closing price is the price at which the last transaction took place prior to an official ¡°closing time¡± (4 p.m. EST for U.S. securities).</seg>
<seg id="377"> Famously, the transaction in question might have taken place just before the close or considerably earlier (a phenomenon that has led some investment managers to attempt to profit at the expense of other shareholders).</seg>
<seg id="378"> Some bond funds value their holdings using the highest available bid price for each security, in an attempt to determine the value for which the portfolio could be sold.</seg>
<seg id="379"> When closing prices are likely to be too stale, equity funds may use a ¡°fair value,¡± which is often estimated by averaging the highest bid and lowest ask price.</seg>
<seg id="380"> For most purposes, one is really interested in the next price, not the last.</seg>
<seg id="381"> At what price could current holdings of a security be sold?</seg>
<seg id="382"> At what price could additional shares be purchased? Answers to these questions require information on current ask and bid prices, respectively.</seg>
<seg id="383"> We follow this approach, using the reservation prices of the investors for each of the securities after trading has ceased.</seg>
<seg id="384"> The results for this case are shown in Figure 2-10.</seg>
<seg id="385"> In this equilibrium, both Mario and Hue were able to purchase or sell shares of the securities if they desired.</seg>
<seg id="386"> For each security, their reservation prices were the same (to two decimal places) and hence there was no basis for further gains through trade. This is not surprising.</seg>
<seg id="387"> If there were a significant difference between Mario¡¯s reservation price for a security and Hue¡¯s reservation price, each could gain through further trading.</seg>
<seg id="388"> Absent constraints on trading, investors will adjust their portfolios until their reservation prices for a security differ by less than the threshold amount required to make a market.</seg>
<seg id="389"> The top line in the security prices table shows the market price for each security.</seg>
<seg id="390"> This is the price at which an additional round of trade would take place if a market were to be conducted following the simulated trading procedure.</seg>
<seg id="391"> For all the cases in this book, the market price for a security is calculated by (1) averaging the reservation prices of all investors who could purchase shares, (2) averaging the reservation prices of all investors who could sell shares, and then (3) finding the average of the first two amounts.</seg>
<seg id="392"> In most cases, no investors are bound by constraints on trading so the market price is simply the average of all the investors¡¯ reservation prices.</seg>
<seg id="393"> Security Returns</seg>
<seg id="394"> The return on a security depends on its price and its payoff at a future date, and the payoff depends on the future state.</seg>
<seg id="395"> It is straightforward to compute the return on a security in each state.</seg>
<seg id="396"> For example, MFC stock costs 4.35 and pays 5.00 in state BadS.</seg>
<seg id="397"> Thus an investment of 1 fish returns 1.149 fish in that state. This is often reported as a percentage change¡ªhere, 14.9 percent.</seg>
<seg id="398"> For convenience, throughout we will use total returns, calculated by dividing future payoffs by the current price.</seg>
<seg id="399"> The returns on the securities for this case are shown in Figure 2-11.</seg>
<seg id="400"> Portfolio Returns</seg>
<seg id="401"> We can also compute the returns on investment portfolios, which exclude the first security (present consumption).</seg>
<seg id="402"> To do so we compute the value of all the other investment securities (by multiplying the price of each security by the number of shares held and summing the products), then divide the total amount received from the portfolio in each state by the initial value of the portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="403"> Figure 2-12 shows the returns in each state for Mario and Hue¡¯s portfolios and for the market portfolio, which includes the holdings of all investors.</seg>
<seg id="404"> Portfolio and Market Returns</seg>
<seg id="405"> The information in Figure 2-12 is graphed in Figure 2-13.</seg>
<seg id="406"> Each point represents a state and a portfolio, with the market return plotted on the horizontal axis and the portfolio return on the vertical axis.</seg>
<seg id="407"> For convenience, points for the same portfolio are connected with lines.</seg>
<seg id="408"> In this case, the steepest line shows Mario¡¯s returns, the least steep shows Hue¡¯s returns, and the line in the middle shows the returns on the market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="409"> In Figure 2-13 there appear to be only two points for each portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="410"> In fact, there are four.</seg>
<seg id="411"> But for each portfolio the return is the same in each of the two bad market states and the return is the same in each of the two good market states.</seg>
<seg id="412"> The result is that each portfolio¡¯s points fall on a curve (here a line).</seg>
<seg id="413"> This is a result of great relevance.</seg>
<seg id="414"> The only source of uncertainty for either Mario or Hue is the overall return on the market.</seg>
<seg id="415"> Each takes only market risk.</seg>
<seg id="416"> Non-market risk can be diversified away and neither Mario nor Hue has chosen to take any of it.</seg>
<seg id="417"> Thus each of the investors is following the advice of the MRRC.</seg>
<seg id="418"> Graphs of this type will prove central in this book.</seg>
<seg id="419"> Any individual for whom portfolio outcomes plot on a single curve in such a diagram can be said to follow a market-based strategy and to take only market risk.</seg>
<seg id="420"> The curve does not have to be a straight line for this to be the case, but the portfolio must provide the same return in all states of the world with a given market return.</seg>
<seg id="421"> For a given investor any scatter of y values for a given x value in such a return graph shows that the investor is taking non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="422"> Expected Returns</seg>
<seg id="423"> In Case 1 there is complete agreement among the investors about the probabilities of alternative states, so we can unambiguously compute the expected return for any security or portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="424"> This is simply a weighted average of the returns in the alternative future states, using the probabilities of the states as weights.</seg>
<seg id="425"> Expected returns occupy center stage in much of finance theory.</seg>
<seg id="426"> Later we cover cases in which people view expected returns differently. But this cannot happen when there is agreement on probabilities.</seg>
<seg id="427"> The expected returns for our securities are shown in Figure 2-14.</seg>
<seg id="428"> The expected return for the bond is, of course, its actual return in all states.</seg>
<seg id="429"> Thus the riskless rate of interest is 4.4 percent.</seg>
<seg id="430"> Both stocks provide higher expected returns (12.6 percent and 12.4 percent). This is not surprising, because their returns are risky.</seg>
<seg id="431"> As we will see, the relevant risk in this regard is the portion related to uncertainty about the overall market, but this discussion must await a richer example.</seg>
<seg id="432"> The portfolio expected returns are shown in Figure 2-15.</seg>
<seg id="433"> Mario¡¯s expected return is considerably greater than that of the market; Hue¡¯s is the smallest of the three.</seg>
<seg id="434"> Mario expects to beat the market by 2.1 percent and Hue expects to underperform it by 2.0 percent.</seg>
<seg id="435"> Of course, if things go badly, Mario will have fewer fish than Hue, as we have seen.</seg>
<seg id="436"> This is a standard property of equilibrium¡ªwith good news (higher expected return) there is bad news (the possibility of worse results).</seg>
<seg id="437"> Mario takes on greater risk and has a higher expected return. Because he has more tolerance for risk, he has chosen a higher combination of risk and expected return.</seg>
<seg id="438"> 2.9.9 Risk Premia</seg>
<seg id="439"> For many purposes it is useful to focus on the difference between a return and the riskless rate of interest. This is usually termed an excess return.</seg>
<seg id="440"> The difference between an expected return and the riskless rate is thus equal to the expected excess return. More commonly, it is termed a risk premium.</seg>
<seg id="441"> Much of financial theory and practice is devoted to the estimation of risk premia and their determinants.</seg>
<seg id="442"> The security and portfolio risk premia for this case are shown in Figures 2-16 and 2-17.</seg>
<seg id="443"> The market risk premium in this case is 8.1 percent per year.</seg>
<seg id="444"> Mario¡¯s expected excess return is greater because he has chosen to be more exposed to market uncertainty. Hue¡¯s is less because she has chosen to be less exposed to market uncertainty.</seg>
<seg id="445"> We can now succinctly characterize key aspects of the equilibrium:</seg>
<seg id="446"> The reward for waiting is 4.4 percent.</seg>
<seg id="447"> The reward for taking market risk is 8.1 percent.</seg>
<seg id="448"> These are plausible results.</seg>
<seg id="449"> Of course they depend on both demand and supply conditions.</seg>
<seg id="450"> The key drivers of demand are investors¡¯ preferences and positions, while supply is represented by security payoffs, the total available numbers of shares of those securities, and probabilities of the alternative states.</seg>
<seg id="451"> In this book we consider only exchange economies, in which supply is fixed, with prices and investors¡¯ holdings determined by the terms on which investors trade available securities.</seg>
<seg id="452"> To obtain plausible results thus requires plausible inputs.</seg>
<seg id="453"> For most of the cases we experimented with different amounts for the total amount of consumption now (security 1) until a plausible expected bond return (reward for waiting) was obtained.</seg>
<seg id="454"> For example, in Case 1 the expected future consumption is 104.</seg>
<seg id="455"> In a typical economy, expected future consumption is greater than current consumption.</seg>
<seg id="456"> To reflect such a condition we thus set the current consumption to be lower than 104.</seg>
<seg id="457"> A level of 100 gave an equilibrium with a very small riskless rate of interest. This led to our choice of a total initial consumption of 98.</seg>
<seg id="458"> It may seem strange that the characteristics of equilibrium in this type of exchange economy are so sensitive to the inputs.</seg>
<seg id="459"> But this should not be a surprise.</seg>
<seg id="460"> If available productive investments lead to a very low interest rate, firms will raise new funds to undertake productive investments offering greater returns.</seg>
<seg id="461"> This will lower the total amount available for current consumption and raise the amounts to be received in various future states of the world, leading to a higher interest rate.</seg>
<seg id="462"> Our goal is to create cases that reflect plausible prospects for security payoffs.</seg>
<seg id="463"> This often leads to the choice of an amount of current consumption designed to accord with a longer run equilibrium process.</seg>
<seg id="464"> Summary</seg>
<seg id="465"> Case 1 describes an extremely simple economy, yet produces an equilibrium with many features of standard asset pricing models.</seg>
<seg id="466"> In particular, our actors, trading with no ultimate social goal in mind, end with positions for which the MRRT holds.</seg>
<seg id="467"> Moreover, each adopts a portfolio consistent with the MRRC, as neither chose to take non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="468"> To see why they make these choices we need to go deeper.</seg>
<seg id="469"> Such is the task of the next two chapters.</seg>
<seg id="470"> PRICES</seg>
<seg id="471"> THIS CHAPTER FOCUSES on prices¡ªthe prices of both securities and the state claims introduced in Chapter 3.</seg>
<seg id="472"> Of particular interest are the relationships among expected returns, various measures of risk, and measures of responsiveness to changes in market-wide variables.</seg>
<seg id="473"> We introduce alternative versions of the Market Risk/Reward Theorem (MRRT) and investigate the conditions under which one or more version may hold.</seg>
<seg id="474"> As in previous chapters, we assume that investors agree on the probabilities of future states, have no outside positions, and discount the utilities from all states at a given time in the same way, leaving for future chapters the investigation of the characteristics of equilibrium when some or all of these assumptions are violated.</seg>
<seg id="475"> Complete Markets</seg>
<seg id="476"> We have defined a state claim as a security that pays 1 unit if and only if a specific state occurs.</seg>
<seg id="477"> We have also argued that such state claims can be considered the atoms of which actual securities are made.</seg>
<seg id="478"> While some such claims may be traded explicitly, this is rare.</seg>
<seg id="479"> Nonetheless it is extremely useful to consider the characteristics of a world in which all possible state claims can be traded.</seg>
<seg id="480"> Such a world is defined as a complete market.</seg>
<seg id="481"> If financial institutions and markets operated without cost, everyone could trade their standard securities for state claims in the manner described in Chapter 3.</seg>
<seg id="482"> However, it might be more efficient if a market maker simply opened a market for trades in one or more state claims, with sellers able to create them and buyers able to purchase them.</seg>
<seg id="483"> In such a case, each state claim, like the bond in our previous cases, would be in zero net supply.</seg>
<seg id="484"> And, as with other such instruments, buyers would have to make certain that issuers (sellers) could de-liver on their promises.</seg>
<seg id="485"> To simulate a complete market we adopt a sequential approach.</seg>
<seg id="486"> We let investors reach an equilibrium using available standard securities; we then open a market for trading state claims.</seg>
<seg id="487"> The possible trading procedures used by the market maker for state claims are the same as those used for standard securities.</seg>
<seg id="488"> In all the cases in this book, we use the simple type of price discovery in which trades are made in each market using a price halfway between the average of the reservation prices of investors who are able to buy and the average of the reservation prices of those who are able to sell.</seg>
<seg id="489"> In the real world, of course, there are costs of all types, financial and otherwise, so a complete market is at best an approximation to reality.</seg>
<seg id="490"> In some cases it may be a good approximation; in others a poor one. We explore these issues in detail later in this chapter.</seg>
<seg id="491"> First we examine the characteristics of prices, risks, and expected returns in a complete market.</seg>
<seg id="492"> Case 6: Quade, Dagmar, and the Index Funds</seg>
<seg id="493"> Case 6 involves only two investors, Quade and Dagmar, but they operate in a considerably richer environment than encountered in earlier examples.</seg>
<seg id="494"> There are now ten future states of the world and six standard securities.</seg>
<seg id="495"> Figure 4-1 shows the securities table and Figure 4-2 the initial portfolios table.</seg>
<seg id="496"> Those with backgrounds in investments may be familiar with the names of most of these securities.</seg>
<seg id="497"> The first is, of course, our usual representation of present consumption.</seg>
<seg id="498"> The second is a riskless security representing borrowing and lending¡ªin this case it is called ¡°STBond¡± to indicate that it is a short-term bond.</seg>
<seg id="499"> Each of the remaining securities is an index fund¡ªthat is, a portfolio that includes proportionate holdings of all the securities of a given type.</seg>
<seg id="500"> In this case, it is assumed that every individual security is included in one and only one of the five index funds, which represent, respectively, government bonds, non-government bonds, large value stocks, large growth stocks, and small stocks.</seg>
<seg id="501"> The latter three funds are typical of equity index funds created by financial firms, with each individual stock included in a specific index fund based on the market capitalization of its company¡¯s shares and the relationship between its market price and the characteristics of one or more of its accounting measures (e.g., per share book value or earnings).</seg>
<seg id="502"> The index fund returns and initial holdings shown in Figures 4-1 and 4-2 are designed to be reasonably representative of the total returns per dollar invested that might be obtained with different types of economic conditions.</seg>
<seg id="503"> As the state names indicate, there are different overall levels of the economy and total returns from the market portfolio (e.g., depression, recession) and, for each overall level, a different allocation of the total market return across the available investments.</seg>
<seg id="504"> Before trading begins, both investors hold market portfolios and avoid non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="505"> Thus Quade will have the same ending value in state Depression1 as in state Depression2, as will Dagmar.</seg>
<seg id="506"> This will also be the case for every other pair of states with the same level of the economy.</seg>

<seg id="508"> Figure 4-3 shows the probabilities table. The least likely states are associated with depression and boom times.</seg>
<seg id="509"> Recession and prosperity states are more likely, and the most likely outcomes are those associated with normal economic times.</seg>
<seg id="510"> Finally, for each possible level of overall economic outcome, there are two equally likely divisions of the overall output of the economy.</seg>
<seg id="511"> The investors¡¯ names provide a clue to their preferences.</seg>
<seg id="512"> Quade has the somewhat improbable preferences represented by a quadratic utility function; thus his risk aversion increases with consumption.</seg>
<seg id="513"> Dagmar¡¯s preferences are less surprising; her relative risk aversion decreases with consumption.</seg>
<seg id="514"> Figure 4-4 shows their discounts and utility parameters, using the conventions described in Chapter 3.</seg>
<seg id="515"> Equilibrium with Conventional Securities</seg>
<seg id="516"> To examine the characteristics of a market with conventional securities we let Quade and Dagmar trade using the short-term bond and the five index funds.</seg>
<seg id="517"> When trading stops we find that our investors have chosen to hold very different portfolios, as shown in Figure 4-5.</seg>
<seg id="518"> Quade is more conservative, choosing to lend $211.4 to Dagmar.</seg>
<seg id="519"> But otherwise it is hard to discern a pattern.</seg>
<seg id="520"> Quade holds more than his proportionate share of nongovernment bonds and value stocks and less than his proportionate share of government bonds, growth stocks, and small stocks.</seg>
<seg id="521"> This is not because they disagree about the future prospects of the securities.</seg>
<seg id="522"> Nor are they adjusting their portfolios to better fit with other sources of income because there are no outside positions.</seg>
<seg id="523"> What is going on here?</seg>
<seg id="524"> The returns graph shown in Figure 4-6 sheds considerable light on this question.</seg>
<seg id="525"> Note that both investors have chosen to take non-market risk, as shown by the fact that neither portfolio has returns that plot as a strictly increasing function of the return on the overall market.</seg>
<seg id="526"> For example, Dagmar, the more aggressive investor, will have different returns in the two boom states (shown at the right of the graph), as will Quade.</seg>
<seg id="527"> This is the case for other levels of market return as well.</seg>
<seg id="528"> Neither investor obeys the Market Risk/Reward Corollary (MRRC).</seg>
<seg id="529"> There is more.</seg>
<seg id="530"> Neither investor¡¯s returns fall near a straight line relating portfolio returns to market returns.</seg>
<seg id="531"> Dagmar¡¯s returns fall closer to a curve that becomes steeper, going from left to right, while Quade¡¯s returns fall closer to a curve that becomes flatter as one proceeds in that direction.</seg>
<seg id="532"> Why?</seg>
<seg id="533"> Because they have very different marginal utility functions.</seg>
<seg id="534"> Dagmar becomes less risk averse as she grows richer while Quade becomes more risk averse.</seg>
<seg id="535"> More specifically, in better times Dagmar¡¯s risk aversion increases relative to the average of all investors while Quade¡¯s decreases relative to the average.</seg>
<seg id="536"> They can each gain in expected utility by arranging for their portfolio returns to reflect this, as they do in Figure 4-6.</seg>
<seg id="537"> But they cannot do this with combinations of the market portfolio and borrowing and lending, so they resort to doing the best that they can with the existing securities.</seg>
<seg id="538"> As a result, they take on non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="539"> Figure 4-7 shows the security prices and our two investors¡¯ reservation prices for the securities (to three decimal places).</seg>
<seg id="540"> Not surprisingly, there are no further gains to be made by trading in conventional securities.</seg>
<seg id="541"> Despite the calm exterior of this equilibrium, our investors are nonetheless frustrated, as can be seen in the state prices table shown in Figure 4-8.</seg>
<seg id="542"> Dagmar would be willing to pay up to 0.085 to buy a claim for 1 unit of consumption if the Depression1 state occurs, while Quade would be willing to sell one for any price above 0.079.</seg>
<seg id="543"> Both could gain by trading at any price between these amounts.</seg>
<seg id="544"> There are also disparities in their reservations prices for other state claims.</seg>
<seg id="545"> Such situations are seldom ignored by financial services firms.</seg>
<seg id="546"> If new securities can attract enough buyers and sellers to more than offset the costs of trading, there is seldom a dearth of firms seeking to profit from the introduction of such securities and/or the creation of markets for trading them.</seg>
<seg id="547"> In this case, there are no markets for trading state claims. But we can determine the prices at which such claims might start trading.</seg>
<seg id="548"> The entries in the top row in Figure 4-8 show the prices that would be chosen by a market maker using the simplest form of our price discovery process. Each price is halfway between the average reservation price for those able to purchase the claim and the average reservation price for those able to sell the claim.</seg>
<seg id="549"> In this case, the ¡°market prices¡± are simply the averages of the two investors¡¯ reservation prices.</seg>
<seg id="550"> Case 7: Quade and Dagmar in a Complete Market</seg>
<seg id="551"> In Case 7 we show compassion for Quade and Dagmar by letting them trade state claims, but only after they have reached an equilibrium using conventional securities.</seg>
<seg id="552"> The trading process is the same as that used for the conventional securities, with each state claim traded in turn and rounds of trading conducted until no trades have been made in a complete round.</seg>
<seg id="553"> As with Case 6, the simple type of price discovery based on the reservation prices is utilized, using the same required level of precision chosen for trading conventional securities.</seg>
<seg id="554"> As always in our simulations, market makers work for nothing.</seg>
<seg id="555"> In every other respect, Case 7 is the same as Case 6.</seg>
<seg id="556"> The only differences arise from the trading in state claims conducted after the conventional security trading is finished (in fact, Case 7 was produced by using the inputs for Case 6 and changing one control variable).</seg>
<seg id="557"> Not surprisingly, the equilibrium portfolios of conventional securities are the same as in Case 6 (previously shown in Figure 4-5).</seg>
<seg id="558"> But in addition, there are now holdings in state claims, as shown in Figure 4-9.</seg>
<seg id="559"> This provides further evidence that our investors were not satisfied with their ability to adequately divide economic outcomes using only available conventional securities.</seg>
<seg id="560"> Since they chose to trade state claims, each believed that he or she was better off as a result.</seg>
<seg id="561"> Figure 4-10 shows what Quade and Dagmar accomplished by making these trades.</seg>
<seg id="562"> They take no non-market risk, and obtain returns that fall nicely on curves that reflect the disparate ways in which their risk aversions change as they become richer.</seg>
<seg id="563"> Were there more states of the world this would be even more evident, since the lines we use to connect points visually would fall closer to the true underlying curves.</seg>
<seg id="564"> In this complete market, the MRRC is not dead.</seg>
<seg id="565"> Since Quade and Dagmar are both risk averse it makes sense for them to avoid non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="566"> Given a rich enough investment environment they can do so and still take on appropriate amounts of market risk for different levels of market return.</seg>
<seg id="567"> A complete market allows them to do just this.</seg>
<seg id="568"> State and Security Prices</seg>
<seg id="569"> Figure 4-11 shows the state prices after the trading in state claims has concluded.</seg>
<seg id="570"> As expected, for each state claim the investors¡¯ reservations prices are the same (to three decimal places) and equal to the market price.</seg>
<seg id="571"> Note also that the price for a claim to $1 in state Depression1 is $0.082, as is the price for a claim to $1 in state Depression2.</seg>
<seg id="572"> As we will see, this reflects the facts that total consumption is the same in each state and that the two states are equally probable.</seg>
<seg id="573"> Similar relationships apply for each of the other pairs of states representing different divisions of a pie of the same size.</seg>
<seg id="574"> While the ability to trade all state claims precludes any need for our investors to return to trading conventional securities, it is important to recognize that if such trades were contemplated, the equilibrium prices for the securities could differ from those in Case 6. However, the differences are small in this case.</seg>

<seg id="577"> Price per Chance</seg>
<seg id="578"> What are the determinants of the state prices shown in Figure 4-11?</seg>
<seg id="579"> Certainly probabilities must be relevant.</seg>
<seg id="580"> Other things equal, one would expect to pay more for a security that provides a given payment in a more likely state.</seg>
<seg id="581"> But other things are not equal in this case.</seg>
<seg id="582"> Note, for example, that it costs $0.082 to obtain $1 in state Depression1 and only $0.061 to obtain $1 in state Prosperity1, despite the fact that the latter state is twice as likely to occur.</seg>
<seg id="583"> To help explain this phenomenon and to provide a foundation for much that is to come, we introduce a well-known concept using a somewhat novel name.</seg>
<seg id="584"> We define the price per chance (PPC) for a state claim as its price divided by the probability that the state will occur.</seg>
<seg id="585"> In a complete market, state prices are observable.</seg>
<seg id="586"> And when investors agree on probabilities, so are probabilities (just ask anyone).</seg>
<seg id="587"> The net result is that PPCs are unique and observable.</seg>
<seg id="588"> As we will see later, this is not always so.</seg>
<seg id="589"> For now, however, we focus on cases involving agreement and complete markets, where PPCs are knowable by all.</seg>
<seg id="590"> A state¡¯s PPC provides a better indication than the state price alone of the extent to which a state claim is cheap or expensive.</seg>
<seg id="591"> To see why, imagine a situation in which an agent offers you an insurance policy that will pay $1,000 if your computer is stolen this year.</seg>
<seg id="592"> The policy costs $60, or 6 cents (0.06) per dollar of coverage.</seg>
<seg id="593"> Should you buy the policy? The answer depends on your assessment of the chance that the computer will be stolen.</seg>
<seg id="594"> If you believe that there is an 8 percent chance, you are more likely to buy the policy than if you think that the chance is 4 percent.</seg>
<seg id="595"> In the former case, the PPC is 0.06/0.08, or 0.75; in the latter it is 0.06/0.04, or 1.50.</seg>
<seg id="596"> The lower the PPC, the more attractive the offer.</seg>
<seg id="597"> We can compute PPC values for investors, using their reservation prices for state claims.</seg>
<seg id="598"> We can also do so for the market, using the market prices for the claims.</seg>
<seg id="599"> Two features are notable. First, it is cheaper to obtain a given chance of consumption in a good (high aggregate output) state of the world than in a bad one.</seg>
<seg id="600"> Second, it costs the same for a given chance of consumption in states that have the same aggregate output.</seg>
<seg id="601"> This is not an artifact of the particular numeric values in this case. Instead, it follows from the quite reasonable assumptions about investor choice that we have made and that underlie much of asset pricing theory.</seg>
<seg id="602"> PPCs and Consumption</seg>
<seg id="603"> Equilibrium is established when people stop trading.</seg>
<seg id="604"> In a complete market, state prices are determined and, given agreement on probabilities, PPC values are known to all.</seg>
<seg id="605"> The causation runs from portfolio choice to asset prices.</seg>
<seg id="606"> But we can view equilibrium in a different manner.</seg>
<seg id="607"> After it is established, imagine telling Dagmar that she can buy and sell any desired amount of each state claim at its equilibrium state price.</seg>
<seg id="608"> After giving the matter some thought she would choose precisely her current portfolio and pattern of consumption over states.</seg>
<seg id="609"> If Quade were given the same opportunities, he would choose his current portfolio and pattern of consumption.</seg>
<seg id="610"> The situation is the same as if the prices caused the investors to choose their current portfolios and consumption levels.</seg>
<seg id="611"> We have argued that in choosing how to respond to these questions, investors look not at state prices, but at PPC values.</seg>
<seg id="612"> Figure 4-12 shows the results, allowing comparisons of the PPC values for the future states with the amounts consumed.</seg>
<seg id="613"> Note that if two states cost the same (have the same PPC), Quade chooses to consume the same amount in each state.</seg>
<seg id="614"> But if one state is cheaper (has a lower PPC) than another, he chooses to consume more in the cheaper state.</seg>
<seg id="615"> Dagmar exhibits similar behavior.</seg>
<seg id="616"> Why do our investors act this way?</seg>
<seg id="617"> The reason is that each feels that ¡°other things equal, the more consumption that I have, the less valuable is one unit more or less.¡±</seg>
<seg id="618"> More formally, as we showed in Chapter 3, each has a marginal utility curve that is downward-sloping when plotted against consumption.</seg>
<seg id="619"> Absent constraints on holdings, in a complete market equilibrium an investor will trade until his or her reservation price equals the market price for the state claim.</seg>
<seg id="620"> Importantly, the expression to the right of the equal sign is the PPC for the state.</seg>
<seg id="621"> Consider two states with the same PPC.</seg>
<seg id="622"> To maximize expected utility, an investor will choose a portfolio with the same consumption in each state in the same time period; otherwise, there will be a discrepancy between his or her reservation price and the market price in one or both states.</seg>
<seg id="623"> Consider next two states, j and k, with state j having a higher PPC than state k. An investor will choose a portfolio that provides a higher reservation price per chance in state j. This requires consumption that leads to a higher marginal utility of consumption, that is, to less consumption.</seg>
<seg id="624"> Thus the investor will choose to have less consumption in state j than in state k.</seg>
<seg id="625"> In the setting of Case 7, we reach the highly reasonable conclusion that each investor will choose the same consumption in states that cost the same (have equal PPCs) and each will choose less consumption in states that are more expensive (have higher PPCs).</seg>
<seg id="626"> The Pricing Kernel</seg>
<seg id="627"> As we have seen, once there is equilibrium in a complete market with agreement we can talk meaningfully about market PPCs. Conventionally, the set of market PPCs is called the pricing kernel.</seg>
<seg id="628"> A key aspect of such an equilibrium is that shown in Figure 4-15, which plots the relationship between the market PPCs (on the vertical axis) and total market consumptions (on the horizontal axis) from Case 7.</seg>
<seg id="629"> For convenience, the points representing the states are connected with lines; however, only the points themselves are relevant.</seg>
<seg id="630"> While there are ten future states of the world in Case 7, only five separate points are visible in the graph because each point includes two states, since states with the same aggregate consumption have the same PPC.</seg>
<seg id="631"> When the points representing future states are connected, the resulting curve is downward-sloping.</seg>
<seg id="632"> These are characteristics of complete market equilibrium when investors¡¯ marginal utilities decrease with consumption and the other conditions of this case hold (agreement, state-independent utilities, no outside positions, and the absence of binding constraints).</seg>
<seg id="633"> Market-Based Strategies</seg>
<seg id="634"> We now have the pieces in place to show that in this type of market, every investor will choose to take only market risk and hence will abide by the MRRC.</seg>
<seg id="635"> We know that in such a setting each investor will choose more consumption in cheaper (lower PPC) states and the same amount in equally expensive (PPC) states.</seg>
<seg id="636"> But total consumption is simply the sum of the amounts consumed by all investors. Thus total consumption will be the same in states with the same PPC values and lower in states with lower PPC values.</seg>
<seg id="637"> Aggregate consumption will be inversely related (only) to PPC.</seg>
<seg id="638"> But then it follows immediately that:</seg>
<seg id="639"> Each investor¡¯s consumption will be directly related (only) to aggregate consumption.</seg>
<seg id="640"> In short, each investor will follow a market-based strategy.</seg>
<seg id="641"> Expected Return and Aggregate Consumption</seg>
<seg id="642"> Only a few steps remain to reach a general version of the MRRT that states that only market risk is rewarded with higher expected return.</seg>
<seg id="643"> To start, we need to compute the expected return in each state.</seg>
<seg id="644"> This is simple enough.</seg>
<seg id="645"> A state¡¯s PPC is its price divided by its probability. The reciprocal is the probability divided by the price.</seg>
<seg id="646"> Since a state claim pays $1 if the state in question occurs, the probability of the state (times 1) is the expected payment. Dividing this by the price gives the expected total return for the claim.</seg>
<seg id="647"> At the risk of belaboring the obvious, Figure 4-16 repeats the information in Figure 4-14, with an extra row for the expected total return in each future state.</seg>
<seg id="648"> We know that in this setting PPCs are the same for states with the same market return and lower for states with higher market return.</seg>
<seg id="649"> It follows that expected returns must be the same for states with the same market return and higher for states with higher market return.</seg>
<seg id="650"> An investor who takes non-market risk chooses a portfolio that provides different payoffs in states with the same market return. But in this setting such states have the same expected return.</seg>
<seg id="651"> Thus there is no reward in higher expected return associated with non-market risk. This establishes part of the MRRT: there is no reward in higher expected return associated with non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="652"> It remains to show that there is a reward in higher expected return associated with taking at least some type of market risk.</seg>
<seg id="653"> To do so it is useful to introduce the concept of market states.</seg>
<seg id="654"> Market States</seg>
<seg id="655"> In all our cases, we have arranged the states in increasing order of aggregate consumption. This may have seemed arbitrary, but the reason should now be clear.</seg>
<seg id="656"> By doing so, all states with the same aggregate consumption are adjacent in a single group, with the groups in increasing order of consumption.</seg>
<seg id="657"> Figure 4-17 shows new market states for Case 7 called, not surprisingly, Depression, Recession, Normality, Prosperity, and Boom.</seg>
<seg id="658"> Each market state combines all the states with the same market return, has a probability equal to the sum of the probabilities of its substates and a state price equal to the sum of the state prices of its substates.</seg>
<seg id="659"> Since each of the states within such a group has the same PPC, the market state has the same PPC as its substates.</seg>
<seg id="660"> This is also true for the expected returns.</seg>
<seg id="661"> Market Risk and Reward</seg>
<seg id="662"> Our next goal is to show that it is possible to obtain a higher expected return by taking market risk.</seg>
<seg id="663"> To start, consider the individual¡¯s decision concerning allocation of investment across market states.</seg>
<seg id="664"> Assume that his or her budget is just enough to purchase a riskless security that pays 1.00 in every state.</seg>
<seg id="665"> Figure 4-18 shows the relevant calculations.</seg>
<seg id="666"> Importantly, the expected return for the portfolio is a weighted average of the expected returns of its components, with the proportions of value used as weights.</seg>
<seg id="667"> Now consider reducing the payment in the Depression market state enough to lower the associated value by 1 cent (0.010), and using the proceeds to increase the payment in the Boom market state as much as possible.</seg>
<seg id="668"> The results are shown in Figure 4-19.</seg>
<seg id="669"> Because money was taken from a market state with a lower expected return and put in a market state with a higher expected return.</seg>
<seg id="670"> As a result, the portfolio expected return, which is a value-weighted average of the component expected returns, increased.</seg>
<seg id="671"> The greater the amount of money reallocated in this manner, the larger will be the portfolio¡¯s expected return.</seg>
<seg id="672"> This shows that taking market risk can increase expected return, but only if it is done intelligently.</seg>
<seg id="673"> For example, we could have started with the riskless security, then taken money from a high expected return state and used it to buy more consumption in a lower expected return state.</seg>
<seg id="674"> This would have reduced expected return to a level below the riskless rate! Clearly, not all types of market risk are rewarded.</seg>
<seg id="675"> This issue is easily dismissed.</seg>
<seg id="676"> Our investors will choose more consumption in cheaper (lower PPC) states.</seg>
<seg id="677"> But these are higher expected return states.</seg>
<seg id="678"> Thus the only market risk they will (and should) take will be associated with upward-sloping portfolio return curves such as the ones shown earlier in Figure 4-2.</seg>
<seg id="679"> In this case, Dagmar¡¯s return curve is steeper than Quade¡¯s. She takes more market risk, but is rewarded with a higher expected return, as shown in Figure 4-17.</seg>
<seg id="680"> We still need a formal measure of market risk to give specific meaning to the MRRT.</seg>
<seg id="681"> We will do so in stages, obtaining a series of asset pricing formulas of increasing specificity.</seg>
<seg id="682"> Asset Pricing Formulas</seg>
<seg id="683"> Cases with agreement, complete markets, investors with decreasing marginal utility, state-independent utility, and no outside positions have a number of properties, each of which figures importantly in asset pricing theory.</seg>
<seg id="684"> We present them here in sequence without caveats.</seg>
<seg id="685"> In subsequent chapters we will consider the extent to which each may or may not hold in more general settings.</seg>
<seg id="686"> The Law of One Price</seg>
<seg id="687"> Anyone able to buy and sell state claims at market prices can construct any desired type of security.</seg>
<seg id="688"> Now, imagine that a security is trading for a price that differs from the price of such a replicating portfolio of state claims. Any clever investor could then make money by purchasing the cheaper alternative and selling the more expensive one.</seg>
<seg id="689"> No matter what the future state of the world, the purchased asset would generate enough money to make the payment required for the asset that was sold.</seg>
<seg id="690"> The difference between the lower purchase price and higher sales price could be spent today, providing truly ¡°something for nothing.¡± Opportunities for this kind of arbitrage are few and fleeting.</seg>
<seg id="691"> In the vast majority of markets at almost all times, they are unavailable.</seg>
<seg id="692"> In a complete market, the price of an asset will equal the price of a replicating portfolio of state claims.</seg>
<seg id="693"> Each state¡¯s payment is priced by multiplying it by the price per unit, and the results are then summed to obtain the price or present value of the security.</seg>
<seg id="694"> A variant of the LOP is widely used in financial engineering.</seg>
<seg id="695"> It is simply a transformation that adds nothing to our version and sometimes conceals the underlying economics.</seg>
<seg id="696"> For completeness we will describe it briefly.</seg>
<seg id="697"> In our version, ps is the price that must be paid today to receive 1 unit at the future date if and only if state s occurs.</seg>
<seg id="698"> The sum of all these state prices is the amount that must be paid today to receive 1 unit at the future date with certainty, since one of the states will occur.</seg>
<seg id="699"> Thus if the sum of the state prices is 0.96, a dollar at the future date costs $0.96 today.</seg>
<seg id="700"> Denote this d.</seg>
<seg id="701"> Now, imagine that you wish to receive $1 at the future date if and only if state s occurs.</seg>
<seg id="702"> You can borrow ps dollars to buy the state claim, which will require you to pay ps/d at the future date, whether state s occurs or not.</seg>
<seg id="703"> This amount is known as the forward price (fs) of a claim for a dollar in state s. One could contract directly to pay fs for each payment Xis; the sum of these amounts would be the forward cost of security i. Multiplying this by the discount factor would give the current price.</seg>
<seg id="704"> Thus the LOP can be stated as follows. Multiply each payment by the forward price of a claim in the corresponding state, then discount the sum using the riskless rate of interest.</seg>
<seg id="705"> Many financial engineers use this approach, calling each forward price a ¡°risk-neutral probability,¡± although this obscures the underlying economics.</seg>
<seg id="706"> While the forward prices will sum to 1 (since a dollar should cost a dollar), they will typically differ from true probabilities.</seg>
<seg id="707"> Moreover, the world is not one in which people are neutral to risk.</seg>
<seg id="708"> Nonetheless the procedure is harmless enough, and those who use this version share our goal of understanding the determinants of state prices.</seg>
<seg id="709"> The Law of One Price in an Incomplete Market</seg>
<seg id="710"> In a complete market with no arbitrage opportunities the LOP must hold and the price of any security (Pi) can be determined by ¡°pricing¡± its payoffs (Xis values) using observable state prices (ps values).</seg>
<seg id="711"> But what about an incomplete market, when only the prices of traded securities can be observed? Can one construct a set of state claim prices that will correctly price all traded securities in the sense that the LOP equation will hold for each one?</seg>
<seg id="712"> As shown in Rubinstein (1976) and Ross (1977), the answer is generally yes, assuming that no arbitrage opportunities exist.</seg>
<seg id="713"> To provide a simple illustration we return to Case 1 in which Mario and Hue could only trade shares of the two fishing companies and a riskless bond.</seg>
<seg id="714"> Fig-ure 4-21 shows key information from the case.</seg>
<seg id="715"> The market state prices provided by the simulator are shown in the top left table, the security payoffs in the top right table, and the actual prices from the equilibrium in the first row of the bottom right table.</seg>
<seg id="716"> Each entry in the bottom row of the latter table is computed by multiplying the state prices by the payoffs for the security in question.</seg>
<seg id="717"> In this case, each such implied price is equal to the actual price.</seg>
<seg id="718"> Thus the state prices in Figure 4-21 do in fact price each of the available securities.</seg>
<seg id="719"> We will see shortly why this is the case.</seg>
<seg id="720"> First, however, it is important to understand that other sets of state prices may also suffice.</seg>
<seg id="721"> Any set of state prices that produces implied security prices equal to the actual prices will satisfy the LOP.</seg>
<seg id="722"> To show that other such prices exist we perform two optimization analyses, each of which focuses (arbitrarily) on the standard deviation of the prices for the future states, shown below the set of state prices.</seg>
<seg id="723"> Figure 4-22 shows the results for the first optimization, in which the goal was to find a set of state prices that (1) produce an implied price for each security equal to its actual price and (2) provide the smallest possible standard deviation of future state prices.</seg>
<seg id="724"> As can be seen, these state prices also price the securities, but they differ significantly from those in Figure 4-21.</seg>

<seg id="726"> Here, too, the state prices conform to the LOP. But they differ very significantly from those in the previous examples.</seg>
<seg id="727"> By construction, any set of state prices that makes each of the differences between actual and implied security prices equal to zero will price all existing securities correctly.</seg>
<seg id="728"> Absent arbitrage, such a set of state prices will also price any security with payoffs that can be replicated using some combination of existing securities.</seg>
<seg id="729"> Any of the three sets of prices shown in Figures 4-21, 4-22, and 4-23 could do the job and other combinations of state prices could work as well.</seg>
<seg id="730"> These examples show both the good news and the bad news about the LOP in an incomplete market.</seg>
<seg id="731"> The good news is that one can find a set of state prices that will correctly price any security that can be replicated with existing securities.</seg>
<seg id="732"> The bad news is that in most cases a set of such state prices will not be unique.</seg>
<seg id="733"> Additional bad news is that the state prices chosen may not provide any useful information about the price for which one could buy or sell a ¡°new¡± security¡ªthat is, one with payments that cannot be replicated using currently available securities.But all is not lost.</seg>
<seg id="734"> Figure 4-21 showed that the market state prices computed in the simulation analysis for Case 1 satisfied the LOP.</seg>
<seg id="735"> And those prices do contain information about the prices at which state claims could be traded, at least initially, if such trades were possible.</seg>
<seg id="736"> The state prices in Figure 4-21 are thus of more economic relevance than those in Figures 4-22 and 4-23 which were obtained by minimizing or maximizing an arbitrary function of the state prices.</seg>
<seg id="737"> It is not difficult to see why this is the case.</seg>
<seg id="738"> In Case 1, and for that matter, all the cases that we have analyzed thus far, no investor is subject to a constraint concerning the purchase or sale of any security or state claim when equilibrium is obtained.</seg>
<seg id="739"> Thus Mario¡¯s reservation prices for the state claims will ¡°price out¡± each of the available securities, as will Hue¡¯s. We compute the market price for each state claim by averaging (1) the average of the reservation prices for all investors who would be able to purchase the claim and (2) the average of the reservation prices for all investors who would be able to sell the claim.</seg>
<seg id="740"> As long as all investors are able to purchase or sell all claims, the set of market state claim prices will thus be an average of all investors¡¯ reservation prices.</seg>
<seg id="741"> But if each investor¡¯s reservation prices conform to the LOP, so will an average of all investors¡¯ reservation prices.</seg>
<seg id="742"> In an incomplete market, any unconstrained investor¡¯s state reservation prices will price any security that is currently available or that can be replicated using available securities.</seg>
<seg id="743"> Averaging the reservation prices of unconstrained investors will also give a set of state prices that will price any such security.</seg>
<seg id="744"> If one is interested only in the prices of securities that exist or that can be replicated by combining currently traded securities, there is no need to even compute a set of state prices that conforms to the LOP.</seg>
<seg id="745"> If, on the other hand, one would like to estimate the prices at which truly new securities might trade, it is desirable to utilize a set of state prices that not only prices currently available securities but also contains information about investors¡¯ reservation prices.</seg>
<seg id="746"> Ultimately, the only way to determine the price at which a new security will trade is to open a market in which it can be traded.</seg>
<seg id="747"> But the ¡°market state prices¡± that we compute provide useful estimates of such prices.</seg>
<seg id="748"> Sufficiently Complete Markets</seg>
<seg id="749"> Thus far we have described two broad types of markets.</seg>
<seg id="750"> In complete markets investors can trade both existing securities and state claims.</seg>
<seg id="751"> Formally, many of the concepts of asset pricing theory are based on the existence of a complete market in this sense.</seg>
<seg id="752"> But in actual markets few state claims are traded explicitly.</seg>
<seg id="753"> In actuality, incomplete markets are the rule, not the exception.</seg>
<seg id="754"> Case 6 provided a good example of an incomplete market.</seg>
<seg id="755"> Quade and Dagmar were prime candidates for new securities that could enable them to achieve additional gains through trade.</seg>
<seg id="756"> This was evident in Figure 4-8, which showed significant disparities in their reservation prices for several state claims.</seg>
<seg id="757"> In Case 7, when trading in such claims was made available, substantial amounts were traded.</seg>
<seg id="758"> This need not be the case in every incomplete market.</seg>
<seg id="759"> Case 1 provided an example of a different situation.</seg>
<seg id="760"> Figure 4-24 shows Mario and Hue¡¯s reservation state prices and the market prices computed by averaging their reservation prices from the equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="761"> While no trading in state claims was allowed in Case 1 and the existing securities did not allow replication of the payments for any such claim, this situation was not fertile ground for an ambitious investment banker.</seg>
<seg id="762"> Indeed, if a market maker were to open markets in all the state claims at the prices shown in the top row in Figure 4-24, little if anything would change.</seg>
<seg id="763"> While the market was incomplete, the equilibrium was consistent with a complete market with state prices similar to those shown in the simulation output.</seg>
<seg id="764"> When an equilibrium is reached with such characteristics we will say that the market is sufficiently complete. Either of two definitions can be used for this purpose: (1) for each state claim investors¡¯ reservation prices are the same or (2) if markets were opened for trading state claims, no trades would be made.</seg>
<seg id="765"> More broadly, in a sufficiently complete market, investors can accomplish their goals with existing securities.</seg>
<seg id="766"> In the real world and in our simulations, incomplete markets are unlikely to strictly conform to this definition.</seg>
<seg id="767"> Actual capital markets have transactions costs and our simulations stop when a desired level of precision is reached.</seg>
<seg id="768"> But many markets, real and simulated, can be almost sufficiently complete and thus conform closely to the results of asset pricing theories that assume markets are in fact complete.</seg>

<seg id="856"> The Capital Asset Pricing Model</seg>
<seg id="857"> The curve in the final graph in Figure 4-26 appears to be almost linear but does have some curvature.</seg>
<seg id="858"> In general, investors with sufficient investment choices may adopt strategies that do not plot as straight lines in a return graph. This was clearly so in Case 7, as shown in Figure 4-10.</seg>
<seg id="859"> But there are conditions in which every investor will choose a linear market-based strategy¡ªa portfolio that provides returns linearly related to the returns on the market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="860"> Assume that every investor has quadratic utility.</seg>
<seg id="861"> As shown in Chapter 3, an investor with such a utility function will care only about the mean and variance of portfolio return.</seg>
<seg id="862"> Moreover, his or her marginal utility will be a linear function of consumption at least over a wide range of outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="863"> Figure 4-27 shows the same set of graphs as Figure 4-26 with a key exception.</seg>
<seg id="864"> Each of the two investors has quadratic utility that applies over the range of consumption chosen in equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="865"> As a result, each investor¡¯s consumption is a linear function of PPC.</seg>
<seg id="866"> But then, so is aggregate consumption.</seg>
<seg id="867"> And, since all the graphs in the top row are linear, so are all the rest.</seg>
<seg id="868"> This is the world of the original version of the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) of Sharpe (1964), Lintner (1965), Mossin (1966), and Treynor (1999). <seg id="869"> The implications are dramatic.</seg>
<seg id="870"> Every investor will choose a linear market-based strategy. This in turn implies that every investor will choose a combination of the market portfolio and the riskless asset.</seg>
<seg id="871"> The reason is not hard to see.</seg>
<seg id="872"> The market portfolio plots as a 45 degree line in a return graph and the riskless portfolio plots as a horizontal line.</seg>
<seg id="873"> Any combination of the two will thus plot as a straight line passing through their intersection.</seg>
<seg id="874"> But in equilibrium there cannot be any strategy that plots as any other straight line. Why?</seg>
<seg id="875"> Imagine that such a strategy existed. Consider a combination of the riskless security and the market portfolio that plots as a parallel straight line.</seg>
<seg id="876"> Each strategy has the same cost.</seg>
<seg id="877"> But one gives a higher return in every possible future state of the world.</seg>
<seg id="878"> Clearly one could sell short the inferior strategy and use the proceeds to buy the superior one. This would have zero cost but provide a net payment in every possible state.</seg>
<seg id="879"> One would thus obtain a riskless arbitrage and get something for nothing, a possibility that is highly unlikely in a modern capital market.</seg>
<seg id="880"> The analysis in Figure 4-27 assumed that a complete market is available.</seg>
<seg id="881"> But in a world of mean/variance investors such a market is not needed.</seg>
<seg id="882"> At the end of the day each investor chooses to hold a combination of the market portfolio and the riskless asset.</seg>
<seg id="883"> As long as borrowing and lending are available, only standard securities are required.</seg>
<seg id="884"> A market populated only by investors with quadratic utility will be sufficiently complete.</seg>
<seg id="885"> Another important implication of a market populated solely with mean/variance investors concerns the pricing kernel.</seg>

<seg id="923"> Sharpe Ratios</seg>
<seg id="924"> If investors care only about the mean and variance of portfolio return and it is possible to borrow or lend at the riskless rate of interest, the desirability of a portfolio can be assessed by computing its Sharpe Ratio: the ratio obtained by dividing (1) the portfolio¡¯s expected excess return over the riskless rate of interest by (2) the standard deviation of its excess return.</seg>
<seg id="925"> In the world of the CAPM the market portfolio will have the highest possible Sharpe Ratio.</seg>
<seg id="926"> The reason is relatively straightforward.</seg>
<seg id="927"> If an investor can borrow or lend as desired, any portfolio can be levered up or down.</seg>
<seg id="928"> A combination with a proportion kinvested in a risky portfolio and 1 ¨C k in the riskless asset will have an expected excess return of k and a standard deviation equal to k times the standard deviation of the risky portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="929"> Importantly, the Sharpe Ratio of the combination will be the same as that of the risky portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="930"> Now, imagine an investor choosing between two alternatives: (1) to invest in portfolio A plus borrowing or lending, as desired or (2) to invest in portfolio B plus borrowing or lending, as desired.</seg>
<seg id="931"> If portfolio A has a higher Sharpe Ratio than B, then for any desired standard deviation of return, a combination of A plus borrowing or lending can provide a higher expected return than a combination of B plus borrowing or lending.</seg>
<seg id="932"> If the investor cares only about expected return and standard deviation of return, portfolio A will be preferred to B, with borrowing or lending used to obtain the optimal amount of risk.</seg>
<seg id="933"> In the world of the CAPM every investor cares only about expected return and standard deviation of return.</seg>
<seg id="934"> As long as it is possible to borrow or lend as desired at the riskless rate of interest, in equilibrium no portfolio will have a higher Sharpe Ratio than the market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="935"> Why?</seg>
<seg id="936"> Because if this were the case, every investor would choose it instead of the market portfolio and the market would not clear.</seg>
<seg id="937"> In the world of the CAPM every investor will also hold a portfolio with the market Sharpe Ratio. Thus each investor will adopt a linear market-based strategy, with a return graph that plots as a straight line.</seg>
<seg id="938"> In more complex cases none of these results may hold exactly, although deviations may be relatively small.</seg>

<seg id="940"> Quade has chosen a portfolio with a higher Sharpe Ratio than that of the market as a whole, while Dagmar has chosen one with a lower Sharpe Ratio than that of the market.</seg>

<seg id="943"> The slope of a line drawn from the riskless asset point to the point representing a portfolio or security equals its Sharpe Ratio.</seg>
<seg id="944"> If the CAPM holds, every portfolio or security will plot on or below the CML and the portfolios that investors choose will plot on it.</seg>
<seg id="945"> In this case, the securities plot below the CML but at least one possible portfolio (Quade¡¯s) plots above it and at least one investor (Dagmar) chooses a portfolio that plots below it.</seg>
<seg id="946"> Why is Dagmar content with a higher standard deviation than she could obtain from a combination of the market portfolio and borrowing or lending with the same expected return? The reason is that her strategy has another desirable property.</seg>

<seg id="948"> While the Sharpe Ratios in Case 7 are not completely consistent with the properties of the CML of the CAPM, the differences are quite small.</seg>

<seg id="950"> Case 8: The Representative Investor</seg>
<seg id="951"> As we have seen, in a complete market with agreement, both state prices and state probabilities are known by all.</seg>
<seg id="952"> Thus the pricing kernel is observable and if it can be well approximated by a function of total market return, the general form of the MRRT will hold, with market risk measured by the beta of an asset with respect to the function of the market determined by the pricing kernel.</seg>
<seg id="953"> Now, consider a market with a single investor holding the entire market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="954"> Such an investor will not enter into any trades, hence security prices will be determined solely by his or her reservation prices for state claims.</seg>
<seg id="955"> As a result, each PPC value will equal the investor¡¯s reservation price for the state in question divided by its probability.</seg>
<seg id="956"> If the marginal utility function is the same for all states at a given time we can use a single discount factor (d) for all the states at time 2.</seg>
<seg id="957"> Moreover, as we have seen, the choices an investor makes are unaffected if all his or her marginal utilities are multiplied by a constant.</seg>
<seg id="958"> For convenience, we can thus assume that m(X1) equals 1. After these changes, for all states at time 2 we have:</seg>
<seg id="959"> This solves the mystery concerning the notation used for the pricing kernel in the financial economics literature.</seg>
<seg id="960"> Each value of the pricing kernel (PPCj) can be considered equal to a constant (d) times the marginal utility m(Xj) of the representative investor for the associated total amount of consumption Xj.</seg>
<seg id="961"> Given a set of PPC values, we can compute a representative investor¡¯s marginal utilities for the amounts of aggregate consumption in each state using the formula above.</seg>
<seg id="962"> But this does not provide any new information concerning possible prices for other amounts of aggregate consumption.</seg>
<seg id="963"> Further, as we have seen, in an incomplete market more than one set of state prices may be consistent with observed security prices.</seg>
<seg id="964"> For these reasons, financial economists have explored the efficacy of approximating observed data by assuming that prices are consistent with those in a market with a representative investor having a particular form of marginal utility function.</seg>
<seg id="965"> The simulation program provides an example of this type of calibration exercise for the case in which the representative investor is required to have constant relative risk aversion.</seg>
<seg id="966"> But this is the equation that the simulator fits to the data in order to derive the coefficient (b) for the power beta calculations.</seg>
<seg id="967"> Standard least-squares regression is used, with each future state constituting an observation, the ln(Xj) values serving as observations for the independent variable and the ln(PPCj) values as observations for the dependent variable.</seg>
<seg id="968"> The resulting slope coefficient is b and the intercept is ln(d). The former serves as the representative investor¡¯s risk aversion and the latter is converted to obtain his or her discount factor.</seg>
<seg id="969"> The extent to which the equation fits the data is given by the R2 value for the regression.</seg>

<seg id="979"> More generally, it is tempting to want to compare an investor¡¯s characteristics with those of a representative investor, tilting the investor¡¯s portfolio away from the market portfolio based on the differences between his or her preferences, predictions, and positions and those of the representative investor.</seg>
<seg id="980"> Unfortunately this is not always easily done.</seg>
<seg id="981"> First, it should be remembered that in markets that are not sufficiently complete it may be possible to create alternative representative investors, depending on the state prices utilized.</seg>
<seg id="982"> Worse yet, with disagreement on probabilities the range of alternatives is likely to be even wider.</seg>
<seg id="983"> Differences in investor positions add to the complications.</seg>
<seg id="984"> Finally, no standard functional form for the representative investor¡¯s preferences may be able to replicate the equilibrium security prices with a great deal of accuracy.</seg>
<seg id="985"> The notion that a single investor with a relatively simple set of preferences can represent a complex market can provide great comfort to those who build models of financial markets.</seg>
<seg id="986"> But here, as in most economic applications, it is important to exercise caution before making too many simplifying assumptions.</seg>
<seg id="987"> Ex Ante and Ex Post Relationships</seg>
<seg id="988"> This book focuses on the relationships among economic variables before the fact and the implications of those relationships for investors¡¯ portfolio choices.</seg>
<seg id="989"> More elegantly, we can say that our analyses deal primarily with ex ante values.</seg>
<seg id="990"> But it is difficult to measure such values.</seg>
<seg id="991"> In recent years, financial economists have begun to use survey results and experiments in which human subjects play the roles of financial market participants in order to obtain measures of individual¡¯s predictions about the future.</seg>
<seg id="992"> Nonetheless, the majority of empirical research in this area relies on the analysis of actual outcomes over many periods.</seg>
<seg id="993"> In effect, historic frequencies of experienced outcomes are used as proxies for predicted probabilities of possible alternative future outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="994"> The data thus measure actual outcomes in past periods (ex post outcomes) rather than predictions of what might happen in the next period (ex ante forecasts).</seg>
<seg id="995"> This would be fine if (1) the ex ante equilibrium had been the same in each past period and (2) the alternative possible outcomes occurred with frequencies equal to their ex ante probabilities.</seg>
<seg id="996"> Unfortunately, in the vast majority of cases neither of these two conditions is likely to have been met.</seg>
<seg id="997"> Thus one should view many ¡°tests¡± of proposed models of equilibrium in financial markets with considerable skepticism.</seg>
<seg id="998"> We illustrate the nature of the problem using the equilibrium results from Case 7.</seg>
<seg id="999"> In the SML graph in Figure 4-28, the six securities plot very close to the line representing the combinations of expected return and beta that can be obtained with combinations of the market portfolio and borrowing or lending.</seg>
<seg id="1000"> The differences are small, with the largest alpha value equal to .003 (0.3 percent/year) and the smallest ¨C.002 (¨C0.2 percent/year).</seg>
<seg id="1001"> The SML equation is thus a good approximation for the ex ante relationship between the securities¡¯ expected returns and their beta values.</seg>
<seg id="1002"> Now imagine that this equilibrium relationship has held for 25 years.</seg>
<seg id="1003"> To simulate a possible historical record for this length of time we use a Monte Carlo procedure using a random number generator.</seg>
<seg id="1004"> In effect, an urn is filled with balls on each of which is printed a state name.</seg>
<seg id="1005"> Five percent of the balls are labeled ¡°Depression1,¡± another 5 percent are labeled ¡°Depression2,¡± and so on.</seg>

<seg id="1021"> Summary</seg>
<seg id="1022"> This chapter has covered a great deal of ground, focusing on cases involving agreement among investors on the probabilities of future outcomes, the absence of outside sources of consumption, and the lack of state-dependent preferences among outcomes at the same time period.</seg>
<seg id="1023"> We have examined conditions in which any type of claim on future consumption can be traded or equivalent results can be obtained using available securities¡ªconditions that will be relaxed in subsequent chapters.</seg>
<seg id="1024"> Nonetheless, the examples in this chapter provide much of the received wisdom of current academic investment theory and advice.</seg>
<seg id="1025"> At the risk of overkill, we summarize by repeating the results.</seg>
<seg id="1026"> In this setting, expected returns on securities or portfolios are solely a function of the relationships between their returns and the returns on the market portfolio as a whole.</seg>
<seg id="1027"> More specifically, the expected return of a security or portfolio is related to its covariance with a function of the return on the market portfolio.</seg>
<seg id="1028"> Thus the MRRT holds:</seg>
<seg id="1029"> Only market risk is rewarded with higher expected return, with market risk measured using the function of the market portfolio¡¯s return determined by the pricing kernel.</seg>
<seg id="1030"> Given this, sensible investors should take only market risk.</seg>
<seg id="1031"> And in this setting, they all do, primarily because they all agree on the probabilities of future events.</seg>
<seg id="1032"> These results follow from the plausible assumption that the less consumption an investor has, the more he or she values additional consumption.</seg>
<seg id="1033"> Equilibrium asset prices reflect this.</seg>
<seg id="1034"> The cost of a chance to get consumption in a bad (low aggregate consumption) state will be greater than that of a chance to get consumption in a good (high aggregate consumption) state.</seg>
<seg id="1035"> From this follows the existence of a risk premium for the market portfolio vis-¨¤-vis the riskless investment, the desirability of more and less aggressive market-based strategies for investors with, respectively, greater and smaller risk tolerance, and many other aspects of standard investment advice.</seg>
<seg id="1036"> These are comfortable worlds for the theorist, and lead to advice favored by many financial economists.</seg>
<seg id="1037"> But they predict that everyone will act in accordance with that advice, which is patently untrue.</seg>
<seg id="1038"> Many investors take non-market risk.</seg>
<seg id="1039"> The points in their portfolio return graphs do not fall neatly on an upward-sloping curve.</seg>
<seg id="1040"> Why do investors do this?</seg>
<seg id="1041"> And what sort of advice should one offer investors in a world in which such behavior takes place?</seg>
<seg id="1042"> To approach these questions we have to leave the comfortable setting of this chapter. Such is the task of Chapters 5 and 6.</seg>
