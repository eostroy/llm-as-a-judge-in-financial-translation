<seg id="1"> Economists have a very well-established theory of market trading and are on the way to possessing a similarly well-developed theory of contractual transactions.</seg>
<seg id="2"> The economic analysis of institutions, however, is in a much more rudimentary state.</seg>
<seg id="3"> This book provides a framework for thinking about firms and other kinds of economic institutions. The basic idea is that firms arise in situations where people cannot write good contracts and where the allocation of power or control is therefore important.</seg>
<seg id="4"> The book is divided into two parts.</seg>
<seg id="5"> Part I is concerned with the boundaries of firms, Part II with the financial structure of firms.</seg>
<seg id="6"> In this Introduction I want to sketch out some of the main themes.</seg>
<seg id="7"> It is useful to start with a true story.</seg>
<seg id="8"> Recently my wife and I entered into negotiations to buy a piece of land from a contractor, on which the contractor would build us a house.</seg>
<seg id="9"> Our first impression of the contractor was favourable; we hope that he felt the same way about us.</seg>
<seg id="10"> None the less, it was clear as we talked about how to structure the deal that each of us had legitimate concerns.</seg>
<seg id="11"> To mention a few of them, my wife and I were worried that we might spend a lot of money and end up with a house we did not like, or that the house might not be finished on time, or that the contractor might quit in the middle of the project and we would have to hire someone else to complete the house.</seg>
<seg id="12"> On the other hand, the contractor was worried that he would build a house designed according to our wishes and we would then not pay for it, or that we would dismiss the contractor in the middle of the project and hire someone else, or that we would choose very expensive kitchen and bathroom fixtures which would raise his costs substantially.</seg>
<seg id="13"> In an ideal world, there would be a simple way to deal with many of these concerns.</seg>
<seg id="14"> The three of us would write a binding contract that laid down each person's obligations in every conceivable eventuality and imposed large penalties if anybody failed to live up to them.1 For example, the contract would provide a description of the house down to the bathroom taps and light fixtures.</seg>
<seg id="15"> It would specify how the price of the house would be altered if my wife and I made any changesˇŞthere would be one price for an extra bathroom, another for an extra fitted wardrobe, etc. There would be provisions for how the price of the house would change if the cost of raw materials rose during construction.</seg>
<seg id="16"> A completion date would be specified, but extra days would be allowed if there were an unusually severe winter or if the foreman fell ill.</seg>
<seg id="17"> And so on.</seg>
<seg id="18"> Unfortunately, it is impossible to write a contract as detailed as this; it is simply too difficult to anticipate all the many things that may happen.</seg>
<seg id="19"> The contract we will write (at this point we have not yet agreed on a final contract!) will specify many aspects of the house, but many contingencies will be left out.2 As the contractor admitted rather disarmingly to us, he is learning how to write contracts as he goes along.</seg>
<seg id="20"> The contract he wrote with his last client had many missing provisions; he hopes this one will have fewer.</seg>
<seg id="21"> Given that we will write an incomplete contract, it is clear that revisions and renegotiations will take place.</seg>
<seg id="22"> In fact, the contract is best seen as providing a suitable backdrop or starting point for such renegotiations rather than specifying the final outcome.</seg>
<seg id="23"> Thus, in thinking about the contract, my wife and IˇŞand I believe the contractor tooˇŞhave found it useful to imagine worst-case scenarios.</seg>
<seg id="24"> We are all looking for a contract that will ensure that, whatever happens, each side has some protection, both against opportunistic behaviour by the other party and against bad luck.</seg>
<seg id="25"> For example, here are two contracts that I do not think the three of us will sign:</seg>
<seg id="26"> Contract 1: My wife and I pay for the house and the land in advance.</seg>
<seg id="27"> The contractor is then obliged to complete the house.</seg>
<seg id="28"> He receives no further payment.</seg>
<seg id="29"> Contract 2: We pay nothing in advance.</seg>
<seg id="30"> However, we become the owner of the land immediately.</seg>
<seg id="31"> We pay for the house and the land when the house is completed.</seg>
<seg id="32"> It is pretty clear why these contracts are unattractive.</seg>
<seg id="33"> With the first one, my wife and I would be concerned that the contractor would disappear after we had paid him the money for the house and land, and we would be left holding only the landˇŞor that, instead of literally disappearing, the contractor might work extremely slowly.</seg>
<seg id="34"> With the second one, the contractor would be concerned that we would exclude him from the property that we had just become the owners of, and hire someone else to build the house. (In justification, we might claim that he was doing bad work.)</seg>
<seg id="35"> Instead of these ˇ®extremeˇŻ contracts, it seems clear that we will end up writing something closer to the following contract (which is similar to contracts the contractor has used before):</seg>
<seg id="36"> Contract 3: We pay some amount initially which corresponds roughly to the value of the land.</seg>
<seg id="37"> We then become the owners of the land.</seg>
<seg id="38"> After this we make a series of specified advances to the builder, which are timed to occur when certain parts of the house are finished. (For example, the contractor receives 20 per cent of the price of the house when the foundation is completed, 10 per cent when the chimney is built, and so on.) A final payment is made some time after the house is completed.</seg>
<seg id="39"> The advantage of this kind of contract is that each side has some protection against worst-case scenarios; or, to put it another way, neither side can take too much advantage of the other.</seg>
<seg id="40"> If the contractor disappears at any point, we could complete the project without incurring too much additional cost since we have paid an amount roughly commensurate with what we have received from him.</seg>
<seg id="41"> On the other hand, if we sack the contractor at some point, he will not suffer too much since he has been paid for the work he has done.</seg>
<seg id="42"> The house story is of course just one example of an economic relationship, but it has two features that I believe are very general.</seg>
<seg id="43"> The first is that contracts are incomplete.</seg>
<seg id="44"> The second is that, because of this, the ex post allocation of power (or control) matters.</seg>
<seg id="45"> Here power refers roughly to the position of each party if the other party does not perform (e.g. if the other party behaves opportunistically).</seg>
<seg id="46"> It was observed in the house example that contract succeeded in sharing power reasonably between the contractor and my wife and me. In contrast, contract 1 gave the contractor too much power and contract 2 gave my wife and me too much power.</seg>
<seg id="47"> In this book, I will argue that these two ideasˇŞcontractual incompleteness and powerˇŞcan be used to understand a number of economic institutions and arrangements.</seg>
<seg id="48"> Before I develop this theme a little further, it is worth pointing out that power is not a standard feature of economic theory.</seg>
<seg id="49"> For example, take the frameworks that economists use to analyse the behaviour of economic agents: general equilibrium theory, game theory, mechanism design or principal-agent theory, and transaction cost theory.</seg>
<seg id="50"> In general equilibrium theory, it is supposed that trade takes place through anonymous competitive markets and that every agent abides by the terms of any transaction he or she enters into.</seg>
<seg id="51"> In such a setting, power is irrelevant.</seg>
<seg id="52"> In game theory, agents may have market power, i.e. the ability to affect price.</seg>
<seg id="53"> However, market power is not the same as the notion of power used in this book.</seg>
<seg id="54"> Market power captures the idea that the contractor can charge my wife and me a lot for our house because there are not many competing contractors around; it says nothing about how we allocate power within our relationship.</seg>
<seg id="55"> In mechanism design or principal¨Cagent theory, it is supposed that it is costless to write a contract.</seg>
<seg id="56"> An implication is that an optimal contract will be ˇ®comprehensiveˇŻ, in the sense that, like the idealized house contract, it will lay down each person's obligations in every conceivable eventuality and impose large penalties if anybody fails to live up to them.</seg>
<seg id="57"> But here again power is irrelevant, since an optimal comprehensive contract will not be breached or renegotiated.</seg>
<seg id="58"> Transaction cost theory comes closest to the framework presented here.</seg>
<seg id="59"> However, although transaction cost theory puts a lot of emphasis on the costs of writing contracts, and the consequent contractual incompleteness, less attention is paid to the idea that power is important or that institutional arrangements are designed to allocate power among agents.</seg>
<seg id="60"> In the remainder of this introduction, I will try to impart a flavour of how contractual incompleteness and power can be used to understand some important economic phenomena.</seg>
<seg id="61"> I will touch on the contents of each chapter except for Chapter 1, which surveys the literature, and Chapter 4, which discusses the foundations of the theory of incomplete contracts.</seg>
<seg id="62"> The Meaning of Ownership</seg>
<seg id="63"> Economists have written a great deal about why property rights are important, and in particular why it matters whether a machine, say, is privately owned or is common property.</seg>
<seg id="64"> However, they have been less successful in explaining why it matters who owns a piece of private property.</seg>
<seg id="65"> To understand the difficulty, consider a situation where I want to use a machine initially owned by you.</seg>
<seg id="66"> One possibility is for me to buy the machine from you; another possibility is for me to rent the machine from you.</seg>
<seg id="67"> If contracting costs are zero, we can sign a rental agreement that is as effective as a change in ownership.</seg>
<seg id="68"> In particular, the rental contract can specify exactly what I can do with the machine, when I can have access to it, what happens if the machine breaks down, what rights you have to use the machine, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="69"> Given this, however, it is unclear why changes in asset ownership ever need to take place.</seg>
<seg id="70"> In a world of contracting costs, however, renting and owning are no longer the same.</seg>
<seg id="71"> If contracts are incomplete, not all the uses of the machine will be specified in all possible eventualities.</seg>
<seg id="72"> The question then arises: who chooses the unspecified uses? A reasonable view is that the owner of the machine has this right; that is, the owner has residual rights of control over the machine, or residual powers.</seg>
<seg id="73"> For example, if the machine breaks down or requires modification and the contract is silent about this, the owner can decide how and when it is to be repaired or modified.</seg>
<seg id="74"> It is now possible to understand why it might make sense for me to buy the machine from you rather than to rent it.</seg>
<seg id="75"> If I own the machine, I will have more power in our economic relationship since I will have all the residual rights of control.</seg>
<seg id="76"> To put it another way, if the machine breaks down or needs to be modified, I can ensure that it is repaired or modified quickly, so that I can continue to use it productively.</seg>
<seg id="77"> Knowing this, I will have a greater incentive to look after the machine, to learn to operate it, to acquire other machines that create a synergy with this machine, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="78"> Chapters 2 and 3 develop a formal theory of asset ownership based on these ideas.</seg>
<seg id="79"> The Boundaries of Firms</seg>
<seg id="80"> A long-standing issue in organization theory concerns the determinants of the boundaries of firms.</seg>
<seg id="81"> Why does it matter if a particular transaction is carried out inside a firm or through the market or via a long-term contract? To put it another way, given any two firms A and B, what difference does it make if the firms transact through an arms-length contract or merge and become a single firm?</seg>
<seg id="82"> It has proved to be difficult to answer these questions using standard theory for the same reason that it is hard to explain why asset ownership matters.</seg>
<seg id="83"> If contracting costs are zero, firms A and B can write a contract governing their relationship that specifies the obligations of all parties in all eventualities.</seg>
<seg id="84"> Since the contract is all-inclusive, it is unclear what further aspects of their relationship could be controlled through a merger.</seg>
<seg id="85"> This is true whether firms A and B are in a vertical relationshipˇŞfirm A is buying an input from firm BˇŞor in a horizontal or lateral relationship, e.g. where firms A and B sell complementary products and want to save on some duplicative production costs.</seg>
<seg id="86"> Once one recognizes that contracts are incomplete, however, it is possible to explain why a merger might be desirable.</seg>
<seg id="87"> Consider the well-known example of Fisher Body, which for many years has supplied car bodies to General Motors.</seg>
<seg id="88"> For a long time Fisher Body and GM were separate firms linked by a long-term contract.</seg>
<seg id="89"> However, in the 1920s GM's demand for car bodies increased substantially.</seg>
<seg id="90"> After Fisher Body refused to revise the formula for determining price, GM bought Fisher out.</seg>
<seg id="91"> Why did GM and Fisher Body not simply write a better contract?</seg>
<seg id="92"> Arguably, GM recognized that, however good a contract it wrote with Fisher Body, situations similar to the one it had just experienced might arise again; that is, contingencies might occur that no contract could allow for.</seg>
<seg id="93"> GM wanted to be sure that next time around it would be in a stronger bargaining position; in particular, it would be able to insist on extra supplies, without having to pay a great deal for them.</seg>
<seg id="94"> It is reasonable to suppose that ownership of Fisher Body would provide GM with this extra power by giving it residual control rights over Fisher Body's assets.</seg>
<seg id="95"> At an extreme, GM could dismiss the managers of Fisher Body if they refused to accede to GM's requests.7</seg>
<seg id="96"> Of course, although the acquisition increased GM's power and made GM more secure in its relationship with Fisher Body, it arguably had the opposite effect on Fisher Body.</seg>
<seg id="97"> That is, Fisher Body may have had more to worry about since the merger.</seg>
<seg id="98"> For example, if Fisher Body's costs fall, GM is now in a stronger position to force a reduction in the (transfer) price of car bodies, hence reducing the return to Fisher managers.</seg>
<seg id="99"> Anticipating this, Fisher managers may have less incentive to figure out how to reduce costs.</seg>
<seg id="100"> Thus, there are both costs and benefits from a merger.8</seg>
<seg id="101"> Chapters2 and 3 develop a theory of the firm based on the idea that firm boundaries are chosen to allocate power optimally among the various parties to a transaction.</seg>
<seg id="102"> I argue that power is a scarce resource that should never be wasted.</seg>
<seg id="103"> One implication of the theory is that a merger between firms with highly complementary assets is value-enhancing, and a merger between firms with independent assets is value-reducing.</seg>
<seg id="104"> The reason is as follows.</seg>
<seg id="105"> If two highly complementary firms have different owners, then neither owner has real power since neither can do anything without the other.</seg>
<seg id="106"> It is then better to give all the power to one of the owners through a merger.</seg>
<seg id="107"> On the other hand, if two firms with independent assets merge, then the acquiring firmˇŻs owner gains little useful power, since the acquired firm's assets do not enhance his activities, but the acquired firm's owner loses useful power, since she no longer has authority over the assets she works with.</seg>
<seg id="108"> In this case, it is better to divide the power between the owners by keeping the firms separate.</seg>
<seg id="109"> Financial Securities</seg>
<seg id="110"> Debt</seg>
<seg id="111"> Suppose you have an interesting idea for a business venture, but do not have the capital to finance it.</seg>
<seg id="112"> You go to a bank to get a loan.</seg>
<seg id="113"> In deciding whether to finance the project, the bank is very likely to consider not only the return stream from the project, but also the resale value of any assets you have or will acquire using the bank's funds; in other words, the bank will be interested in the potential collateral for the loan.</seg>
<seg id="114"> In addition, the durability of your assets and how quickly the returns come in are likely to determine the maturity structure of the loan.</seg>
<seg id="115"> The bank will be more willing to lend long-term if the loan is supported by assets such as property or machines than if it is supported by inventory, and if the returns arrive in the distant future rather than right away.</seg>
<seg id="116"> These observations fit in well with the ideas emphasized in this book.</seg>
<seg id="117"> Like the parties in the house transaction, the bank wants some protection against worst-case scenarios.</seg>
<seg id="118"> If there is very little collateral underlying the loan, then the bank will worry that you will use its money unwisely or, in an extreme case, disappear with the money altogether.</seg>
<seg id="119"> Similarly, if the collateral depreciates rapidly or the returns come in quickly, then the bank would be unhappy with a long-term loan since it would have little protection against your behaving opportunistically when the collateral was no longer worth much or after the project returns had been realized (and ˇ®consumedˇŻ).</seg>
<seg id="120"> Basically, the bank wants to ensure a rough balance between the value of the debt outstanding and the value remaining in the project, including the value of the collateral, at all times. (Similar considerations made contract 3 in the house example attractive: neither party was in significant deficit or surplus with respect to the other party at any point in the transaction.) Chapter 5 builds a model of debt finance based on these ideas, and derives results about the kinds of project that will be financed.</seg>
<seg id="121"> Equity</seg>
<seg id="122"> Investors who finance business ventures sometimes take equity in the venture rather than debt.</seg>
<seg id="123"> Equity, unlike debt, does not have a fixed set of repayments associated with it, with nonpayment triggering default.</seg>
<seg id="124"> Rather, equity-holders receive dividends if and when the firm chooses to pay them.</seg>
<seg id="125"> This puts equity-holders potentially at the mercy of those running the firm, who may choose to use the firmˇŻs profits to pay salaries or to reinvest rather than to pay out dividends.</seg>
<seg id="126"> Thus, equity-holders need some protection.</seg>
<seg id="127"> Typically, they get it in the form of votes.</seg>
<seg id="128"> If things become bad enough, equity-holders have the power to remove those running the firm (the board of directors) and replace them with someone else.</seg>
<seg id="129"> However, giving outside equity-holders voting power brings costs as well as benefits.</seg>
<seg id="130"> Equity-holders can use their power to take actions that ignore the (valid) interests of insiders.</seg>
<seg id="131"> For example, they might close down an established, family-run business or force the business to terminate long-standing employees.</seg>
<seg id="132"> The optimal allocation of power between insiders and outsiders is another of the topics covered in Chapter 5.</seg>
<seg id="133"> Dispersed Power</seg>
<seg id="134"> So far I have supposed that those with power wield it.</seg>
<seg id="135"> That is, I have assumed that an owner will exercise her residual control rights over assets; e.g. an equity-holder will use her votes to replace a bad manager.</seg>
<seg id="136"> However, if power is held by many people, then no one of them may have an incentive to be active in exercising this power.</seg>
<seg id="137"> It is then important that there exist automatic mechanisms that will achieve what those with power are unable or unwilling to do by themselves.</seg>
<seg id="138"> A leading example of dispersed power is the case of a public company with many, small shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="139"> Shareholders cannot run the company themselves on a day-to-day basis and so they delegate power to a board of directors and to managers.</seg>
<seg id="140"> This creates a free-rider problem: an individual shareholder does not have an incentive to monitor management, since the gains from improved management are enjoyed by all shareholders, whereas the costs are borne only by those who are active.</seg>
<seg id="141"> Because of this free-rider problem, the managers of a public company have a fairly free hand to pursue their own goals: these might include empire-building or the enjoyment of perquisites.</seg>
<seg id="142"> Chapters6 and 8 explore two ˇ®automaticˇŻ mechanisms that can improve the performance of management: debt (in combination with bankruptcy) and take-overs.</seg>
<seg id="143"> Debt imposes a hard budget constraint on managers.</seg>
<seg id="144"> If a company has a significant amount of debt, management is faced with a simple choice: reduce slackˇŞthat is, cut back on empire-building and perquisitesˇŞor go bankrupt.</seg>
<seg id="145"> If there is a significant chance that manager swill lose their jobs in bankruptcy, they are likely to choose the first option.</seg>
<seg id="146"> Take-overs provide a potential way to overcome collective action problems among shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="147"> If a company is badly managed, then there is an incentive for someone to acquire a large stake in the company, improve performance, and make a gain on the shares or votes purchased.</seg>
<seg id="148"> The threat of such action can persuade management to act in the interest of shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="149"> I derive some implications of these views of debt and take-overs.</seg>
<seg id="150"> Chapter 6 shows that the view of debt as a constraining mechanism can explain the types of debt a company issues (how senior the debt is, whether it can be postponed).</seg>
<seg id="151"> Chapter 8 shows that the possibility of take-overs can explain why many companies bundle votes and dividend claims togetherˇŞthat is, why they adopt a one share¨Cone vote rule.</seg>
<seg id="152"> One share¨Cone vote protects shareholder property rights in the sense that it maximizes the chance that a control contest will be won by a management team that provides high value for shareholders, rather than high private benefits for itself.</seg>
<seg id="153"> Of course, if a company takes on debt, then there is always the chance that it will go bankrupt.</seg>
<seg id="154"> If contracting costs were zero, there would be no need for a formal bankruptcy procedure because every contract would specify what should happen if some party could not meet its debt obligations.</seg>
<seg id="155"> In a world of incomplete contracts, however, there is a role for bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="156"> In Chapter 7 I argue that a bankruptcy procedure should have two main goals.</seg>
<seg id="157"> The first is that a bankrupt company's assets should be placed in their highest-value use.</seg>
<seg id="158"> The second is that bankruptcy should be accompanied by a loss of power for management, so as to ensure that management has the right incentive to avoid bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="159"> Chapter 7 suggests a procedure that meets these goals, and at the same time avoids some of the inefficiencies of existing US and UK procedures.</seg>
<seg id="160"> An Omitted Topic: Public Ownership</seg>
<seg id="161"> The book is concerned with the optimal allocation of privately owned assets.</seg>
<seg id="162"> A very important topic not considered concerns the optimal balance between public and private ownership.</seg>
<seg id="163"> Which assets should be publicly owned and which should be privately owned? This issue has always been a central one in the economic and political debate, but it has attracted new attention in the last few years as major industries have been privatized in the West and the socialist regimes in Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union have dissolved.</seg>
<seg id="164"> It is natural to analyse public choice versus private choice using the ideas of incomplete contracts and power.</seg>
<seg id="165"> If contracting costs are zero, there is no difference between the optimal regulation of a private firm on the one hand, and nationalization or public ownership on the other.</seg>
<seg id="166"> In both cases the government will write a ˇ®comprehensiveˇŻ contract with the firm or its managers that will anticipate all future contingencies.</seg>
<seg id="167"> The contract will specify the manager's compensation scheme, how the price of the firm's output should change if costs fall, how the nature of the firm's product should change if there is a technological innovation or a shift in demand, etc.</seg>
<seg id="168"> In contrast, in a world of incomplete contracts, public and private ownership are different, since in one case the government has residual control rights over the firm's assets, while in the other case a private owner does. The public¨Cprivate case is not a simple extension of the pure private property rights model, however.</seg>
<seg id="169"> At least two new questions arise.</seg>
<seg id="170"> First, what is the government's objective function? Much existing work views the government as a monolith, but this is unsatisfactory since, even more than in the case of a corporation, the government represents a collection of agents with conflicting goals: civil servants, politicians, and the citizens themselves.</seg>
<seg id="171"> Second, what ensures that the government respects an agreed-on allocation of property rights? The government, unlike a private agent, can always change its mind: it can nationalize assets it has privatized or privatize assets it has nationalized.</seg>
<seg id="172"> There is a small, but growing, literature that analyses public versus private ownership in incomplete contracting terms.</seg>
<seg id="173"> However, much remains to be done.</seg>
<seg id="174"> Developing a satisfactory theory, which deals among other things with the issue of the government's objective function and its commitment to property rights, is a challenging but fascinating task for future research.</seg>

<seg id="177">  Part II relaxesthisassumption and providesan analysisof firms' financial decisions.</seg>
<seg id="178">  Chapter 5 studies the optimal financial contract written by an entrepreneur who raises funds for an investment project from a rich investor (or a set of investors).</seg>
<seg id="179">  The issue is how control should be allocated between the entrepreneur and the investor(s).</seg>
<seg id="180">  Chapter 6 discusses the role of capital structure in constraining the behaviour of the managers of a public company that hasmany small investorswho find it difficult to exercise control.</seg>
<seg id="181">  Chapter 7 is different in character from the rest of the book.</seg>
<seg id="182">  It applies some of the ideas of Chapters 5 and 6 to study an important practical issue, the design of bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="183">  In contrast to the other chapters, there isno model and the analysisisfairly informal.</seg>
<seg id="184">  Finally, Chapter 8 considers how votes should be allocated across shares in a public company, subject to a takeover threat.</seg>
<seg id="185">  This chapter, unlike the othersin Part II, isnot concerned with debt, but shareswith Chapters6 and 7 the idea that automatic mechanismsˇŞin thiscase take-oversˇŞare an essential tool for controlling the management of a public company.</seg>
<seg id="186">  Since most of the large literature on capital structure does not (at least explicitly) take an incomplete contracting view, it isworth saying a few wordsabout why incomplete contracting ideasprovide a natural way to think about financial decisions.</seg>
<seg id="187">  In the absence of contracting costs, the parties to a transactionˇŞentrepreneurs, managers and investorsˇŞwould write an initial contract that anticipates all future events.</seg>
<seg id="188">  Given that all decisions are specified, it is difficult to find a role for financial structure.</seg>
<seg id="189">  For example, consider debt.</seg>
<seg id="190">  The simplest interpretation of a debt contract is that a sequence of fixed paymentsisagreed to; if the paymentsare not made, creditorscan foreclose on the debtor'sassetsand decide what to do with them.</seg>
<seg id="191">  In a comprehensive contracting world, however, all the uses of the debtor's assets are specified and so there is nothing left for the creditorsto decide.</seg>
<seg id="192">  Or take equity.</seg>
<seg id="193">  Equity isa claim with votesattached.</seg>
<seg id="194">  But in a comprehensive contracting world there is nothing to vote on since there are no residual decisions. (Similarly, there is no role for the take-over mechanism, which relies on the idea that someone acquires enough votes to get control.) Finally, bankruptcy is a situation in which existing claims are inconsistent.</seg>
<seg id="195">  Yet in a comprehensive contracting world such an event would be anticipated and the inconsistency removed in the contract.</seg>
<seg id="196">  Thus, there would be no need for a formal bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="197">  These themes are elaborated on in the chapters below.</seg>
<seg id="198">  5 Theories of Financial Contracting and Debt</seg>
<seg id="199">  PREVIOUS chapters have shown that an agent should own an asset if he has important asset-specific investment decisions to make or if he has human capital essential for the asset's use.</seg>
<seg id="200">  This chapter considers what happens if the agent iswealth-constrained and cannot buy the asset outright.</seg>
<seg id="201">  Under these conditions, the agent will have to raise funds from an outside investor (or from a set of investors) to purchase the asset.</seg>
<seg id="202">  This creates a new agency problem.</seg>
<seg id="203">  The agent may behave opportunistically, thus depriving the investor of an adequate return on her investment.</seg>
<seg id="204">  The chapter studies how the investor can protect herself against such opportunistic behaviour. (I now depart from the buyer-seller relationship and focus on a single agent and a single investor.) One possibility is for the investor to take a controlling interest in the asset.</seg>
<seg id="205">  However, the investor may then abuse her power; for example, she may sell the asset without taking into account the agent's benefit from operating it.</seg>
<seg id="206">  It will be shown that in some cases it is better for the investor to enter into a financial contract with the agent of the following form.</seg>
<seg id="207">  The agent borrows money from the investor and promises to make certain repayments.</seg>
<seg id="208">  If he makes them, he retains control of the asset. If he does not make them, control shifts to the investor.</seg>
<seg id="209">  In other words, the theory of incomplete contracts and property rightsˇŞwhen extended to the case of limited wealthˇŞcan explain the use of debt financing.</seg>
<seg id="210">  The chapter begins with a description of the leading incomplete contracting theory of debt, which isdue to Aghion and Bolton (1992).</seg>
<seg id="211">  I argue that the Aghion¨CBolton model explains transfersin control but does not explain the use of a standard debt contract.</seg>
<seg id="212">  Section 2 discusses a model, based on Hart and Moore (1989), which comes closer to justifying a standard debt contract. (For a related analysis, see Bolton and Scharfstein (1990).) The model in Section 2 extends for two periods and supposes perfect certainty.</seg>
<seg id="213">  It turns out to be easy to generalize the analysis to many periods, and this is done in Section 3. (The discussion in Section 3 is based on Hart and Moore (1994 a).) The dynamic version throws light on the maturity structure of debt repayment paths, and on the role of collateral in determining whether a project is financed.</seg>

<seg id="257">  Comments on the Aghion¨CBolton Model</seg>
<seg id="258">  The Aghion¨CBolton model demonstrates that, in a world where one party is wealth-constrained (but there are no relationship-specific investments), it may be optimal to transfer control from that party to another party in certain states of the world.</seg>
<seg id="259">  The Aghion¨CBolton model capturesa key aspect of debt financing: shifts in control.</seg>
<seg id="260">  However, in other respects, the model's characterization of debt is not completely convincing.</seg>
<seg id="261">  1. One of the most basic features of a debt contract is the idea that what triggers a shift in control is the nonpayment of a debt.</seg>
<seg id="262">  In other words, a debt contract has the form: ˇ®I owe you P. If I pay, I keep control of my business.</seg>
<seg id="263">  If I don't, you get control (or you can force me into bankruptcy).ˇŻ However, the Aghion¨CBolton contract does not have this property.</seg>
<seg id="264">  Shifts in control are stochastic or contingent on the realization of a verifiable state of the world, ¦Č, not on a failure to pay.</seg>
<seg id="265">  2. Related to this, a standard debt contract has the property that control shifts from the debtor to the creditor when the debtor's profits are low.</seg>
<seg id="266">  However, it has been observed that this is not a necessary implication of the Aghion¨CBolton model.</seg>
<seg id="267">  The next section discusses a model that attempts to deal with some of these problems.</seg>
<seg id="268">  Before proceeding, however, I should note that the above features may not really be weaknesses at all.</seg>
<seg id="269">  It may be a strength of the Aghion¨CBolton model that it can explain financial arrangements that are more general than standard debt contracts since more general arrangements are indeed observed in practice.</seg>
<seg id="270">  As a conclusion to this section, it is useful to consider some of the empirical evidence about the determinants of debt contracts and maturity structure.122 The evidence suggests that long-term loans are used for property, leasehold improvements, machinery, and the like.</seg>
<seg id="271">  Also, debt with the longest term is typically on property: real estate mortgages.</seg>
<seg id="272">  Short-term loans, on the other hand, tend to be used for working capital purposesˇŞe.g. payroll needs, the financing of inventory, and the smoothing of seasonal imbalances.</seg>
<seg id="273">  Moreover, the collateral is usually made up of assets such as the inventories or the accounts receivable.123</seg>
<seg id="274">  The analysis is consistent with the evidence.</seg>
<seg id="275">  The analysis has shown that, if assets are long-lived, they will support long-term debt.</seg>
<seg id="276">  Property and machinery are obvious examples of highly durable assets.</seg>
<seg id="277">  Conversely, if the assets are short-lived, as in the case of inventories (which may not retain their value, or which can be disposed of relatively easily) or accounts receivable, then the debt islikely to be short-term.</seg>
<seg id="278">  The evidence on short-term financing is also consistent with result B, i.e. that the faster the returns arrive, the shorter will be the maturity of debt (in the fastest path).</seg>
<seg id="279">  A firm that is raising money for payroll needs, for purchasing inventory, or for smoothing seasonal imbalances, is typically the kind of firm whose returns will be coming in soon.</seg>
<seg id="280">  There is also evidence suggesting that the amount of ˇ®equityˇŻ an entrepreneur has to put into the project himself, and the value of the collateralized assets, are important factors in determining whether a project is financed.</seg>
<seg id="281">  124 This finding is hardly surprising, but it fits in well with the model, in light of result (A) and the fact that w isa crucial variable in determining whether (5.16) is satisfied.</seg>
<seg id="282">  Finally, the model can explain the conventional wisdom among practitioners that ˇ®assets should be matched with liabilitiesˇŻ.</seg>
<seg id="283">  I am not suggesting that the above evidence is hard to explain or that other theories cannot explain it.</seg>
<seg id="284">  However, it is perhaps a selling point of the model that, as well as being simple and tractable, it can be used to understand the basic facts about maturity structure.</seg>

<seg id="288">  Unfortunately, the uncertainty case is not yet very well understood, and so I will provide only a brief discussion.</seg>

<seg id="294">  Multiple Investors and Hard Budget Constraints</seg>
<seg id="295">  The analysis has so far considered the case of one investor.</seg>
<seg id="296">  In reality, of course, there will often be more than one.</seg>
<seg id="297">  This might be because no investor is rich enough to finance the project alone, or because no investor wishes to bear the risk of financing the project alone (if investors are risk-averse).</seg>
<seg id="298">  There is another reason for multiple investors, however: to harden E's budget constraint.</seg>
<seg id="299">  To understand this idea, return to the two-period model of Sections 2 and 4.</seg>
<seg id="300">  In the one-investor case the entrepreneur may default even when he can pay his debts, in order to renegotiate the debt payment down to L. Unfortunately, such a strategic default can have undesirable ex ante consequences.</seg>
<seg id="301">  In particular, it puts a ceiling on the investor's return from the project and may deter the investor from financing certain projects.129</seg>
<seg id="302">  It has been pointed out in the literature that multiple investors may make strategic default less attractive (see e.g. Bolton and Scharfstein 1994).</seg>
<seg id="303">  The basic idea is that renegotiation is more likely to break down with multiple investorsˇŞbecause of free-rider and hold-out problems combined with asymmetric informationˇŞand so the entrepreneur may simply choose to pay P even when P&gt; L.</seg>
<seg id="304">  A simple way to understand this is to consider the case where there are N investors, each owed P/N. Suppose E defaults and makes the following (take-it-or-leave-it) offer to the investors: ˇ®I propose that at least M of you agree to forgive your debt down to (slightly above) L/N. Otherwise bankruptcy and liquidation will ensue.ˇŻ</seg>
<seg id="305">  Then each investor should reason as follows: ˇ®My decision to forgive my debt is very unlikely to be pivotal in determining whether the critical number M agrees to forgive.</seg>
<seg id="306">  (With a small amount of noise, the probability that any investor is pivotal can be shown to tend to zero as NˇúˇŢ if M/N is bounded away from zero and from one.) If I think that at least M investors will forgive, I am better off not forgiving since E will then have to pay me the full amount P/N. If I think the critical number M will not forgive, then there is certainly no advantage to my forgiving and there may be a disadvantage if I have a claim in bankruptcy to L/N rather than P/N. ˇŻ</seg>
<seg id="307">  Given this logic, no creditor will accept E's offer, and E's attempt to reduce the debt will fail.</seg>
<seg id="308">  Moreover, anticipating this, E will pay the full amount owed, P. 131</seg>
<seg id="309">  Unfortunately, the inflexibility exhibited by a large number of creditors detersnot only strategic default, but also productive renegotiation.</seg>
<seg id="310">  Consider example 3.</seg>
<seg id="311">  In state 2, E cannot pay his debt of 50.</seg>
<seg id="312">  In the absence of renegotiation, liquidation would occur at a social cost of y ? L=70.</seg>
<seg id="313">  With renegotiation, the debt is reduced to 30 and a more efficient outcome is achieved.</seg>
<seg id="314">  However, it may be difficult to persuade a large number of creditors to reduce the debt from 50 to 30.</seg>
<seg id="315">  The reason is similar to that given above. Each creditor will argue that her decision to forgive is unlikely to be pivotal in determining whether the renegotiation succeeds.</seg>
<seg id="316">  If the creditor expects enough other people to forgive, it is better for her not to forgive (i.e. to hold-out or free-ride) since that way she gets her full 50/ N. On the other hand, there is certainly no reason to forgive in the event that the renegotiation fails.</seg>
<seg id="317">  Since each creditor thinks the same way, a socially desirable renegotiation fails.</seg>
<seg id="318">  So there are costs and benefits from having multiple investors.</seg>
<seg id="319">  Multiple investors are good at deterring strategic default but bad at preventing productive renegotiation.</seg>
<seg id="320">  133 It may be possible to develop a theory of the optimal number of creditors along these lines.</seg>
<seg id="321">  So far I have considered the case of multiple investors with the same claim.</seg>
<seg id="322">  Another interesting line of researchˇŞdue to Dewatripont and Tirole (1994) and Bergl?f and von Thadden (1994)ˇŞlooksat multiple investors with different types of claims.</seg>
<seg id="323">  This is discussed briefly below.</seg>
<seg id="324">  Related Work</seg>
<seg id="325">  There is a considerable literature on financial contracting that is related directly or indirectly to the work described above.</seg>
<seg id="326">  Unfortunately, I have space to mention only a few contributions and themes.</seg>
<seg id="327">  The BoltonˇŞScharfstein (1990) paper hasalready been noted.</seg>
<seg id="328">  This paper develops a model that is similar in many ways to the HartˇŞMoore (1989) model; the main difference is that the penalty for nonpayment of debt is that the creditor withholds future finance rather than liquidating existing assets.135 I have also mentioned the costly state verification model of Townsend and Gale-HellwigˇŞa detailed analysis of which is provided in the Appendix.</seg>
<seg id="329">  The CSV model is based on comprehensive contracting ideas but it shares the feature of both the HartˇŞMoore and BoltonˇŞScharfstein models that a debtor pays his debts only because he will be penalized otherwise.</seg>
<seg id="330">  In the CSV model, however, the penalty isthat the debtor is inspected.</seg>
<seg id="331">  Although most of the analysis of this chapter has been concerned with debt levels rather than with debt maturity, the structure of repayment paths for the special case where returns and liquidation values are perfectly certain was also studied.</seg>
<seg id="332">  A rather different approach to debt maturity is contained in some interesting work by Diamond (1991).</seg>
<seg id="333">  Diamond analyses the trade-off between short-term and long-term debt in a two-period model which combines asymmetric information and incomplete contracts.</seg>
<seg id="334">  He argues that an entrepreneur who knows that his project is profitable will finance it with short-term debtˇŞwith the intention of refinancing later on when new information arrivesˇŞwhile an entrepreneur who knows that his project is unprofitable will use long-term debt.</seg>
<seg id="335">  The reason is that the ˇ®high-qualityˇŻ entrepreneur is prepared to bear the risk that the new information about the project's profitability will be adverse, and that the project will therefore not be refinanced, while the low-quality entrepreneur is not prepared to bear this risk.</seg>
<seg id="336">  One benefit of the Diamond approach is that it pins down the optimal contract as well as the optimal repayment path.</seg>
<seg id="337">  (In contrast in the perfect certainty model analysed in Section 3, there were many optimal contracts, most of which were renegotiated along the equilibrium path.)</seg>
<seg id="338">  However, it is not clear how easy it isto generalize the model to many periods.</seg>
<seg id="339">  The chapter briefly considered the role of multiple investors in hardening the entrepreneur's budget constraint.</seg>
<seg id="340">  The analysis focused on the case of identical creditors.</seg>
<seg id="341">  Another possibility is to have investors with different claims.</seg>
<seg id="342">  Two interesting papers that explore this theme are Dewatripont and Tirole (1994) and Bergl?f and von Thadden (1994).</seg>
<seg id="343">  Dewatripont and Tirole consider a model in which the firm's profit is verifiable but the entrepreneur has an unverifiable effort choice.</seg>
<seg id="344">  They show that it is optimal to have two outside investors holding debt and equity in different proportions. (Debt-holders and equity-holders are distinguished partly by the fact that they have different claims on verifiable profit.) The basic idea is that, if both investors hold debt and equity in the same proportions, the investors will be too soft on the entrepreneur, i.e. they won't intervene enough.</seg>
<seg id="345">  In particular, they won't liquidate the firm if first-period profits are low because the value of continuation may exceed the value of liquidation (as shareholders, they see the upside gains as well as the downside losses).</seg>
<seg id="346">  In contrast, if one of the investors holds debt and the firm defaults in the first period, this investorˇŞto whom control now shiftsˇŞwill be eager to liquidate (since she doesn't see the upside gains from continuation).</seg>
<seg id="347">  As a result, the entrepreneur will be forced to make concessions and will also have a strong incentive to avoid default.</seg>
<seg id="348">  Bergl?f and von Thadden, in independent but related research, show that similar considerations explain why a firm's short-term and long-term debt should be allocated to different investors.</seg>
<seg id="349">  In their analysis, the short-term creditor plays the role of the aggressive creditor in the Dewatripont¨CTirole model, while the long-term creditor plays the role of the passive shareholder.</seg>

<seg id="353">  Capital Structure Decisions of a Public Company</seg>
<seg id="354">  IN the last chapter, I discussed the optimal contract written by an entrepreneur who raises capital from an investor (or set of investors).</seg>
<seg id="355">  I assumed that the entrepreneur obtained significant (private) benefits from running a firm, and analysed how control rights could be allocated between the entrepreneur and investor, so as to give the entrepreneur maximum freedom to pursue his own goals, subject to the investor breaking even.</seg>
<seg id="356">  In this chapter I extend the analysis to the case of a large, public company.</seg>
<seg id="357">  Although there is no sharp economic distinction between the private and public cases, I take the point of view that in the case of a public company the issue of allocating control rights to managers in order to give them a chance to enjoy private benefits may not be of primary importance.</seg>
<seg id="358">  Also, it may not be necessary to give managers control rights in order to motivate them to undertake relationship-specific investments, or to be innovative or inventive, given that many of the actions of the managers of a large company are relatively routine.137 Rather, the crucial issue may be how the company's investors can design financial structure so as to limit management's ability to pursue its own goals at the expense of investors.</seg>
<seg id="359">  For example, managers may overpay themselves and give themselves extravagant perks; they may carry out unprofitable but power-enhancing investments; or they may refuse to give up their jobs to others who can run the company better. (In contrast, it is supposed that investors are interested only in profit or net market value.)138</seg>
<seg id="360">  In the case of a single investor, it is clear how to deal with this problem: give the investor all the control rights; i.e., make her the 100 per cent owner. (See the Aghion¨CBolton model in the case where the entrepreneur's utility does not matter, or where the investor has all the bargaining power.) However, this chapter supposes that investors are wealth-constrained and so the company has many small investors.</seg>
<seg id="361">  This creates two new issues that were not relevant in the case of the private company considered in Chapter 5.</seg>
<seg id="362">  First, those who own the company, the shareholders, are too small and numerous to exercise control on a day-to-day basis.</seg>
<seg id="363">  Given this, they delegate day-to-day (residual rights of) control to a board of directors who in turn delegate it to management.</seg>
<seg id="364">  In other words, to use the phrase made famous by Berle and Means (1932), there is a separation of ownership and control.</seg>
<seg id="365">  Second, dispersed shareholders have little or no incentive to monitor management.</seg>
<seg id="366">  The reason is that monitoring is a public good: if one shareholder's monitoring leads to improved company performance, all shareholders benefit.</seg>
<seg id="367">  Given that monitoring is costly, each shareholder will free-ride in the hope that other shareholders will do the monitoring.</seg>
<seg id="368">  Unfortunately, all shareholders think the same way and the net result is that noˇŞor almost noˇŞmonitoring will take place.</seg>
<seg id="369">  Sometimes this free-rider problem can be overcome by someone who acquires a large stake in the company and takes it over (or exerts control in some other way).</seg>
<seg id="370">  However, the take-over mechanism does not always work well.</seg>
<seg id="371">  141 The conclusion is that in many cases the managers or board of directors of a public company can pursue their own goals, possibly at the expense of those of shareholders, with little or no outside interference.</seg>
<seg id="372">  This chapter studies how the owners of a company can constrain the behaviour of management when there is a separation between ownership and control.</seg>
<seg id="373">  One possibility is to put managers on an incentive scheme.</seg>
<seg id="374">  However, while an incentive scheme may work well in motivating managers to exert effort, it is likely to be less effective in getting managers to cut back on empire-building or to relinquish control.</seg>
<seg id="375">  The reason is that, if managers have a strong interest in power, empire, and perks, a very large bribe may be required to persuade managers to give up these things.</seg>
<seg id="376">  It may be better for investors to force managers to curb their empire-building tendencies.</seg>
<seg id="377">  Placing debt in the capital structure is one way to do this.</seg>
<seg id="378">  Moreover, debt is more flexible than an incentive scheme in that it makes the set of choices available to the manager sensitive to the market's assessment of company prospects.</seg>
<seg id="379">  This chapter illustrates the constraining role of debt using two simple models.</seg>
<seg id="380">  In both there is a conflict of interest over the assets that should be under management control.</seg>
<seg id="381">  In the first model the focus is on whether management should shrink its empire over time.</seg>
<seg id="382">  The second model considers the closely related question of how much management should expand its empire.</seg>
<seg id="383">  The reason for considering two models is that the situations of asset liquidation and expansion are analytically distinct.</seg>
<seg id="384">  In the first short-term debt is critically important, while in the second long-term debt plays a more significant role.</seg>
<seg id="385">  However, there is a short discussion in the text, and a longer discussion in the Appendix, of a special case in which the two models can be merged.</seg>
<seg id="386">  The chapter is organized as follows.</seg>
<seg id="387">  Section 1 introduces some of the assumptions behind the models, while the models are developed in Sections2¨C4.</seg>
<seg id="388">  Section 5 argues that, even though the models are very simple and stylized, they can explain some of the empirical evidence about capital structure.</seg>
<seg id="389">  Section 6  compares the ˇ®incentiveˇŻ or ˇ®agencyˇŻ approach described here with other theories of capital structure in the literature.</seg>
<seg id="390">  Much of the literature has either ignored agency problems altogether, stressing the tax or market completion benefits of debt, or has focused on the conflict of interest between shareholders and creditors, rather on that between investors and management.</seg>
<seg id="391">  144 I shall argue that only by considering the conflict between investors and management can one explain why companies issue senior debt, and why a failure to make a debt payment leads to a penalty in the form of bankruptcy, i.e. why debt is associated with a hard budget constraint.</seg>
<seg id="392">  Introduction to the Models</seg>
<seg id="393">  The models used in this chapter differ from those in Chapter 5 in several respects.</seg>
<seg id="394">  It is worth spelling out some of the key assumptions made before the formal analysis begins.</seg>
<seg id="395">  1. It is now supposed that the company has a large number of small investors; that is, its equity and debt are dispersed.</seg>
<seg id="396">  2. It is assumed that capital structure decisions are made to maximize the expected return to investors, rather than to maximize management's utility subject to investors breaking even.</seg>
<seg id="397">  It is also supposed that management's utility is at least as high as what it can get elsewhere, i.e. that management's participation constraint is not binding.</seg>
<seg id="398">  3. The analysis considers the (admittedly) extreme case where the manager's utility is strictly increasing in the assets under his control, and is completely independent of his monetary compensation (his salary).</seg>
<seg id="399">  4. It is supposed that management cannot divert all of the firm's profit for its own use.</seg>
<seg id="400">  As a result, (outside) equity will have a positive market value in equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="401">  The above four assumptions, although strong, seem to be reasonable starting points for a study of a public company.</seg>
<seg id="402">  The first captures the idea that investors are wealth-constrained.</seg>
<seg id="403">  Note that this assumption effectively rules out renegotiation with creditors (because of free-rider problems; see Ch. 5, ˇě5, for a discussion).</seg>
<seg id="404">  Thus, if a company defaults on its debts then it automatically goes into bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="405">  Assumption 2 formalizes the idea that managers'  preferences are unimportant relative to those of investors.</seg>
<seg id="406">  Assumption 3 is made for simplicity; it implies that incentive schemes have essentially no role to play in motivating management.</seg>
<seg id="407">  It allows the analysis to focus on debt as a way of constraining managers' behaviour.</seg>
<seg id="408">  The final assumption makes the models of this chapter in some ways richer than the Hart¨CMoore model of Chapter 5, since (outside) equity has a positive market value.</seg>
<seg id="409">  Readers will be in a better position to understand the role of these assumptions, and to judge how restrictive they are (or are not), as the analysis proceeds.</seg>
<seg id="410">  Model 2</seg>
<seg id="411">  Model 1 is concerned with a situation where the only issue is whether the firm should shrink.</seg>
<seg id="412">  Under these conditions, short-term debt is of primary importance (to trigger liquidation), while long-term debt is somewhat less important.</seg>
<seg id="413">  Model 2 allows for the possibility of expansion.</seg>
<seg id="414">  This provides a more interesting role for long-term debt as a means of regulating the inflow of new capital.154 Unfortunately, it is difficult to combine liquidation and new investment, and so model 2 makes a simplifying assumption (see Assumption 1 below).</seg>
<seg id="415">  This assumption implies that it is optimal to set short-term debt equal to zero, so that no liquidation occurs in equilibrium.</seg>
<seg id="416">  The analysis can therefore focus on long-term debt.</seg>
<seg id="417">  The timing is as before, except that at date 1 the firm may undertake an investment project (see Figure 6.2).</seg>
<seg id="418">  The project costs i and yields r at date 2.</seg>
<seg id="419">  The variables i and r are uncertain as of date 0, but the uncertainty is resolved at date 1.</seg>
<seg id="420">  The manager's empire-building tendencies are such that he always wants to invest if he can.</seg>
<seg id="421">  That is, just as the manager wants to avoid liquidation at all cost, so he wants to invest at all cost.</seg>
<seg id="422">  The only thing that can stop him is an inability to raise the capital.</seg>
<seg id="423">  However, as in model 1, once the investment is financed, the manager has no further use for company funds.</seg>
<seg id="424">  The lesson from this second model complements that from the first.</seg>
<seg id="425">  If management is interested in empire-building, the danger for investors is that management will try to raise capital for unprofitable investment projects by issuing claims against earnings from existing assets.</seg>
<seg id="426">  Senior long-term debt, by mortgaging part of long-term earnings, reduces management's ability to do this.</seg>
<seg id="427">  However, too much long-term debt prevents managers from carrying out even those projects that are profitable.</seg>
<seg id="428">  Assumption 1 was useful in simplifying model 2, but it ruled out an interesting economic case, stressed particularly by Jensen (1986).</seg>
<seg id="429">  If y &gt; i, the firm has free cash flow and it may use this to make unprofitable investments (i.e. it may invest when r&lt; i).</seg>
<seg id="430">  Short-term debt then has a role in forcing the manager to pay out this free cash flow.</seg>
<seg id="431">  Note that this role of short-term debt differs from that in model 1, where short-term debt was used to trigger liquidation, rather than to prevent expansion.</seg>
<seg id="432">  I analyse this new role of short-term debt in the Appendix.</seg>
<seg id="433">  To do so I combine models 1 and 2.</seg>
<seg id="434">  However, merging the models in general is difficult and so two simplifying assumptions are made.</seg>
<seg id="435">  First, it is supposed that the manager has an unlimited number of new investment projects (rather than just one) and that they are unprofitable; i.e., r&lt; i for each of them. (In fact, for simplicity, r is taken to be zero.) Second, it is supposed that y &gt; L; i.e. liquidation is always inefficient (as in Assumption 1).</seg>
<seg id="436">  The choice of debt is then determined by the following considerations.</seg>
<seg id="437">  To minimize the resources the manager has for unprofitable reinvestment, P and P2 should be set high.</seg>
<seg id="438">  Observed Patterns of Capital Structure</seg>
<seg id="439">  Having developed some simple models of debtˇŞequity choice, I consider next what light these models can throw on actual capital structure choices.</seg>
<seg id="440">  A great deal of empirical work has been done on capital structure.</seg>
<seg id="441">  Although not all the findings agree, some stylized facts have emerged.</seg>
<seg id="442">  I shall take these facts as given in what follows.</seg>
<seg id="443">  The stylized facts are that profitable firms have low debt; that firms with a large proportion of tangible assets have high debt; that firms with stable cash flows have high debt; that debt-for-equity swaps raise share prices; that equity-for-debt swaps lower share prices; and that pure equity issues lower share prices.157</seg>
<seg id="444">  I shall argue that all of these facts can be explained by the models described above.</seg>
<seg id="445">  However, not all are inevitable consequences of these models.</seg>
<seg id="446">  In some cases the models predict that reversals could also be observed.</seg>
<seg id="447">  Some may regard this ambiguity as a weakness of the agency approach to debt.</seg>
<seg id="448">  I suspect, however, that this feature is shared by other theories of capital structure.</seg>
<seg id="449">  That is, if ˇ®suitableˇŻ assumptions are made about parameter values and the structure of information, other theories can also predict reversals.</seg>
<seg id="450">  I shall argue below that one advantage of the agency approach over other theories is that the agency approach can explain something that other theories cannot explain: why firms issue ˇ®hardˇŻ debt, i.e. debt that is senior and that triggers bankruptcy in the event of default.</seg>
<seg id="451">  Other Theories of Capital Structure</seg>
<seg id="452">  AsI have noted previously, much of the literature on capital structure does not take an agency perspective.</seg>
<seg id="453">  In fact, since Modigliani and Miller's famous irrelevance theorem (Modigliani and Miller 1958), the literature has tended to focus on the role of taxes, asymmetric information, or incomplete markets as explanationsof capital structure decisions, rather than on agency problems.</seg>
<seg id="454">  In the last part of this chapter, I want to discuss briefly why I think an agency perspective is important, and in particular why the conflict of interest between a company's managers and its investors is crucial for an understanding of capital structure.</seg>
<seg id="455">  I shall argue that a great advantage of the agency approach over other theories in the literature is that the agency approach can explain why firms issue senior debt and why a failure to make debt payments leads to a penalty in the form of bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="456">  A considerably more detailed account of the ideas presented here can be found in Hart (1993).</seg>
<seg id="457">  It is helpful to use model 1 as a vehicle for this discussion.</seg>

(It is supposed that the manager is reliable and honest, but has a ˇ®normalˇŻ interest in money.) <seg id="460">  7 Bankruptcy Procedure</seg>
<seg id="461">  THE last two chapters have analysed the financial structure of a closely held company (Chapter 5) and a public company (Chapter 6).</seg>
<seg id="462">  It was argued that debt can play an important role in constraining managers or owners of both kinds of company.</seg>
<seg id="463">  In the Hart¨CMoore model of Chapter 5, debt forced an entrepreneur to pay out funds to investors rather than to himself.</seg>
<seg id="464">  In the various models of Chapter 6, debt forced the sale of assets and limited a manager's ability to make unprofitable, but power-enhancing, investments.</seg>
<seg id="465">  Of course, given that a company takes on debt, there will be circumstances, arising perhaps from an unexpected shock, in which the company will default on this debt.</seg>
<seg id="466">  In such a situation, the company is (in effect) bankrupt.</seg>
<seg id="467">  The question is: what should happen in a bankruptcy state? (To put it another way, what lies behind the ˇ®liquidationˇŻ value L in Chapters5 and 6?) This is the topic of the present chapter.</seg>
<seg id="468">  The chapter begins with a discussion of the need for, and goals of, a formal bankruptcy procedure (Sections 1 and 2).</seg>
<seg id="469">  Section 3 describes existing procedures in the West, with a particular emphasis on US and (to a lesser extent) UK procedures.</seg>
<seg id="470">  The two main US procedures are ˇ®Chapter 7ˇŻ and ˇ®Chapter 11ˇŻ.</seg>
<seg id="471">  Chapter 7 calls for a bankrupt company to be sold off for cash.</seg>
<seg id="472">  In contrast, Chapter 11 is an attempt to allow companies to reorganize.</seg>
<seg id="473">  Unfortunately, there is widespread dissatisfaction with both procedures.</seg>
<seg id="474">  Chapter 7 is thought to lead to the liquidation of healthy companies.</seg>
<seg id="475">  Chapter 11 has been criticized for being cumbersome and biased in favour of reorganization under incumbent management.</seg>
<seg id="476">  Chapter 11 is also flawed because it mixes the decision of what should happen to the company with the decision of who gets what.</seg>
<seg id="477">  This leads to a great deal of haggling.</seg>
<seg id="478">  Section 4 turns to a procedureˇŞproposed elsewhere by Philippe Aghion, John Moore, and myselfˇŞthat tries to combine the best features of Chapters 7 and 11.170 (The procedure uses in a significant way an idea of Lucian Bebchuk; see Bebchuk (1988).) In the Aghion¨CHart¨CMoore (AHM) procedure, debt claims are converted into equity, and the decision about whether to liquidate or reorganize is then put to a vote by the new shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="479">  Sections 5¨C7 discus s some practical difficulties concerning the AHM procedure and how they might be resolved, and provide some concluding remarks.</seg>
<seg id="480">  As mentioned earlier, the style of this chapter is very different from that of the others in the book.</seg>
<seg id="481">  There is no model, and the argument is conducted at a fairly informal level.</seg>
<seg id="482">  The chapter attempts to apply theoretical ideas to throw light on an important practical issue.</seg>
<seg id="483">  It should be judged by whether it is successful in this regard.</seg>
<seg id="484">  The Need for a Formal Bankruptcy Procedure</seg>
<seg id="485">  It is helpful to begin the discussion with the case studied in Chapter 5 of a single debtor and a single creditor.</seg>
<seg id="486">  It was observed there that default is an event where control of the assets shifts from the debtor to the creditor; that is, the creditor becomes the new owner of the assets.</seg>
<seg id="487">  In this simple case there is little if any need for a formal bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="488">  Bankruptcy procedure should do no more than ensure that a transfer of ownership and control does take place in the default state, i.e. that the terms of the debt contract are upheld.</seg>
<seg id="489">  After this, the creditor should have the right to decide whether to sell off the assets, or renegotiate her debt contract with the entrepreneur, so as to keep the assets in place.</seg>
<seg id="490">  Matters become more complicated when there are many creditors (the situation studied in Chapter 6).</seg>
<seg id="491">  The problem is that in this case it is not really clear what a ˇ®transfer of ownership and control to (all the) creditorsˇŻ means.</seg>
<seg id="492">  In the absence of a formal bankruptcy procedure, the law provides a creditor with two main remedies in the event of a default.</seg>
<seg id="493">  First, with regard to a secured loan, the creditor can seize the assets that serve as collateral for the loan.</seg>
<seg id="494">  Second, with regard to an unsecured loan, the creditor can sue the debtor and call on a sheriff to enforce the court's judgment, possibly by selling the debtor's assets (see Baird and Jackson 1985).</seg>
<seg id="495">  Difficulties arise when the debtor has insufficient assets to cover his liabilities (which is very likely to be the caseˇŞotherwise why would the debtor have gone bankrupt?).</seg>
<seg id="496">  Creditors may then engage in a socially wasteful race to be first to seize their collateral or to obtain a judgment against the debtor.</seg>
<seg id="497">  172 AlsoˇŞand perhaps more importantˇŞthis race may lead to the dismantlement of the debtor's assets and to a loss of value for all creditors if these assets are worth more as a whole than as a collection of pieces.</seg>
<seg id="498">  Given this, it is in the collective interest of creditors that the disposition of the debtor's assets be carried out in an orderly manner.</seg>
<seg id="499">  This is the rationale for a formal bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="500">  Nothing so far implies that the formal bankruptcy procedure needs to be provided by the state rather than by the parties themselves.</seg>
<seg id="501">  In a world of costless contracting, debtors and creditors would anticipate the possibility of default and the consequent collective action problems and specify as part of their initial contract what should happen in a default stateˇŞin particular, whether the company should be reorganized or liquidated and how its value should be divided up among various claimants.</seg>
<seg id="502">  In practice, transaction costs may be too large for debtors and creditors to design their own bankruptcy procedure contractually, particularly in situations where debtors acquire new assets and new creditors as time passes.</seg>
<seg id="503">  Instead, parties may prefer to rely on a ˇ®standard formˇŻ state-provided bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="504">  It is a long way from this observation, however, to any conclusions about the nature of an optimal standard-form procedure.</seg>
<seg id="505">  The problem is that a satisfactory analysis of an optimal procedure requires a theory both of why contracts are incomplete, and of how the state can overcome this incompleteness.</seg>
<seg id="506">  Chapters 2¨C4 shed some light on the first of these questions, but not on the second.</seg>
<seg id="507">  In particular, in Chapters2¨C4 contractual incompleteness was the result of the courts' inability to observe variables that the parties could observe.</seg>
<seg id="508">  Thus, the courts were the source of contractual incompleteness rather than the solution.</seg>
<seg id="509">  In what follows, therefore, optimal bankruptcy procedure is not derived from first principles. Instead, some goals that an efficient bankruptcy procedure should satisfy are suggested, and a procedure is described that does a reasonable job of meeting these goals.</seg>
<seg id="510">  It will be argued that the procedure presented is practical and avoids some of the pitfalls of existing procedures.</seg>
<seg id="511">  Also, the procedure is sufficiently simple and natural that future work may show it to be optimal within a reasonable class of procedures.</seg>
<seg id="512">  Goals of a Bankruptcy Procedure</seg>
<seg id="513">  As mentioned above, the analysis doesnot proceed from first principles.</seg>
<seg id="514">  However, economic theory suggests that the following are desirable goals for a bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="515">  1. A good bankruptcy procedure should achieve an ex post efficient outcome (that is, an outcome that maximizes the total value of the proceedsˇŞmeasured in money termsˇŞreceived by the existing claimants).175</seg>
<seg id="516">  2. A good bankruptcy procedure should preserve the ( ex ante) bonding role of debt by penalizing managers adequately in bankruptcy states.</seg>
<seg id="517">  However, bankruptcy should not be so harsh that managers try to avoid it at any cost, e.g. by ˇ®gamblingˇŻ with the company's assets.</seg>
<seg id="518">  3. A good bankruptcy procedure should preserve the absolute priority of claims; that is, the most senior creditors should be paid off before anything is given to the next most senior creditors, and so on down the ladder (with ordinary shareholders at the bottom).</seg>
<seg id="519">  A brief justification for these goals follows.</seg>
<seg id="520">  Goal 1 simply reflects the fact that, other things equal, more is preferred to less; in particular, if a procedure can be modified to deliver higher total ex post value, then, given that absolute priority is preserved (i.e. goal 3), everybody will be better off.</seg>
<seg id="521">  The motivation for goal 2 should be clear, given the analysis of Chapter 6 on the disciplinary role of debt.</seg>
<seg id="522">  It was observed there that debt may have an important role in constraining or bonding managers to act in the interest of claim-holders.</seg>
<seg id="523">  A bankruptcy procedure that lets managers off too lightly if they fail to pay their debtsˇŞfor example by favouring them in the reorganization processˇŞwill interfere with this ex ante bonding role of debt.</seg>
<seg id="524">  Goal 3 is desirable for several reasons.</seg>
<seg id="525">  First, if a structure of debt priority that was agreed to contractually can always be violated within bankruptcy, then people may be unwilling to lend to the company in the first place, since their claims will be unprotected.</seg>
<seg id="526">  Second, a discrepancy between entitlements inside and outside bankruptcy can lead to inefficient rent-seeking, with some people bribing management into deliberately precipitating bankruptcy and other people attempting to forestall bankruptcy (see Jackson 1986).</seg>
<seg id="527">  Third, model 2 of Chapter 6 shows that management may issue senior debt in order to commit itself not to finance unprofitable projects.</seg>
<seg id="528">  This ability to commit will be weakened if the seniority of claims is not respected.</seg>
<seg id="529">  Although goals1¨C3 are appealing, they are not beyond question.</seg>
<seg id="530">  Bankruptcy scholars have raised doubts about goal 3 in particular.</seg>
<seg id="531">  Critics argue that, if equity-holders get little or nothing in a bankruptcy proceeding, then managementˇŞacting on the equity-holders' behalfˇŞwill engage in risky, but inefficient, behaviour when a company is close to bankruptcy, because, while the shareholders gain if things go well, it is the creditors that lose if things go badly (see White 1989).</seg>
<seg id="532">  This argument supposes that management acts on behalf of shareholders, an assumption that may be plausible for small owner-managed companies but is questionable for large, public companies.</seg>
<seg id="533">  Chapter 6 argued that it is more reasonable to suppose that the management of a public company is self-interested.</seg>
<seg id="534">  Under these conditions, there is a case for making bankruptcy procedure less harsh for managersˇŞto prevent them from engaging in highly risky behaviour to save their jobsˇŞbut this is already covered under goal 2.</seg>
<seg id="535">  Even in the case of small, owner-managed companies, it is far from clear that departures from absolute priority are the best way to soften the blow of bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="536">  A better method might be to give managers and/or owners a ˇ®golden handshakeˇŻ (or ˇ®parachuteˇŻ) in the form of senior debt.</seg>
<seg id="537">  Given the above, goals 1¨C3 will be assumed to be desirable, ceteris paribus.</seg>
<seg id="538">  It is worth noting, however, that the procedure proposed below could be modified to allow for departures from absolute priority if this were felt to be a good idea.</seg>
<seg id="539">  A final point to make is that some of the three goals may be in conflict.</seg>
<seg id="540">  For example, if incumbent management has special skills, then ex post efficiency (goal 1) might call for the incumbent management of a bankrupt company to be retained.</seg>
<seg id="541">  However, knowing this, management might have little incentive to avoid bankruptcy; i.e. goal 2 would not be served.177</seg>
<seg id="542">  In view of this, the best one can hope for is a reasonable balance between the above goals, particularly goals 1 and 2.</seg>
<seg id="543">  The procedure discussed below is constructed with this in mind.</seg>
<seg id="544">  Although I will argue that it does a satisfactory job, the procedure could be fine-tuned if the balance were felt to be wrong; this point is considered further below.</seg>
<seg id="545">  Existing Procedures</seg>
<seg id="546">  This section discusses existing procedures.</seg>
<seg id="547">  Although there are many different bankruptcy procedures used around the world, they fall into two main categories: cash auctions and structured bargaining.</seg>
<seg id="548">  These are discussed in turn, with particular attention being paid to their application in the USA and the UK.</seg>
<seg id="549">  Cash Auctions</seg>
<seg id="550">  In a cash auction, a trustee or receiver supervises the sale of the company's assets.</seg>
<seg id="551">  Often the assets are sold piecemeal; in other words, the company is liquidated (having been closed down).</seg>
<seg id="552">  Sometimes, however, the company is sold as a going concern.</seg>
<seg id="553">  Whichever occurs, the receipts from the sale are distributed among former claimants according to absolute priority (usually secured debt, then various priority claims, then unsecured debt, then subordinated debt, and finally equity).</seg>
<seg id="554">  In a world of perfect capital markets, a cash auction would (presumably) be the ideal bankruptcy procedure (see Baird 1986).</seg>
<seg id="555">  Anybody who could make the company profitable would be able to raise cash from some source (a commercial bank, an investment bank, the stock market) and make a bid for the company.</seg>
<seg id="556">  Perfect competition among bidders would ensure that the company was sold for its true value, and that the company's assets ended up in their highest value use; that is, the company would be maintained as a going concern if and only if its going concern value exceeded its liquidation value.</seg>
<seg id="557">  In practice, there is widespread scepticism about the efficacy of cash auctions.</seg>
<seg id="558">  The feeling is that a combination of transaction costs, asymmetric information, and moral hazard makes it difficult for bidders to raise sufficient cash to maintain a company as a going concern (i.e. capital markets are not perfect).</seg>
<seg id="559">  Asa consequence, there may be a lack of competition in the auction and few bids to keep the company whole.</seg>
<seg id="560">  The result will be that some companies are liquidated piecemeal and/or sold at a low price.</seg>
<seg id="561">  It is worth spelling out a transaction cost reason for imperfect capital markets.</seg>
<seg id="562">  Suppose that a huge public company such as General Electric were put on the block.</seg>
<seg id="563">  Say the expected present value of GE's earnings is$100 billion.</seg>
<seg id="564">  Would any bidder be prepared to offer this much for the company?</seg>
<seg id="565">  The answer to this question might well be no. Someone making a cash bid for the company is, in effect, taking GE private. (This is unless the bidder itself represents a public company.) The bidder's intention may well be to take GE public again later, but in the meantime the bidder is bearing the risk of changes in GE's value.</seg>
<seg id="566">  The bidder will, of course, ˇ®chargeˇŻ for this risk-bearing by offering a lower price in the original auction.</seg>
<seg id="567">  The consequence of this is twofold.</seg>
<seg id="568">  First, the going-concern bid may lose to a collection of piecemeal bids for GE's assets, since the latter achieve risk-sharing by spreading risk over a large number of bidders.</seg>
<seg id="569">  Second, regardless of who wins the auction, the amount of cash raised will tend to be lower.</seg>
<seg id="570">  It should be emphasized that the transaction cost being identified here is not to do with management having private information about the company's value (and being unable to verify it to the market); rather, it is to do with the difficulty of assembling a suitable group of investors to be risk-bearers for the new company.179 Note, however, that there is a natural group of risk-bearers at hand: the former claimants (who were, after all, the previous risk-bearers).</seg>
<seg id="571">  Transaction costs would be reduced if bidders could reach this group directly by offering them securities in the post-bankruptcy company.</seg>
<seg id="572">  This is not allowed for in a cash-only auction like ˇ®Chapter 7ˇŻ but is a key feature of the bankruptcy procedure described below (and also of ˇ®Chapter 11ˇŻ).180</seg>
<seg id="573">  Neither the above theoretical argument nor the empirical evidence cited in n. 10 provides much indication of the magnitude of the imperfections in capital markets.</seg>
<seg id="574">  Given this, it is desirable to adopt a bankruptcy procedure that works well whether capital markets are imperfect or not.</seg>
<seg id="575">  The procedure described later has this flexibility.</seg>
<seg id="576">  One version of it consists of an auction in which both cash and noncash bids for the company are allowed.</seg>
<seg id="577">  If capital markets are perfect, the company will go to the bidder with the highest willingness to payˇŞmoreover, this bidder can do no better than to offer cashˇŞand thus the outcome will be exactly the same as in a cash-only auction.</seg>
<seg id="578">  On the other hand, if capital markets are imperfect, the procedure can deliver an outcome that is superior to that achievable by a cash auction.</seg>
<seg id="579">  Structured Bargaining</seg>
<seg id="580">  Because of the concern about the effectiveness of cash auctions, a number of countries have developed alternative procedures based on the idea of structured bargaining.</seg>
<seg id="581">  The basic idea behind these procedures is that the company's claimants are encouraged to bargain about the future of the companyˇŞin particular, whether it should be liquidated or reorganized and how its value should be divided upˇŞaccording to predetermined rules.</seg>
<seg id="582">  The leading example of a structured bargaining procedure in the West is Chapter 11 of the US Bankruptcy Code; however, UK Administration is based on similar ideas, as are procedures in France, Germany, and Japan.</seg>
<seg id="583">  The details of Chapter 11 are complicated, but the basic elements are as follows.</seg>
<seg id="584">  A stay is put on creditors' claims(that is, they are frozen; no creditor is allowed to seize, or have a sheriff sell, any of the company's assets during the process); claim-holders are grouped into classes according to the type of claim they have (secured or unsecured, senior or junior); committees or trustees are appointed to represent each class; and a judge supervises a process of bargaining among the committees to determine a plan of action and a division of value for the company.</seg>
<seg id="585">  During the process, incumbent management usually runs the company.</seg>
<seg id="586">  An important part of the procedure is that a plan can be implemented if it receives approval by a suitable majority of each claimant class; unanimity is not required.181</seg>
<seg id="587">  UK Administration was introduced in the 1986 Insolvency Act as ˇ®the British version of Chapter 11ˇŻ.</seg>
<seg id="588">  An important difference between UK Administration and Chapter 11 is that the UK administrator (who is an insolvency practitioner) runs the company during bankruptcy, rather than incumbent management.</seg>
<seg id="589">  There are also a number of differences in the voting rules between the two procedures.</seg>
<seg id="590">  To date, the costs of UK Administration are such that it has rarely been used.</seg>
<seg id="591">  Chapter 11 has been subject to a great deal of criticism in the last few years.</seg>
<seg id="592">  Among other things, practitioners and commentators have claimed that it is time-consuming, that it involves significant legal and administrative costs, that it causes considerable loss in the bankrupt company's value, that it is (relatively) soft on management, and that the judges who run it sometimes abuse their powers.</seg>
<seg id="593">  It would undoubtedly be possible to modify Chapter 11ˇŞand procedureslike itˇŞto improve matters, and a number of suggestions along these lines have been made.</seg>
<seg id="594">  However, there are two fundamental problems inherent in any structured bargaining procedure that no amount of tinkering can solve.</seg>
<seg id="595">  These problems are associated with the fact that a structured bargaining procedure like Chapter 11 attempts to make two decisions at once: what to do with the company, and who should get what in the event of a restructuring of claims.</seg>
<seg id="596">  Problem 1.  Restructured companies do not have an objective value.</seg>
<seg id="597">  Consequently, it is hard to know what fraction of the post-bankruptcy company's securities each group of creditors is entitled to receive.</seg>
<seg id="598">  This is true even if there is no dispute about the amount and seniority of each creditor's claim.</seg>
<seg id="599">  As a result, there can be a great deal of haggling.</seg>
<seg id="600">  Problem 2.  Perhaps even more serious, there is a danger that the wrong decision will be made concerning the company's future.</seg>
<seg id="601">  The voting mechanism is fixed in advance, which means that those people whose payoff ought not to be affected by the outcome (either because they are fully protected anyway, or because they are not entitled to anything) may end up controlling the pivotal votes.</seg>
<seg id="602">  Problem 1 is well understood, having been discussed at some length in the literature.183 Problem 2 has also been noted but has been subject to less analysis.</seg>
<seg id="603">  An example may help to illustrate it.</seg>
<seg id="604">  Example 1.</seg>
<seg id="605">  Suppose senior creditors are owed 100 (dollars or pounds) and the liquidation value of the company is 90.</seg>
<seg id="606">  Assume that, if the company were maintained as a going concern for six months, it would be worth on average 110 (suppose the interest rate is zero).</seg>
<seg id="607">  However, there is uncertainty: if things go well, it will be worth 180; if things go badly, it will be worth only 40, where the outcomes are equally likely. (The average of 180 and 40 is110.) Clearly, the value-maximizing choice is to keep the company going, since 110 exceeds the liquidation value of 90.</seg>
<seg id="608">  However, it is not in the senior creditors' interest to do this.</seg>
<seg id="609">  If things go well and the company is worth 180, the senior creditors get only the 100 they are owed; but if things go badly, they get just 40.</seg>
<seg id="610">  The average of these amounts is 70, which is less than the 90 the senior creditors receive from immediate liquidation.</seg>
<seg id="611">  In this example senior creditors may vote to liquidate the company immediately rather than enter into a lengthy negotiation which might lead to the company's being saved.</seg>
<seg id="612">  This is in spite of the fact that there is enough value in the efficient outcome for the senior creditors to be paid off in full: 110 exceeds the 100 senior debt.</seg>
<seg id="613">  Had the senior creditors been paid off, and the vote left in the hands of the junior creditors and the shareholders(whose money is at stake), then the junior creditors would have made the efficient decision about the company's future.</seg>
<seg id="614">  Things may go the other way, though.</seg>
<seg id="615">  Consider a variant on example 1.</seg>
<seg id="616">  Example 2.  Make assumptions as in example 1, except that the upside value from continuation is lowerˇŞonly 120 rather than 180.</seg>
<seg id="617">  Thus, the average value from continuation is 80 (the average of 120 and 40).</seg>
<seg id="618">  In this example the junior creditors and shareholders are not entitled to anything, since the best that can happen to the company is that it is liquidated for 90, which is less than the senior debt.</seg>
<seg id="619">  So the junior creditors and shareholders ought not to be party to the decision over the company's future.</seg>
<seg id="620">  And yet, the rules of Chapter 11 dictate that they do have votes, and as a result they may be in a position to press for continuation (since they can see the upside potential of 120).</seg>
<seg id="621">  If the junior creditors and shareholders have enough votes to veto a liquidation plan, then at best the senior creditors may have to bribe them to accept itˇŞwhich would lead to a violation of absolute priorityˇŞand at worst the company may be inefficiently kept going.</seg>
<seg id="622">  185 Notice that, had the vote been left in the hands of senior creditors, they would have made the correct decision about the company's future.</seg>
<seg id="623">  At this point, it is worth asking why the various claimants cannot bargain around the inefficiencies described in examples1 and 2. Probably the most important reason is that, in the case of large companies, there are often numerous claimants(bondholders, trade creditors, and shareholders), which can make negotiation around a given (inefficient) procedure very difficult and lengthy (owing to free-rider and hold-out problems, combined with asymmetries of information among claimants).</seg>
<seg id="624">  Consider example 1, where senior creditors might vote to liquidate the company.</seg>
<seg id="625">  This outcome could be avoided if junior creditors and shareholders could bribe the senior creditors not to liquidate; for example, they could buy out the senior creditors at a price between 90 and 100.</seg>
<seg id="626">  However, the more numerous and heterogeneous the junior creditors and shareholders are, the more difficult it isˇŞand the longer it will takeˇŞto co-ordinate such an offer. (Each junior claimant will want the other junior claimants to bribe the senior creditors.) As a result, either an agreement will not be reached, or it will require lengthy negotiation (there may be a ˇ®war of attritionˇŻ).187</seg>
<seg id="627">  Similar problems arise in example 2, where senior creditors must collectively decide to make concessions to junior claimants to compensate them for not pursuing reorganization.</seg>
<seg id="628">  It may be easier to achieve agreement in example 2, however, to the extent that the number of senior creditors is relatively small and co-ordination problems are therefore reduced.</seg>
<seg id="629">  A structured procedure like Chapter 11 reduces the severity of the above bargaining problems by making the majority's will binding on the minority. (In contrast to an out-of-bankruptcy workout, unanimity is not required, which mitigates free-riding and hold-out behaviour.) However, even in this case an efficient outcome may not be reached, e.g. because of asymmetries of information.</seg>
<seg id="630">  Suppose, in example 1, that junior claimants are unsure whether the company's liquidation value is really 90 as opposed to some lower figure, while senior creditors know the true value.</seg>
<seg id="631">  Then junior creditors may quite rationally ˇ®low-ballˇŻ the senior creditors by offering them less than 90 to compensate them for not liquidating.</seg>
<seg id="632">  In this case the senior creditors will turn them down if the true liquidation value is 90, and a valuable going-concern opportunity will be lost.</seg>
<seg id="633">  An Alternative Procedure</seg>
<seg id="634">  The previous discussion can be summarized as follows.</seg>
<seg id="635">  Existing bankruptcy procedures are flawed for two reasons: either they assume perfect capital markets (as in US Chapter 7), or they mix the decision of what should happen to the company with the decision of who gets what (as in US Chapter 11).</seg>
<seg id="636">  I now describe a procedure (the AHM procedure) that does not suffer from these defects.</seg>
<seg id="637">  The key lies in transforming a group of people with different claims (and therefore different objectives) into a homogeneous class of shareholders, and then putting the company's future to a simple vote.</seg>
<seg id="638">  The AHM procedure also avoids bargaining over the division of the pie, since it uses a mechanical scheme for distributing shares that preserves absolute priority. (The scheme is due to Lucian Bebchuk; see Bebchuk 1988.)189</seg>
<seg id="639">  It turns out that the idea behind the proposal is more important than the details.</seg>
<seg id="640">  Thus, there are several versions of the procedure, all of which are consistent with the same basic philosophy.</seg>
<seg id="641">  Two versions are presented here.</seg>
<seg id="642">  They differ with respect to their treatment of secured debt and with respect to the role played by the judge, or bankruptcy practitioner, in charge of the procedure.</seg>
<seg id="643">  Version 1 is closer in spirit to US Chapter 11; version 2 is closer in spirit to UK Administrative Receivership or UK Administration. (It also bears a resemblance to old US Chapter X.) If the proposal were to be adopted, the version appropriate for a particular country would depend on that country's institutional structure and legal traditions. (Mixtures of versions 1 and 2 are also possible.) Version 1</seg>
<seg id="644">  At the outset, after the company has declared (or been pushed into) bankruptcy, the company's debts are cancelled.</seg>
<seg id="645">  The company's creditors do not go away empty-handed, however; as described in task B below, they will typically become significant shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="646">  What matters is that the company starts out life in bankruptcy as a ˇ®newˇŻ, all-equity company.</seg>
<seg id="647">  An individualˇŞa judge, s ayˇŞis appointed to supervise the process.</seg>
<seg id="648">  The judge has two immediate tasks: (A) soliciting cash and noncash bids for the new all-equity company; and (B) allocating rights to the shares in this company.</seg>
<seg id="649">  These tasks could be carried out in parallel and completed within a pre-specified period of time: three months might be reasonable.</seg>
<seg id="650">  Task A: Soliciting bids.  The judge solicits bids for the company over a three-month period.</seg>
<seg id="651">  However, in contrast to a standard auction, individuals can make cash or noncash bids for the company.</seg>
<seg id="652">  In a noncash bid, someone would offer securities in the post-bankruptcy company instead of cash; thus, a noncash bid embraces the possibility of reorganization and/or recapitalization of the company as a going concern.</seg>
<seg id="653">  The following are some examples of a noncash bid: 1. The old managers propose to keep their jobs, and offer claimants a share in the post-bankruptcy company.</seg>
<seg id="654">  2. The same financial arrangement might be offered by a new management team.</seg>
<seg id="655">  3. The managers of another company might propose to buy the bankrupt company, offering shares in their company aspayment.</seg>
<seg id="656">  4. Management (old or new) might induce some debt in the company's capital structure.</seg>
<seg id="657">  One way to do this would be to arrange for a bank to lend money to the post-bankruptcy company (the loan is conditional on the bid succeeding), and offer claimants a combination of cash and equity in the (levered) company.</seg>
<seg id="658">  Another way would be to offer claimants a combination of shares and bonds in the post-bankruptcy company.</seg>
<seg id="659">  Note that, for the bidding process to work well, it is important that potential bidders have reasonably accurate information about the company's prospects.</seg>
<seg id="660">  Part of the bankruptcy judge's job will therefore be to ensure that (serious) bidders have access to the company's books during the three-month bid solicitation process.</seg>
<seg id="661">  Task B: Allocating rights.  Before the judge can allocate rights to the shares of the new (all-equity) company, he must first determine who the company's aimants are and what are the amounts and priorities of their claims.</seg>
<seg id="662">  This is a difficult job, which must be carried out currently in bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="663">  The procedure envisages no changes regarding how this job is carried out.</seg>
<seg id="664">  Thus, it is supposed that the judge's deliberations lead to the identification of n classes of creditors who are owed (in total) the amounts D1 , . . . , Dn, respectively, with class 1 having the most senior claim, class 2 the next most senior claim, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="665">  The company's shareholders form the ( n+1)th class, with a claim junior to all others.</seg>
<seg id="666">  Having identified these classes, the judge can proceed to allocate rights to shares in the new (all-equity) company.</seg>
<seg id="667">  If the ˇ®trueˇŻ value, V, of the company were publicly known (i.e. were verifiable), then it would be easy to figure out the total (monetary) share each class i is entitled to, based on absolute priority.</seg>
<seg id="668">  Unfortunately, the value of a noncash bid is not objectively known. (This is why there is so much haggling in Chapter 11.) Therefore in order to allocate shares the procedure adopts an approach due to Bebchuk (1988).</seg>
<seg id="669">  The nice feature of Bebchuk's approach is that it achieves absolute priority in spite of the fact that V is not objectively known.</seg>
<seg id="670">  The bankruptcy practitioner has two main duties.</seg>
<seg id="671">  First, she has a general obligation to act in the interest of shareholders and option-holders combined, that is, to maximize the value of the company during her term of office.</seg>
<seg id="672">  Second, she is obliged to draw up a plan (or plans) for the future of the company, to be presented to shareholders at the end of month 3 (before the options are exercised).195 No restrictions are put on the nature of the plan.</seg>
<seg id="673">  For example, the plan might be to reorganize the companyˇŞmaybe with new management and/or a new financial structure (in which case shareholders would receive new securities in the company); to sell it as a going concern; or to close the company down and sell off its assets piecemeal.</seg>
<seg id="674">  In fact, a plan is just like a cash or noncash bid.</seg>
<seg id="675">  The plan is put to a shareholders' meeting at the end of month 4 (after the options are exercised) and is implemented as long as it receives majority approval. (The bankruptcy practitioner may choose to put more than one plan to shareholders and see which one receives greatest support.)</seg>
<seg id="676">  An important difference from version 1 is that a formal auction isdispensed with.</seg>
<seg id="677">  The bankruptcy practitioner may hold one if she believes this would increase value, but she is not obliged to do so.196 Similarly, the practitioner has discretion about who should have access to the company's books andˇŞif bids are madeˇŞwhich bids should be presented to shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="678">  In this respect, the practitioner is in a similar position to the board of directors of a solvent company that is thinking of selling it.</seg>
<seg id="679">  In version 2, the bankruptcy practitioner would also have the authority to take other actions.</seg>
<seg id="680">  For example, she could sell some or even all of the company's (unsecured) assets before month 3 if she deemed it necessary to do so. (This might be important in cases where uncertainty about the company's future threatened the customer or supplier base.) In addition, she would have the authority to raise new capital or to release cash from the business.</seg>
<seg id="681">  For example, she might use profit earned by the business or cash from asset sales to pay off (secured) creditors or to pay out a dividend to the (new) shareholders. (In the latter case, the option exercise price for junior claimants would be reduced accordingly.)</seg>
<seg id="682">  The bankruptcy practitioner's appointment is obviously an important issue in version 2, given the power of the position.</seg>
<seg id="683">  In many cases a large (senior) creditor is in the best position to make the appointment.</seg>
<seg id="684">  For example, in the UK a senior creditorˇŞusually a bankˇŞoften negotiates as part of its debt contract the right to appoint a ˇ®receiverˇŻ.</seg>
<seg id="685">  It might make sense for such a creditor also to appoint the bankruptcy practitioner in version 2 of our procedure.</seg>
<seg id="686">  A similar arrangement might be used in Germany or Japan, where companies often have a ˇ®lead bankˇŻ asa significant creditor.</seg>
<seg id="687">  In other cases, when there is no natural large creditor the appointment might be made by the court.</seg>
<seg id="688">  Note that, once appointed, the practitioner has a responsibility to all shareholders (not just the person making the appointment).</seg>
<seg id="689">  This raises the question of who has the authority to remove the practitioner if she is doing a bad job.</seg>
<seg id="690">  Prior to month 4, it probably makes most sense to leave this decision in the hands of the court, with action being taken only when a majority of each class of claimants has expressed dissatisfaction.</seg>
<seg id="691">  The point is that, until the ownership of shares has been settled, it is not clear whom the practitioner is representing, and so she should have a degree of security and a broad remit to ˇ®maximize the value of the companyˇŻ.</seg>
<seg id="692">  Another possibility, however, is to give shareholders the power to dismiss the practitioner from the beginning.</seg>
<seg id="693">  The disadvantage of this is that new shareholders may push the practitioner towards a quick sale of the company: they know that if good news arrives within the four months they will be bought out by the option-holders, where as if bad new comes in they will be 197 An obvious danger with giving the practitioner so much discretion is that she may abuse her power. For example, the practitioner, hoping to extend her term of office past month 4, might engage in empire-building activities.</seg>
<seg id="694">  An Assessment</seg>
<seg id="695">  Consider how well the AHM procedure satisfies the three goals of a bankruptcy procedure discussed earlier.</seg>
<seg id="696">  Arguably, the AHM procedure does a reasonable job of meeting goal 1: since the new owners decide the future of the company, they have an incentive to vote for an efficient outcome.</seg>
<seg id="697">  Also, the procedure preserves the ex ante bonding role of debt (goal 2).</seg>
<seg id="698">  In particular, since shareholders may accept a bid or plan involving another management team, managers' jobs are at risk.</seg>
<seg id="699">  However, managers have the opportunity to convince shareholders that the bankruptcy was not their fault and that they should be retained.</seg>
<seg id="700">  In this respect, bankruptcy is arguably not so harsh that managers will avoid it at any cost.</seg>
<seg id="701">  Note also that the procedure could be modified to be softer or harder on incumbent management (at some probable cost in terms of ex post inefficiency) if that were thought to be desirable.</seg>
<seg id="702">  For example, in version 1 incumbent management could be favoured by handicapping other bidders in the auction; for example, the auction rules could state that an outside bidder has to win more than two-thirds of the votes.</seg>
<seg id="703">  (Another way to soften the blow of bankruptcy in both versions 1 and 2 is to give managers a ˇ®golden handshakeˇŻ (or ˇ®parachuteˇŻ) in the form of senior debt in their company.) Conversely, management could be disfavoured in version 1 by a requirement that they must win more than two-thirds of the votes to retain their jobs.</seg>
<seg id="704">  Finally, the option scheme ensures that absolute priority (goal 3) is met (as long as junior claimants have enough cash to exercise their options; this is discussed further below).</seg>
<seg id="705">  It is worth returning to examples 1 and 2 to see how the AHM procedure compares with a structured bargaining procedure like US Chapter 11.</seg>
<seg id="706">  In example 1 there are two alternatives: liquidate for 90, or keep the company going for an average value of 110.</seg>
<seg id="707">  The big difference between the AHM procedure and structured bargaining is that, if the former creditors as shareholders get to vote, they will choose to keep the company going, since they enjoy all of the potential upside gains from continuation.</seg>
<seg id="708">  Of course, in this instance the former shareholders will be eager to exercise their options, since by spending 1 they obtain a share worth 1.1. (Junior creditors are being ignored.) In other words, the former creditors will get paid their 100 in full by the former shareholders, and the former shareholders, as residual claimants, will vote to maintain the company asa going concern.</seg>
<seg id="709">  A good company has been saved.</seg>
<seg id="710">  In example 2, the alternatives are to liquidate for 90 or keep the company going for an average value of 80.</seg>
<seg id="711">  Here, the former shareholders will not exercise their options, and the former creditorsˇŞas the new shareholdersˇŞwill vote to liquidate and receive 90.</seg>
<seg id="712">  A bad company has been shut down.</seg>
<seg id="713">  Notice that problems1 and 2 have been resolved without eliminating the possibility of reorganization.</seg>
<seg id="714">  In example 1, incumbent managers are able to retain their jobs even though they may not have the cash in hand, and any incentive on the part of the creditors to liquidate the company prematurely is avoided.</seg>
<seg id="715">  In example 2, managers are rightly unable to keep their jobs.</seg>
<seg id="716">  In neither example is there room for haggling.</seg>
<seg id="717">  And in both examples the people who end up voting over the future of the company are the residual claimants (i.e. those who bear the consequences of their actions); as a result, the final outcome is the value-maximizing choice.</seg>
<seg id="718">  Further Considerations</seg>
<seg id="719">  This section briefly raises some additional issues and discusses a number of practical problems that might arise under the AHM procedure.</seg>
<seg id="720">  Treatment of Junior Creditors and Former Shareholders</seg>
<seg id="721">  In the AHM procedure, junior creditors, before they receive anything, are required to buy out senior creditors.</seg>
<seg id="722">  A concern may be that junior creditors do not have the cash on hand to exercise their options and, therefore, will be unduly disadvantaged. (Also, they may be unable or unwilling to raise the necessary funds by borrowing.) There is a modification of the procedure which ameliorates this problem. (The following discussion concerns version 1, but similar arguments apply to version 2.) Once the bids are in, the bankruptcy judge will be able to place a lower bound on the value of the company, equal to the size of the best cash bid, Vc (an objective amount).</seg>
<seg id="723">  Given this, he could proceed as if the firm were worth Vc, and distribute shares accordingly.</seg>
<seg id="724">  If Vc exceeds the amount owed to senior creditors, the junior creditors will receive a fraction of the shares in the initial distribution.</seg>
<seg id="725">  For example, if the senior creditors are owed 100 and the best cash bid that comes in is for 150, then the senior creditors would be issued two-thirds of the shares and the junior creditors would be issued one-third.</seg>
<seg id="726">  Of course, there may be a noncash bid which the junior creditors perceive to be worth more than 150, in which case the senior creditors would still be getting too much; but in that case the junior creditors could exercise their options to buy them out.</seg>
<seg id="727">  Of course, even with this modification, junior creditors might still be short-changed.</seg>
<seg id="728">  The worst case would be that there is no cash bid; here Vc=0, and all the equity is nitially allocated to senior creditors.</seg>
<seg id="729">  How bad are things for the junior creditors in such a case? Arguably not too bad, for at least three reasons.</seg>
<seg id="730">  First, junior creditors do not collectively have to raise the cash to buy out the senior creditors.</seg>
<seg id="731">  Each junior creditor can act as an individual.</seg>
<seg id="732">  The pro rata cash injection may be quite small. (Indeed, an individual need not exercise her options in full; she may choose to exercise only a fraction.) Second, a market for options may well develop during the bankruptcy processˇŞespecially for large firms. (Indeed, the bankruptcy judge might be obliged to establish such a market.) In this case, junior creditors need not come up with cash; they could simply sell their options.</seg>
<seg id="733">  Third, even if some junior creditors are unable to raise the cash, and so are left empty-handed, they will probably fare no worse than they do under current arrangements.200</seg>
<seg id="734">  Finally, it is important to realize that the problem facing junior creditors who wish to raise cash in order to exercise their options is quite different from that of a bidder who wishes to make a cash bid for the whole company.</seg>
<seg id="735">  Because junior creditors act individually, no junior creditor who exercises her option bears much risk; nor does someone who buys the option and exercises it on her behalf.</seg>
<seg id="736">  In contrast, someone who makes a cash bid for the whole company may bear a great deal of risk.</seg>
<seg id="737">  201 Hence, there is no contradiction between supposing on the one hand that capital markets are sufficiently imperfect that noncash bids have a role to play, and supposing on the other hand that junior claimants will be able to obtain a reasonable fraction of the post-bankruptcy pie by exercising or selling their options.</seg>
<seg id="738">  Claims Disputes</seg>
<seg id="739">  The discussion has so far paid little attention to the question of how the amounts and seniorities of creditors' claims are established.</seg>
<seg id="740">  The adjudication process is complex and forms an important part of any bankruptcy procedure, including the above one.</seg>
<seg id="741">  It may be argued that the time scale of three months is too short for the purpose of allocating shares and options.</seg>
<seg id="742">  There is a way of dealing with awkward claims disputes without jeopardizing the procedure, as long as a reasonable proportion of the claims can be established within the three months: take the claims that can be established, allocate shares and options on the basis of these claims alone, carry out the vote, and emerge from bankruptcy with the contentious claims still outstanding.</seg>
<seg id="743">  New Money</seg>
<seg id="744">  Companies typically need new money during the bankruptcy procedure, for example to pay suppliers and workers.</seg>
<seg id="745">  The bankruptcy practitioner or management (with the approval of the judge) can raise such money in the usual way that solvent companies raise money.</seg>
<seg id="746">  The point is that, thanks to the debt¨Cequity conversion, this company is a solvent company (either it has no debts or its only debts are secured)ˇŞand so there is scope to issue new debt, which of course would be junior to any remaining secured debt, but senior to equity.</seg>
<seg id="747">  Voting Procedures</seg>
<seg id="748">  Another issue concerns the voting procedure per se.</seg>
<seg id="749">  If there are only two bids, it seems natural to have a simple vote between them.</seg>
<seg id="750">  However, with more than two bids, there are many possibilities.</seg>
<seg id="751">  Shareholders could cast their votes for their most preferred plan, with the plan that receives the most votes being the winner; or they could rank the plans, with the plan that receives the highest total ranking being chosen; or there could be two rounds, in which shareholders rank the plans in the first round, with a subsequent run-off between the two highest ranked plans in the second round.</seg>
<seg id="752">  One point to note is that thorny issues in voting theory (such as the Condorcet Paradox) are less likely to arise in the present context, given that shareholders have a common objective: value maximization.</seg>
<seg id="753">  Small Companies</seg>
<seg id="754">  The procedure is likely to be most valuable in the case of medium to large companies with multiple creditors, for which bankruptcy raises the thorniest problems.</seg>
<seg id="755">  However, most bankruptcies relate to small companies, for which a bank is typically the single main creditor.</seg>
<seg id="756">  Under the AHM procedure, the bank would get all the equity (presuming that it is not bought out) and could ˇ®voteˇŻ on whether to liquidate or reorganize the company. (It might choose to liquidate if it needs cash and does not feel that it will be able to sell its shares.) In addition, the procedure allows junior creditorsˇŞe.</seg>
<seg id="757">  g. trade creditorsˇŞto buy out the bank; trade creditorsmight have an incentive to vote to keep the company going because they anticipate profitable trade with the company in the future.</seg>
<seg id="758">  In short, the procedure may also have a role to play in the case of small companies.</seg>
<seg id="759">  Workouts.</seg>
<seg id="760">  Many of the problems of bankruptcy plague company workouts.</seg>
<seg id="761">  (There are free-rider and hold-out problems.) There is no reason why companies could not, of their own accord, choose the AHM procedure as a vehicle for facilitating workouts in the event that the procedure isnot adopted by the state.</seg>
<seg id="762">  Concluding Remarks</seg>
<seg id="763">  It is worth summarizing the main point of this chapter.</seg>
<seg id="764">  Current reorganization procedures are flawed because they mix the decision of who should get what with the decision of what should happen to a bankrupt company.</seg>
<seg id="765">  The chapter has described a procedure that separates these two issues.</seg>
<seg id="766">  The key lies in transforming a group of people with different claims (and therefore different objectives) into a homogeneous class of shareholders, who then decide on the company's future by a simple vote.</seg>
<seg id="767">  It may help to say a few more words about the philosophy underlying the procedure.</seg>
<seg id="768">  The basic idea is that a bankrupt company is not fundamentally different from a solvent company that is performing badly.</seg>
<seg id="769">  In the case of a solvent company, shareholders elect a board of directors, who are entrusted with deciding, on a day-to-day basis, whether to keep the company going, sell it, or close it down.</seg>
<seg id="770">  The same menu of options should be available to the claimants of a bankrupt company.</seg>
<seg id="771">  In other words, there is no particular reason why bankruptcy should automatically trigger the termination of a company via a cash sale (either as a going concern or in pieces).</seg>
<seg id="772">  According to the agency cost theory of debt presented in Chapter 6, bankruptcy is an indication that something is wrong with management rather than with the company itself. (Management has failed to live up to a commitment.) The appropriate response is to allow new management teams the opportunity to replace existing management.</seg>
<seg id="773">  The AHM procedure does this through the device of a noncash bid (or a plan).</seg>
<seg id="774">  Noncash bids allow for Chapter 11-type reorganization plans.</seg>
<seg id="775">  However, in contrast to Chapter 11, the company's future isdecided by a simple voteˇŞa procedure that is standard for solvent companiesˇŞrather than by a complex bargaining procedure that is never seen outside bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="776">  An interesting insight into how the procedure might work is provided by the recent (1994) take-over battle for Paramount in the USA.</seg>
<seg id="777">  There were two bidders for ParamountˇŞViacom and QVCˇŞand each put in a bid with a noncash component as well as a cash component.</seg>
<seg id="778">  Paramount shareholders chose between the bidsˇŞand the option of keeping Paramount independentˇŞby what was in effect a vote. (Viacom won the vote.)</seg>
<seg id="779">  Thus, the choice Paramount shareholders were asked to make is analogous to the choice claimants would make in the AHM procedure in the presence of noncash bids.</seg>
<seg id="780">  In closing, it is worth emphasizing a point made earlier.</seg>
<seg id="781">  A good bankruptcy procedure should work well both when capital markets are imperfect and when they are perfect.</seg>
<seg id="782">  The AHM procedure has this feature.</seg>
<seg id="783">  If capital markets are perfect, the company will go to the bidder willing to pay the highest amountˇŞmoreover, this bidder can do no better than offer cashˇŞand thus the outcome will be exactly the same as in US Chapter 7. (Similarly, the bankruptcy practitioner should always propose a cash sale if capital markets are perfect.) For this reason, while believers in perfect capital markets may not see the merit of the AHM procedure relative to US Chapter 7, they should not be strongly opposed to it.</seg>
<seg id="784">  In contrast, those with doubts about the adequacy of capital markets should find value in the procedure.</seg>
<seg id="785">  8 The Structure of Voting Rights in a Public Company.</seg>
<seg id="786">  CHAPTER6 discussed some of the agency problems that arise in a public company when there is a separation of ownership and control (or management).</seg>
<seg id="787">  It was argued that these agency problems are greater than in the case of a closely held company because of a free-rider problem: individual, small shareholders have little incentive to monitor management, given that monitoring is a public good.</seg>
<seg id="788">  204 A similar free-rider problem affects the corporate voting process: it may not be in the interest of an individual shareholder to incur the often considerable expense of launching a proxy fight to replace an incompetent management team, given that the costs are borne by this one shareholder, while the gains from improved management are enjoyed by all shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="789">  205 In fact, shareholders may not even vote (intelligently) in corporate board elections, in view of the fact that an individual, small shareholder's vote is unlikely to be pivotal.</seg>
<seg id="790">  The above free-rider problems can sometimes be overcome by someone who acquires a large stake in a company and takes it over.</seg>
<seg id="791">  207 In practice, the ease with which a take-over can occur depends on a variety of factors, including the range of defensive measures available to management, the attitudes of the courts, the existence of anti-takeover legislation, the ability of a successful bidder to expropriate minority shareholders, etc.</seg>
<seg id="792">  This chapter argues that, in addition to these institutional, legal, and political factors, an important influence on the take-over process is a company's security-voting structureˇŞin particular, how the company's votes are allocated across securities.</seg>
<seg id="793">  Before a company goes public, it is in the interest of the company's initial owner to design the security-voting structure in such a way that future management is subject to an appropriate amount of pressure from the market for corporate control, and to ensure that changes in management occur in the right situations.</seg>
<seg id="794">  It will be shown that, under a reasonable set of assumptions, the optimal security-voting structure is a single class of shares with votes attached (ˇ®one share¨Cone voteˇŻ).</seg>
<seg id="795">  This result may explain why many companies in the USA and UK adopt a one share¨Cone vote rule and also why stock exchanges and regulatory authorities are often suspicious of companies that try to do otherwise.</seg>
<seg id="796">  The basic idea behind the analysis is simple.</seg>
<seg id="797">  Suppose the total value of a company under a particular management team is divided into two parts: one part representing the present value of the dividend stream received by shareholders (call this the company's public value), the other part representing a (monetary) private benefit enjoyed by management (call this the private value).</seg>
<seg id="798">  If a company has several classes of shares with different voting rights, a bidder can obtain controlˇŞthat is, 50 per cent of the votesˇŞby acquiring less than 50 per cent of the company's dividend stream if restricted or partial offers are allowed (that is, if the bidder does not have to offer to buy all the shares in a particular class); or by acquiring less than 100 per cent of the company's dividend stream if restricted offers are not allowed (that is, if the bidder does have to offer to buy all the shares in a particular class).</seg>
<seg id="799">  As a result, a rival management team, which produces low public value for shareholders but has large private value, may be able to seize control from a superior incumbentˇŞi.e. an incumbent with high public value but low private valueˇŞby paying a premium on shares representing a small fraction of the company's dividend stream.</seg>
<seg id="800">  In the same way, an incumbent with low public value for shareholders but large private value may be able to maintain control by outbidding a superior rivalˇŞi.e. a rival with high public value but low private valueˇŞover shares representing a small fraction of the company's dividend stream.</seg>
<seg id="801">  Thus, deviations from one share¨Cone vote create two kindsof ˇ®errorˇŻ: some control transfers take place that should not, and some control transfers do not take place that should.</seg>
<seg id="802">  The risk of both kinds of error is reduced (if restricted offers are allowed) or eliminated (if they are not) by one share¨Cone vote.</seg>
<seg id="803">  Although one share¨Cone vote works well in a wide class of cases, it is not always optimal.</seg>
<seg id="804">  The analysis will show that, if both the incumbent and the rival have significant private benefits, a departure from one share¨Cone vote can increase the intensity of competition in the market for corporate control and enable shareholders to extract some of the incumbent'sand rival's private benefits.</seg>
<seg id="805">  This can raise the total value of the company.</seg>
<seg id="806">  For certain classes of companies there may be another reason why one share¨Cone vote is not optimal.</seg>
<seg id="807">  This chapter operates within the framework of Chapter 6, where management's preferences are ˇ®unimportantˇŻ relative to those of investors and do not receive any weight in the choice of capital structure.</seg>
<seg id="808">  However, in some companies it may be efficient to allocate control rights to managers in order to allow them to enjoy their private benefits, or to motivate them to undertake relationship-specific investments.</seg>
<seg id="809">  Alternatively, the company's initial owner may ˇ®sellˇŻ the private benefits to a large investor, along with voting control, so that the investor can consume the private benefits without risk of expropriation.</seg>
<seg id="810">  If managers and investors are wealth-constrained and cannot afford to purchase a large equity stake (or if they are risk-averse), it may be necessary to depart from one share¨Cone vote to achieve these outcomes.</seg>
<seg id="811">  The AghionˇŞBolton model of Chapter 5 shows that allocating control to an agent who enjoys private benefits may indeed be optimal under certain conditions. (See the comparison between ˇ®E controlˇŻ and ˇ®C controlˇŻ.)</seg>
<seg id="812">  Ineffciencies: An Example</seg>
<seg id="813">  In this section an example is presented to illustrate how a departure from one share¨Cone vote can create inefficiencies.</seg>
<seg id="814">  Throughout the example, and the chapter, it is supposed that the company under consideration has a large number of very small (negligible) shareholders and that each shareholder regards herself (correctly) as too small to affect the outcome of a control contest.</seg>
<seg id="815">  Suppose a company has two classes of shares: class A has a claim to 50 per cent of the dividends and no votes; class B has a claim to 50 per cent of the dividends and all the votes.</seg>
<seg id="816">  Suppose that the company's public value under the incumbent management team is200 (so each class is worth 100), and that there is a rival management team which is less efficient: the public value under the rival is 180 (so each class is worth 90).</seg>
<seg id="817">  However, the rival has a significant private benefit of control, 15 say, whereas the incumbent's private benefit is negligible in comparison.</seg>
<seg id="818">  Clearly, it is undesirable for the rival to obtain control since she reduces total value.</seg>
<seg id="819">  However, the rival can get control by making an unconditional tender offer for all the class B shares at 101, say. (In an unconditional offer, a bidder offers to buy the shares tendered, regardless of whether the bid is successful.) In the absence of a counter-offer, a class B shareholder faces the choice of not tendering, and thus holding on to a claim worth 100 if the rival loses and 90 if she wins, or of tendering to the rival and receiving 101.</seg>
<seg id="820">  Since no shareholder regards herself as pivotal, all shareholders tender and the rival wins.210</seg>
<seg id="821">  The rival makes a capital loss of 11 on the shares she purchases. (She pays 101 for them and their public value is only 90.) However, this capital loss is offset by her private benefit of 15.</seg>
<seg id="822">  Shareholders lose in aggregate since the two classes combined receive only 191ˇŞclass B shareholders are bought out for 101 and class A shareholders are left holding shares worth 90.</seg>
<seg id="823">  Finally, note that the incumbent cannot successfully resist this offer by making a counter-offer, since the most he is prepared to pay for the class B shares is 100. (He has insignificant private benefits.) But with such a counter-offer, class B shareholders will tender to the rival.</seg>
<seg id="824">  Under one share¨Cone vote, the rival cannot get control. (I am ruling out restricted offers.) If class B has all the votes and all the dividends, the rival needs to offer more than 200 to get class B shareholders to tender to her.</seg>
<seg id="825">  But this means that she makes a capital loss of 20 (200 ¨C 180), which exceeds her private benefit.</seg>
<seg id="826">  Moreover, even if her private benefit is more than 20, so that a bid is profitable, shareholders do not lose from the transaction, since they are bought out at a premium on shares representing 100 per cent of the dividend stream.</seg>
<seg id="827">  In this example, a departure from one share¨Cone vote is bad because it allows an inferior rival (with a high private benefit) to get control.</seg>
<seg id="828">  The example can easily be modified to show that a departure from one share¨Cone vote can also prevent a superior rival from obtaining control.</seg>
<seg id="829">  Suppose that the public value under the incumbent is still 200, but the public value under the rival is now 220.</seg>
<seg id="830">  Assume now that the incumbent's private benefit is15, whereas the rival's is negligible in comparison.</seg>
<seg id="831">  Then, under the above dual-class structure, the rival cannot get control, even though the company is worth more under her.</seg>
<seg id="832">  The reason is that the most she is prepared to offer for the class B shares is 110, since this is their public value under the rival's management and she has an insignificant private benefit.</seg>
<seg id="833">  If the rival makes such an offer, however, the incumbent will make a counter-offer at 111, say.</seg>
<seg id="834">  The incumbent will make a capital loss of 11 on the counter-offer, but this capital loss is offset by his private benefit of 15.</seg>
<seg id="835">  The conclusion is that the rival will not bother to bid, and the incumbent will retain control.</seg>
<seg id="836">  Again, this problem doesnot arise under one share¨Cone vote.</seg>
<seg id="837">  If class B has all the dividends and all the votes, the rival is prepared to offer up to 220 to the class B shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="838">  The incumbent cannot resist such a bid since the class B shares are worth only 215 to him.</seg>
<seg id="839">  In the above examples, only one of the management teams had a significant private benefit.</seg>
<seg id="840">  It will be seen below that, if both teams have a significant benefit, a departure from one share¨Cone vote may increase the total value of the company.</seg>
<seg id="841">  The Model</seg>
<seg id="842">  This section presents a general model of the effects of security-voting structure on control transactions.</seg>
<seg id="843">  Consider an initial owner who designs the security and voting structure of a company that he will take public.</seg>
<seg id="844">  The objective of the owner is to maximize the total value of the securities issued; imagine that he plans to retire.211 The owner recognizes that the management team of the company cannot be relied on to run the company well.</seg>
<seg id="845">  In particular, a situation may arise where an outside management team can run the company better, but incumbent management is unwilling to cede control, because of the large private benefitsit obtains from running the company.</seg>
<seg id="846">  212 Given this, the initial owner creates a security-voting structure for the public company that allows(hostile) take-oversto occur in certain states of the world.</seg>
<seg id="847">  The total value of the company under a particular management team (the incumbent or the rival) is divided into two parts: the public value (enjoyed by shareholders) and the private value (enjoyed by the management team).</seg>
<seg id="848">  Denote the public value under the incumbent and the rival by yI and yR, respectively, and the private value under the incumbent and the rival by bI and bR, respectively.213</seg>
<seg id="849">  Private benefits may be realized in several ways.</seg>
<seg id="850">  For example, managers may be able to pay themselves large salaries and obtain large perquisites, thus reducing the amount of profit available for shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="851">  Or it may be the case that part of the value of the company is in the form of a synergy which accrues directly to another company that management owns.</seg>
<seg id="852">  Another possibility is that managers merge the target company with another they own at a price that is( ex post) disadvantageous to minority shareholders (a ˇ®freeze-out mergerˇŻ).</seg>
<seg id="853">  Finally, managers may be able to divert value by buying inputs from other companies they own at inflated prices or selling inputs to other companies they own at deflated prices.</seg>
<seg id="854">  The company goes public at date 0 and at this time the security-voting structure is chosen and the incumbent management team is installed.</seg>
<seg id="855">  At date 1, a single rival team appears.</seg>
<seg id="856">  At date 0, there is uncertainty about the characteristics y, b of both incumbent and rival teams.</seg>
<seg id="857">  Suppose that the uncertainty about y and b is resolved at date 1 and there is symmetric information throughout.</seg>
<seg id="858">  Also, take y and b to be exogenous for each management team; i.e., they do not depend on managerial actions.216 At date 1 either the rival launches a take-over or she does not.</seg>
<seg id="859">  If the rival does launch a take-over, the incumbent must choose whether to resist.</seg>
<seg id="860">  Management is replaced if and only if the rival's bid is successful.217 At date 2 the company is wound up and dividends are paid to shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="861">  All investors are risk-neutral and the interest rate is zero.</seg>
<seg id="862">  The time-line is shown in Figure 8.1.</seg>
<seg id="863">  The Control Contest</seg>
<seg id="864">  Suppose that, to get control, the rival must make a public tender offer.</seg>
<seg id="865">  If the rival makes a bid, the incumbent will choose whether or not to resist it.221 Faced with one or two offers, shareholders decide whether to tender to the rival, to tender to the incumbent, or to hold on to their shares.</seg>
<seg id="866">  At the time of the bid, the characteristics of both bidders are known.</seg>
<seg id="867">  Assume that there is a large number of very small (negligible) shareholders and that no shareholder can affect the outcome of the control contest.222 Asa result of shareholders' tender decisions, the rival will either accumulate more than 50 per cent of the company's votes necessary for her to win control or she won't.223 Thus, the outcome of the control contest is deterministic.224 Assume that shareholders have rational expectations.</seg>
<seg id="868">  In the present context, this means that shareholders predict the winner with certainty.</seg>
<seg id="869">  The assumption of shareholder negligibility combined with the assumption of rational expectations has the important implication that it never pays a bidder to make a losing bid.</seg>
<seg id="870">  This is because a bidder cannot make a capital gain on any shares purchased, since if he offers less than syw for i  class i ( i= A or B), where yw represents the public value under the winning management team ( w= R or I), then individual shareholders in class i can do better by holding on to their shares and receiving their pro rata share of the value syw in the post-takeover company.</seg>
<seg id="871">  Hence the only return a bidder can make is through the private benefit that he receives if he wins.225</seg>
<seg id="872">  Form of Bid</seg>
<seg id="873">  Confine attention to ˇ®any or allˇŻ (that is, unrestricted) tender offers.</seg>
<seg id="874">  That is, suppose that, if a bidder offers to buy any shares in a particular class, the bidder must offer to buy all the shares in that class at the same price. (But the bidder does not have to extend the offer to the other class.)</seg>
<seg id="875">  One justification for this is the idea that the courts may be unsympathetic to restricted offers, regarding them as coercive. (For an example of a coercive restricted offer, see Section 3.)</seg>
<seg id="876">  In any case, this assumption is merely a simplification; in Section 3 it is shown that the main results generalize to the case of restricted offers. (See also Grossman and Hart (1988) and Harris and Raviv (1988, 1989).) It is also supposed that any tender offer is unconditionalˇŞthat is, the bidder agrees to buy shares tendered even if the bid fails.</seg>
<seg id="877">  The analysis would not change in any way, however, if conditional offers were allowed as well.</seg>
<seg id="878">  Given that bidders must make any or all offers, one can conclude that the competition for control will take place over the class B sharesˇŞwhich have more than 50 per cent of the votes.</seg>
<seg id="879">  The point is that, since the rival cannot get control by bidding only for the class A shares, she must bid for (all) the B shares.</seg>
<seg id="880">  There is no point bidding for the A shares in addition, since she cannot make a capital gain on these. (If she could, a (negligible) class A shareholder would do better not to tender to her and make the same capital gain himself.) Equally, if the incumbent resists, he will do so by making a counteroffer for the B shares, since the A shares do not provide him with enough votes to defeat the rival.226</seg>
<seg id="881">  The Results</seg>
<seg id="882">  It is now shown that one share¨Cone vote is optimal when either the incumbent's private benefit or the rival's private benefit is insignificant (or both).</seg>
<seg id="883">  That is, the example in Section 1 generalizes.</seg>
<seg id="884">  However, one share¨Cone vote may not be optimal if the incumbent and rival both have significant private benefits.</seg>
<seg id="885">  The analysis is divided into four cases.</seg>
<seg id="886">  Consider next one share¨Cone vote.</seg>
<seg id="887">  Now the rival will win control by making an offer for all the shares at 301, say.</seg>
<seg id="888">  Shareholders will tender because they are being offered more than the post-takeover value of the company under either the rival or the incumbent.</seg>
<seg id="889">  (If the rival offers less than 300, shareholders who expect the bid to succeed will not tender, preferring to remain as minority shareholders.) Moreover, the incumbent cannot resist this offer since the most he can afford to pay for 100 per cent of the shares is 251, comprising the public value under his management plus his private benefit.</seg>
<seg id="890">  Hence under one share¨Cone vote the rival will win and the value of the company will be 301.</seg>
<seg id="891">  To put it very simply, shareholders benefit when the rival and incumbent compete over products for which they have a similar willingness to pay.</seg>
<seg id="892">  In the example, a class consisting of 50 per cent of the dividends and 100 per cent of the votes qualifies better for this than shares and votes bundled together in the same proportion.</seg>
<seg id="893">  Note, however, that the example is quite fragile.</seg>
<seg id="894">  If the incumbent's private benefit is54 rather than 51, the incumbent will be able to outbid the rival for the votes, and the value of the company will be only 200. (The rival will not bid at all.) In contrast, under one share¨Cone vote, the rival would win and the company would be worth 301.</seg>
<seg id="895">  How important is case 4 relative to the other case? For some kinds of companies, private benefits may be large and case 4 may be significant.</seg>
<seg id="896">  Examples might be newspapers(where the ability to influence public opinion yields significant (non-monetary) private benefits) and sports teams and entertainment companies (where being associated with a winning team or mixing with famous people yields high (non-monetary) private benefits).</seg>
<seg id="897">  However, for many other companies private benefits are likely to be less important.</seg>
<seg id="898">  One reason for this is that corporate law makes it difficult for a controlling party to realize significant (monetary) private benefits.</seg>
<seg id="899">  The corporation's directors have a fiduciary duty to all shareholders, and overt diversion of wealth to the controlling party violates this duty.230 Of course, the courts cannot always be relied on to ensure that the minority is protected, and the level of protection may vary greatly from one country to another or from one legal jurisdiction to another.</seg>
<seg id="900">  Hence, the initial owner who is designing the security-voting structure must take into account the possibility that bR, bI are large.</seg>
<seg id="901">  However, for a reasonably wide class of companies, it may be unlikely that both are large at the same time (and that both are in the form of money, i.e. that both can be used to purchase votes).</seg>
<seg id="902">  For such companies, cases 1¨C3 are the relevant ones. (For a further discussion of this point, see Section 4.)</seg>
<seg id="903">  More Complex Security-Voting Structures</seg>
<seg id="904">  So far the optimal security-voting structure has been analysed under the assumption that all claims to dividends are proportional, i.e. that each security is characterized by a share s of profit.</seg>
<seg id="905">  More general structures are, however, possible.</seg>
<seg id="906">  For example, security i could be entitled to some nonlinear share f( y) of total profit y. Harrisand Raviv (1989) i</seg>
<seg id="907">  show that, if the functions f( y) are restricted to be non-decreasing, then (as long as private benefits are not i  simultaneously large) an optimal security-voting structure consists of (any amount of non-voting) riskless debt and a single class of shares with votes attached.</seg>
<seg id="908">  Since one shareˇŞone vote, with no debt, is a special case of this, one may conclude that (monotonic) nonlinear securities do not add anything.</seg>
<seg id="909">  The intuition behind the riskless debt result is as follows.</seg>
<seg id="910">  A claimant holding riskless debt is unaffected by a control transaction, and so the winner of the control contest imposes no externality on such a claimant. (It is supposed that the rival creates enough public value so that the debt remains riskless.) Therefore an efficient outcome is achieved by giving all the votes to claimants other than the riskless debt-holders.</seg>
<seg id="911">  In fact, if riskless debt-holders are given votes, this can lead to an inefficient outcome, since a bidder may be able to obtain control by buying their votes instead of the votes of the variable claim-holders, who are actually affected by the outcome. (Harris and Raviv (1989) call this the ˇ®no cheap votesˇŻ principle.)</seg>
<seg id="912">  The result that riskless debt-holders should not have votes accords with observation.</seg>
<seg id="913">  However, it is not clear that the analysis can explain why risky debt-holders also typically do not have votes.</seg>
<seg id="914">  Vote Selling</seg>
<seg id="915">  It has been assumed throughout that it is illegal or infeasible for a bidder to unbundle a security-voting structure by making an offer for the votes or proxies f shareholders in which the bidder pays only for the votes and gets none of the dividend claims.</seg>
<seg id="916">  In particular, I have not allowed a bidder to make an offer to shareholders of the following form: in exchange for each share tendered, a shareholder will receive a small amount of cash together with a share of a new company.</seg>
<seg id="917">  The new company, whose assets will consist entirely of the tendered shares and votes of the original company, will pass through all dividends of the original company, but the bidder will maintain voting control of the new company (that is, the bidder will be able to vote the original company's shares).</seg>
<seg id="918">  If offers like this were allowed, it isnot difficult to show that the outcome of a control contest would be as if the company had a dual-class structure in which one class had all the votes and none of the dividends, and the other class had all the dividends and none of the votes.</seg>
<seg id="919">  Thus, if votes can be unbundled from shares, the security-voting structure does not matter.</seg>
<seg id="920">  Note that one shareˇŞone vote is neither better nor worse than any other structure under these conditions.</seg>
<seg id="921">  In practice, it may be hard to unbundle votes from shares.</seg>
<seg id="922">  For example, Easterbrook and Fischel (1983) argue that a public market in votes separated from shares is illegal in the USA.</seg>
<seg id="923">  236 To the extent that this is true, the earlier results apply and one shareˇŞone vote dominates all other security-voting structures.</seg>
<seg id="924">  Conclusions</seg>
<seg id="925">  This chapter has argued that a company's security-voting structure can have an important influence on whether the company will be taken over.</seg>
<seg id="926">  It has been shown that, if a competition between bidders with significant private benefits is unlikely, the optimal choice of security-voting structure is one shareˇŞone vote.</seg>
<seg id="927">  There is a link between the material in this chapter and that in earlier chapters.</seg>
<seg id="928">  Chapter 3 gave some reasons why residual control rights (in the form of votes) and residual income rights (in the form of dividends) should be bundled together.</seg>
<seg id="929">  In that chapter, however, it was supposed that the holder of residual control rights and the holder of residual income rights could bargain costlessly.</seg>
<seg id="930">  The analysis of this chapter provides further justification for the linking of voting rights and income rights, but for the case where large numbers make bargaining impossible and where shareholders face free-rider and collective action problems.</seg>
<seg id="931">  It is interesting to relate the results of this chapter to the empirical evidence on departures from one shareˇŞone vote.</seg>
<seg id="932">  DeAngelo and DeAngelo (1985: 39) identified 78 companies publicly traded on the American Stock Exchange and over the counter that had classes of securities with different voting rights (out of a universe of thousands of companies).237 They found that, in most of the companies that deviated from one shareˇŞone vote, incumbent managers had enough votes so that a change in control was impossible without their approval.</seg>
<seg id="933">  That is, the deviation from one shareˇŞone vote did not correspond to a situation in which widely held securities had different effective voting rights; instead, it corresponded to a situation in which the incumbent had all the effective votes necessary to maintain control.</seg>
<seg id="934">  Moreover, in many of the cases the incumbent represented a family.238</seg>
<seg id="935">  The evidence suggests that the forces responsible for a deviation from one shareˇŞone vote may not be those discussed in case 4 of the model of this chapter, where the incumbent's and rival's private benefits are both significant, so much as those analysed in the AghionˇŞBolton model of Chapter 5.</seg>
<seg id="936">  As noted earlier, this chapter has supposed that management's preferences are ˇ®unimportantˇŻ relative to those of investors and do not receive any weight in the choice of capital structure.</seg>
<seg id="937">  However, if this is not the caseˇŞand family-run companie smay be a leading example where it is notˇŞthen it might be efficient to allocate control rights to managers in order to allow them to enjoy their private benefits, or to motivate them to undertake relationship-specific investments.239 Alternatively, the initial owner might ˇ®sellˇŻ the private benefits to a large investor, along with voting control, so that the investor can consume the private benefits without risk of expropriation.</seg>
<seg id="938">  In fact, if private benefits are reliably significant, it would perhaps be surprising if one of the above scenarios did not arise; that is, it would be surprising if the company remained widely held.</seg>
<seg id="939">  But this means that case 4, where there are no large shareholders and yet where there is a high probability of a competition between bidders with significant private benefits at some future date, is not a leading one.</seg>
<seg id="940">  In future work, it would be desirable to incorporate the entrepreneurial incentives and private benefits of Chapter 5  into the model of this chapter.</seg>
<seg id="941">  It would be interesting to see whether the optimal security-voting structure consists of a single class of shares that are widely held, together with a class of superior voting stock held by insiders.</seg>
<seg id="942">  Such a result would appear to be consistent with the empirical evidence.</seg>
<seg id="943">  Finally, it is useful to relate the results of this chapter to the recent US policy debate on whether a company should be required to adopt one shareˇŞone vote as a condition for listing on various stock exchanges.</seg>
<seg id="944">  The chapter has argued that an initial owner has an incentive to choose a value-maximizing security-voting structure since he bears the full consequences of his actions through the effect on the prices of the company's securities.</seg>
<seg id="945">  It has been shown that one shareˇŞone vote is often, but not always, optimal.</seg>
<seg id="946">  Given this, there seems little reason to put restrictions on the security-voting structures of a new company; such restrictions will simply raise the cost of capital.</seg>
<seg id="947">  However, deviations from one shareˇŞone vote often occur at a later stage in a company's life, when the company's shareholders are dispersed.</seg>
<seg id="948">  At this point, managementˇŞunlike an initial ownerˇŞdoesnot have the right incentive to choose a value-maximizing security-voting structure since the consequences are borne by existing shareholders rather than by management.</seg>
<seg id="949">  There is thus a suspicion that ex post changes may be carried out by management to entrench itself and may be value-reducing.240</seg>
<seg id="950">  Of course, changes in security-voting structure are sometimes justified as new events occur that were not anticipated when the original corporate chapter was written.</seg>
<seg id="951">  Thus, some mechanism for change should probably be in place.</seg>
<seg id="952">  However, in order to reduce the possibility of abuse, it makes sense to put the burden of proof on those who want to make the change.</seg>
<seg id="953">  In particular, there should be protection for shareholders who oppose the transaction.</seg>
<seg id="954">  One possibility is to allow a (relatively small) number of shareholders to veto the transaction.</seg>
<seg id="955">  Another possibility is to give dissenting shareholders appraisal rights; that is, if the change in security-voting structure goes through, a shareholder could ask to be bought out at the ˇ®appraisedˇŻ value of the assets, in the absence of the change.</seg>  
<seg id="956"> To put it very simply, if Fisher is a separate firm, Fisher management can threaten to make both Fisher assets and its own labour unavailable for the uncontracted-for supply increase.</seg>
<seg id="957"> In contrast, if Fisher belongs to GM, Fisher management can only threaten to make its own labour unavailable.</seg>
<seg id="958"> The latter threat will generally be weaker than the former.</seg>
<seg id="959"> Although the status quo point in the contract renegotiation may depend on whether GM and Fisher are one firm rather than two, it does not follow that the outcomes after renegotiation will differ.</seg>
<seg id="960"> In fact, if the benefit to GM of the extra car bodies exceeds the cost to Fisher of supplying them, one would expect the parties to agree that the bodies should be supplied, regardless of the status quo point.</seg>
<seg id="961"> However, the divisions of surplus in the two cases will be very different.</seg>
<seg id="962"> If GM and Fisher are separate, GM may have to pay Fisher a large sum to persuade it to supply the extra bodies.</seg>
<seg id="963"> In contrast, if GM owns Fisher's plant, it may be able to enforce the extra supply at much lower cost since, in this case, Fisher management has much reduced bargaining and threat power.</seg>
<seg id="964"> Anticipating the way surplus is divided, GM will typically be more prepared to invest in machinery that is specifically geared to Fisher bodies if it owns Fisher than if Fisher is independent, since the threat of expropriation is reduced; for example, as noted, GM can always run the Fisher factory itself if Fisher management tries to extract too much surplus.</seg>
<seg id="965"> The incentives for Fisher, however, are quite the opposite.</seg>
<seg id="966"> Fisher management will generally be more willing to come up with cost-saving or quality-enhancing innovations if Fisher is an independent firm, because Fisher management is more likely to see a return on its activities.</seg>
<seg id="967"> If Fisher Body is independent, it can extract some of the surplus from an innovation by incorporating the innovation in bodies it supplies to another car manufacturer.</seg>
<seg id="968"> In contrast, if GM owns Fisher, Fisher management faces partial (or even total) expropriation of the value of an innovation to the extent that GM can deny Fisher management access to the assets necessary to incorporate the innovation (or can even hire a new management team to implement the innovation if the innovation is asset-specific rather than management-specific).</seg>
<seg id="969"> In summary, the benefit of integration isthat the acquiring firm'sincentive to make relationship-specific investments increases since, given that it has more residual control rights, it will receive a greater fraction of the ex post surplus created by such investments.</seg>
<seg id="970"> On the other hand, the cost of integration is that the acquired firm'sincentive to make relationship-specific investments decreases since, given that it has fewer residual control rights, it will receive a smaller fraction of the incremental ex post surplus created by its own investments.</seg>
<seg id="971"> I now present a model that formalizes the above ideas.</seg>
<seg id="972"> Before I do so, it is worth noting that so far the discussion of control changeshasbeen restricted to the impact on top management.</seg>
<seg id="973"> Workers' incentiveswill also be affected by changes in ownership, however.</seg>
<seg id="974"> I postpone a discussion of worker incentives to Chapter 3.</seg>
<seg id="975"> In that chapter I also cover a number of other aspects of the integration decision.</seg>
<seg id="976"> A Formal Model of the Costs and Benefis of Integration</seg>
<seg id="977"> In order to develop a formal model, I now abstract from many of the real-world aspects of an economic relationship involving entities such as General Motors and Fisher Body.</seg>
<seg id="978"> I focus on a highly stylized situation in which there are just two assets, a1 and a2, and two managers operating them, M1 and M2.</seg>
<seg id="979"> Suppose that M2, in combination with a2, supplies a single unit of inputˇŞcalled a widgetˇŞto M1.</seg>
<seg id="980"> M1, in combination with a1, then uses this widget to produce output that is sold on the output market.</seg>
<seg id="981"> The economic relationship lasts for two dates.</seg>
<seg id="982"> Ex ante relationship-specific investments are made at date 0, and the widget is supplied at date 1.</seg>
<seg id="983"> Assume the assets are already in place at date 0, so that the investments are in making these assets more productive.</seg>
<seg id="984"> The parties have symmetric information throughout.</seg>
<seg id="985"> Also, there is no uncertainty about the parties' costs or benefits.</seg>
<seg id="986"> However, there is uncertainty about the type of widget M1 requires.</seg>
<seg id="987"> This uncertainty is resolved at date 1; that is, at this time it becomes clear what the relevant widget type is.</seg>
<seg id="988"> The ex ante uncertainty about widget type makes an effective long-term contract impossible; the point is that specifying the widget price in advance meansnothing, given that the widget type cannot be described.37 Thus, the parties negotiate about the widget type and price at date 1 from scratch.38 I shall supposeˇŞand this assumption will be discussed further in Chapter 4ˇŞthat the parties have rational expectationsabout the recontracting processwhen they make their investmentsat date 0.</seg>
<seg id="989"> In particular, even though the partiescannot write a comprehensive contract, they can make correct calculationsabout the expected return from any action.</seg>
<seg id="990"> Assume that the parties are risk-neutral and have large (unlimited) amounts of initial wealth, so that each party can purchase any asset it is efficient for her to own.</seg>
<seg id="991"> For simplicity, take the interest rate to be zero.</seg>
<seg id="992"> I suppose that it is too costly for the parties to specify particular uses of assets a1 and a2 in a date 0 contract.</seg>
<seg id="993"> Thus, whoever owns asset a1 or a2 has not just residual rights of control, but all control rights over the asset.</seg>
<seg id="994"> In other words, the owner can use the asset in any way she wants.</seg>
<seg id="995"> Investments and Payoffs</seg>
<seg id="996"> To conclude thischapter, I would like to consider whether the theory'spredictionsmatch up with the actual organizational arrangementsobserved.</seg>
<seg id="997"> Unfortunately, there hasto date been no formal testing of the property rights approach, and so in what follows I do not attempt to go beyond what is impressionistic.</seg>
<seg id="998"> One very simple implication of the theory is that, ceteris paribus, a party is more likely to own an asset if he or she has an important investment decision (where the investment decision might represent figuring out how to make the asset more productive or looking after the asset).</seg>
<seg id="999"> As an example of this, consider the fact that it isusually thought efficient for someone to own the house they live in or the car they drive (as long as they can afford to; wealth constraints are not part of the present story, but see Chapter 5).</seg>
<seg id="1000"> Presumably the reason is that the person with most influence on the house's or car's value is the user; giving anybody else ownership or control rights in the asset would dilute the incentives of the user, with no compensating gains since this other person has no effect on asset value.</seg>
<seg id="1001"> Another stylized fact consistent with this implication of the theory is that lower-level employees in an organization usually do not have significant ownership or control rights in the organization.</seg>
<seg id="1002"> Arguably the reason is that lower-level employeescarry out (relatively) routine tasks.54 Motivating such employees by giving them ownership rights may not therefore achieve very much in termsof increased productivity. (It isasif Rˇä or Cˇä ssmall in the above model.)</seg>
<seg id="1003"> It makes more sense to allocate the scarce ownership rights so as to motivate key higher-ups, whose actions (usually) have a greater effect on company value (Proposition 2(B)) or whose human capital is very important (Proposition 2(E)).</seg>
<seg id="1004"> A second implication of the theory is that highly complementary assets should be under common ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1005"> There are many instances of this in practice (some trivial): a window of a house and the house itself are usually owned together; so are a lock and a key, the engine of a truck and its chassis; a list of clients' names and the list of their addresses.56 There are also less obvious and more significant examples.</seg>
<seg id="1006"> Joskow (1985) has investigated the ownership arrangementsgoverning electricity generating plants that site next to coal mines.</seg>
<seg id="1007"> Such assets are highly complementary, and not surprisingly he findsa high incidence of common ownership in the form of vertical integration.</seg>
<seg id="1008"> Stuckey (1983) has investigated the case of aluminium refineries that site next to bauxite mines. In this situation, the degree of complementarity is arguably even greater, since, not only are the two entities located next to each other, but also the refinery installs equipment that is specific to the particular bauxite mine.</seg>
<seg id="1009"> Stuckey finds that vertical integration occurs in essentially every case.</seg>
<seg id="1010"> Related to the idea that complementary assets should be owned together is the idea that increasing returns to scale should lead to the formation of large firms.</seg>
<seg id="1011"> Under increasing returns to scale, one large asset is more productive than two assets of half the size.</seg>
<seg id="1012"> In a first-best world one large asset would be chosen, although, as was argued in Chapter 1, it isnot clear that one can interpret thisoutcome ascorresponding to a firm.</seg>
<seg id="1013"> In a second-best world the choice of the large asset is less clear, since moving from two assets to one may have undesirable incentive consequences. (M2 may invest less if she loses ownership rights.) However, if the technological returns to scale are strong enoughˇŞa limiting case is where the smaller assets are completely unproductiveˇŞthen the large asset outcome will be second-best optimal.</seg>
<seg id="1014"> Since, according to Proposition 2(D), it makes no sense to allocate ownership of different parts of a single asset to different people, one can interpret this outcome as corresponding to a single, large firm.</seg>
<seg id="1015"> Just as the theory predicts that complementaritiesˇŞotherwise known as synergiesˇŞmake integration more likely, so it predicts the opposite: independent assets should be separately owned.</seg>
<seg id="1016"> This advice is not always heeded by companies considering acquisitionsˇŞconsider for example the conglomerate merger wave of the 1960s in the USA and the UKˇŞbut, at least in the 1990s, it seems to command considerable support.</seg>
<seg id="1017"> And, of course, there are so many instances of independent assets under separate ownership that this might be said to be the leading case empirically.</seg>
<seg id="1018"> An interesting application of the idea that independent assets should be separately owned is the case of a standard competitive spot market where there are many buyers trading with many sellers.</seg>
<seg id="1019"> It is usually thought that non-integration isan efficient arrangement in such a market.</seg>
<seg id="1020"> The theory supports this.</seg>
<seg id="1021"> Consider a typical non-integrated buyer M1 and a typical non-integrated seller M2.</seg>
<seg id="1022"> For example, M1 might be Oxford University and M2 might be Apple Computers, which, say, supplies Oxford with computers. If Oxford and Apple fail to reach agreement about the price and quality of computers, each can fairly easily switch to another partner.</seg>
<seg id="1023"> The simple model of this chapter may also help us to understand changes in organizational structure over time.</seg>
<seg id="1024"> A number of commentatorshave argued that a trend toward de-integration hasoccurred in the 1980sand 1990s.</seg>
<seg id="1025"> This trend is often traced to the fact that the large factories of the past are being replaced by smaller-scale, more flexible technologies, causing a reduction in lock-in effects; and to the fact that, because of advances in information technology, agents who were previously engaged in routine tasks need to be motivated to make wise decisions on the basis of the increasing amount of information at their disposal.</seg>
<seg id="1026"> The model in Section 2 can explain why the above factorscould be responsible for a trend toward de-integration.</seg>
<seg id="1027"> The increased flexibility in technology means that assets are becoming less complementary. (An asset can more easily be modified to be suitable for a new trading partner.) The importance of individual initiative meansthat M1'sand M2'sinvestment decisionsare both significant.</seg>
<seg id="1028"> According to Proposition 2(B) and (C), both forces suggest that non-integration is more likely to be optimal than before (on this, see also Brynjolfsson 1994).</seg>
<seg id="1029"> It might be thought that another explanation for de-integration isthat advancesin information technology have reduced the costs of writing contracts.</seg>
<seg id="1030"> However, the theory developed here does not predict that non-integration is more likely the lower are contracting costs.</seg>
<seg id="1031"> I argued in Chapter 1 that in a zero-transaction-cost world organizational form doesnot matter, i.e. that non-integration and integration are equally efficient.</seg>
<seg id="1032"> On the other hand, thischapter has considered the case where transaction costs are so large that long-term contracts cannot be written at all.</seg>
<seg id="1033"> It has been shown that under these conditions non-integration and integration can both be optimal depending on the circumstances.</seg>
<seg id="1034"> It follows that a simple monotonic relationship between transaction costs and integration does not exist.61</seg>
<seg id="1035"> Finally, it isworth returning to the traditional U-shaped average cost diagram in Figure 1.1.</seg>
<seg id="1036"> I argued that it ishard for neoclassical theory to justify this figure.</seg>
<seg id="1037"> The property rights approach may have an easier time.</seg>
<seg id="1038"> At small scales of production, a firm uses assets that are highly complementary (two machines located next to each other or several rooms of a building), or assets that exhibit increasing returns to scale, and Proposition 2(D) tells us that hold-up problems would be increased if these assets were not under common ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1039"> In other words, initially the average cost of carrying out activities within the firm isdeclining.</seg>
<seg id="1040"> However, when the firm extendsbeyond a certain point, synergies between assets may fall.</seg>
<seg id="1041"> In particular, the manager and assets at the centre will become less important with regard to operations at the periphery, in the sense that investments at the periphery are unlikely to be specific either to the manager or to the assets at the centre. (In a large firm, the centre and the periphery may for many purposes operate almost independently.) In addition, investments at the centre are unlikely to be enhanced very much by the existence of the periphery.</seg>
<seg id="1042"> According to Proposition 2(C), the average cost of carrying out activities within the firm starts to rise.</seg>
<seg id="1043"> In a rough sense, then, the theory developed in this chapter is consistent with the story about firm size found in every standard microeconomics textbook.</seg>
<seg id="1044"> THIS chapter touches on a number of issues omitted from the simple property rights approach presented in Chapter 2. These include the nature of authority, worker incentives, delegation, the role of reputation, investment in physical capital, and information dissemination within firms.</seg>
<seg id="1045"> The Role of Nonhuman Assets and the Nature of Authority</seg>
<seg id="1046"> The two crucial featuresof the theory described in Chapter 2 are (i) that contractsare incomplete and (ii) that there are some significant nonhuman assets in the economic relationship.</seg>
<seg id="1047"> I have already explained in Chapter 1 why contractual incompleteness is important.</seg>
<seg id="1048"> I now provide some justification for the idea that (at least some) nonhuman assets are an essential feature of a theory of the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1049"> These might be ˇ®hardˇŻ assets such as machines, inventories, and buildings, or ˇ®softerˇŻ assets such as patents, client lists, files, existing contracts, or the firm'sname or reputation.</seg>
<seg id="1050"> To understand better the role of nonhuman assets, consider a situation where ˇ®firmˇŻ 1 acquires ˇ®firmˇŻ 2, which consists entirely of human capital.</seg>
<seg id="1051"> Ask the following question: what is to stop firm 2'sworkersfrom quitting, possibly en masse?</seg>
<seg id="1052"> In the absence of any physical assetsˇŞe.g. buildingsˇŞfirm 2'sworkerswould not even have to relocate physically.</seg>
<seg id="1053"> For example, if they are linked by telephone or computer terminal (assets which they own themselves), they could simply announce one morning that they have become a new firm.</seg>
<seg id="1054"> For firm 1's acquisition of firm 2 to make any economic sense, there must be some source of firm 2 value over and above the workers' human capital, i.e. some ˇ®glueˇŻ holding firm 2's workers in place.</seg>
<seg id="1055"> This source of value may consist of aslittle asa place to meet; the firm'sname, reputation, or distribution network;64 the firm's files, containing important information about itsoperationsor itscustomers;65 or a contract that prohibits firm 2'sworkersfrom working for competitorsor from taking existing clientswith them when they quit.66 The source of value may even just represent the difficulty firm 2'sworkersface in co-ordinating a move to another firm.</seg>
<seg id="1056"> But without something holding the firm together, the firm isjust a phantom.</seg>
<seg id="1057"> A firm's nonhuman assets, then, simply represent the glue that keeps the firm together, whatever thismay be.</seg>
<seg id="1058"> The concept of nonhuman assets is also helpful in clarifying the notion of authority.</seg>
<seg id="1059"> Ronald Coase, in his pioneering 1937 article, argued that the distinguishing feature of the employer¨Cemployee relationship is that an employer can tell an employee what to do, whereasone independent contractor must bribe another independent contractor to do what he or she wants.</seg>
<seg id="1060"> Herbert Simon made a similar observation in his 1951 article.</seg>
<seg id="1061"> However, as Alchian and Demsetz (1972) point out, it isunclear what isthe source of an employer'sauthority over an employee.</seg>
<seg id="1062"> It istrue that an employer can tell an employee what to do, but it isalso true that one independent contractor can tell another independent contractor what to do.</seg>
<seg id="1063"> The interesting question is why the employee pays attention whereas the independent contractor (perhaps) does not.</seg>
<seg id="1064"> When nonhuman assets are present, it is not difficult to understand the difference between the employer¨Cemployee situation and the independent contractor situation.</seg>
<seg id="1065"> In the former case, if the relationship breaks down, the employer walks away with all the nonhuman assets, whereas in the latter case each independent contractor walks away with some nonhuman assets.</seg>
<seg id="1066"> This difference gives the employer leverage.</seg>
<seg id="1067"> Individual i ismore likely to do what individual j wants, if j can exclude i from assets that i needsto be productive than if i can take these assets with her, or than if someone elseˇŞ k, sayˇŞowns these assets (in which case i islikely to do what k wants).</seg>
<seg id="1068"> In other words, control over nonhuman assets leads to control over human assets.</seg>
<seg id="1069"> This is discussed further in Section 2 below.</seg>
<seg id="1070"> The above views on the role of nonhuman assets can be summarized as follows.</seg>
<seg id="1071"> On the one hand, in many firmsˇŞeven those without obvious ˇ®physicalˇŻ equipmentˇŞsome significant nonhuman assets can be identified.</seg>
<seg id="1072"> On the other hand, if such assets do not exist, then it is not clear what keeps the firm together, or what definesauthority within the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1073"> One would expect firms without at least some significant nonhuman assets to be flimsy and unstable entities, constantly subject to the possibility of break-up or dissolution. My impression is that the (casual) evidence isnot inconsistent with thisview.</seg>
<seg id="1074"> Worker Incentives</seg>
<seg id="1075"> The model of Chapter 2 focused on how ownership affects the incentives of top managers.</seg>
<seg id="1076"> However, workers' incentives will also be affected by changesin ownership and thiscan influence the costs and benefitsof integration.</seg>
<seg id="1077"> The main lesson to be drawn from the example isthat changesin control affect the incentives of those who do not have control rightseither before or after the change.</seg>
<seg id="1078"> When M1 hasessential human capital it isbetter for M1 to own asset a2 since this reduces hold-up problems: W has to bargain only with M1 to realize the income Y asopposed to having to bargain with M1 and M2.</seg>
<seg id="1079"> The opposite is the case if M2 has essential human capital.</seg>
<seg id="1080"> The example thus confirms the thrust of Proposition 2(E), that assets should reside in the hands of those with important human capital.</seg>
<seg id="1081"> In fact, the example strengthens Proposition 2(E) in the sense that it shows that an additional benefit of concentrating ownership of assets in the hands of those with important human capital is that worker incentives will be improved.</seg>
<seg id="1082"> The example can easily be extended to illustrate how worker incentives are affected by a merger of firmswith complementary, or independent, assets.</seg>
<seg id="1083"> Suppose that M1 owns asset a1.</seg>
<seg id="1084"> Then, if a1 and a2 are strictly complementary, it is as if M1 has essential human capital. (He is a critical player because he owns a1.) Therefore, W's incentive to invest isstronger if M1 ownsa2 aswell asa1, than if a2 and a1 are separately owned.</seg>
<seg id="1085"> On the other hand, if a1 and a2 are independent and M1'shuman capital isirrelevant, while M2'sissignificant, then W's incentive to invest is stronger if M2 owns a2. (The reason is that W has to bargain only with M2 about the return from his investment, rather than with M1 and M2.</seg>
<seg id="1086"> For details, see Hart and Moore (1990).)</seg>
<seg id="1087"> Thus, the example also confirms the thrust of Proposition 2(D), that highly complementary assets should be owned together.</seg>
<seg id="1088"> In fact, the example again strengthens Proposition 2(D), in the sense that it shows that an additional benefit of concentrating ownership of highly complementary assets is that worker incentives are improved.</seg>
<seg id="1089"> Finally, the example can be used to shed some light on the discussion about authority in Section 1.</seg>
<seg id="1090"> If M1's human capital is essential, one can interpret W's investment as being specific to M1, in the sense that M1 is necessary for the investment to pay off.</seg>
<seg id="1091"> The example shows that the identity of W's employer matters: W is more likely to undertake an M1-specific investment if M1 isW'semployer (M1 ownsthe asset a2 that W workswith) than if M2 isW'semployer.</seg>
<seg id="1092"> In other words, the example shows how control over nonhuman assets can lead to authority over human assets, in that employees will tend to carry out actions or investments that are of interest to their boss.</seg>
<seg id="1093"> Delegation and Other Forms of Intermediate Ownership</seg>
<seg id="1094"> The theory developed in Chapter 2 applies most directly to closely held or owner-managed firms, in the sense that it has been supposed that those who own an asset actually exercise residual control rightsover the asset.</seg>
<seg id="1095"> In reality, the ownersof a companyˇŞparticularly a large oneˇŞoften cannot run the company themselves on a day-to-day basis and so they delegate power to a board of directors and to managers.</seg>
<seg id="1096"> This delegation createspotential conflicts of interest, which will be the subject of Chapters 6¨C8.</seg>
<seg id="1097"> In particular, managersmay have their own reasons for merging, which have little to do with profit: for example, they may wish to build a bigger empire.</seg>
<seg id="1098"> Thus, some mergers may be carried out that do not increase value (for the companies concerned).</seg>
<seg id="1099"> Even though there are important differences between the owner-managed and large company cases, the main insights of the property rights approach continue to be relevant.</seg>
<seg id="1100"> It is still useful to think of a large company as consisting of nonhuman assets.</seg>
<seg id="1101"> It is also helpful to analyses a merger in terms of a change in residual control rights.</seg>
<seg id="1102"> Prior to a merger, some residual control rights are delegated to the board of the acquiring company and some to the board of the company that isto be acquired; after the merger, all the residual control rightsare delegated (in the first instance) to the board of the acquiring company.</seg>
<seg id="1103"> An interesting topic for further research is to extend the model of Chapter 2 to the case of public companies.</seg>
<seg id="1104"> Just as the shareholders of a public company delegate power to the board of directors and managers, so top managers delegate power to subordinates, who delegate power to their subordinates, and so on down the line.</seg>
<seg id="1105"> It is hard to make sense of this process in a comprehensive contracting world, because even the concept of authority is difficult to define.</seg>
<seg id="1106"> What does it mean to put someone ˇ®in chargeˇŻ of an action or decision if all actions can be specified in a contract?</seg>
<seg id="1107"> Thus, the incomplete contracting framework is a natural one for studying authority.</seg>
<seg id="1108"> An interesting recent paper that analyses authority from an incomplete contracting perspective is Aghion and Tirole (1995).</seg>
<seg id="1109"> These authors make a distinction between real authority and formal (legal) authority.</seg>
<seg id="1110"> They argue that someone with superior information may have effective power, even though he does not have legal power, because those with legal powerˇŞownersˇŞmay follow his suggestions.</seg>
<seg id="1111"> In fact, it may be in the interest of ownersto create an asymmetry of information, so that subordinates can wield power, and can thereby be rewarded for making relationship-specific investments.</seg>
<seg id="1112"> The cost of such an arrangement is that the subordinates will sometimes take actions that are not in the interest of the owner.</seg>
<seg id="1113"> Aghion and Tirole use these ideas to develop a theory of information acquisition and the optimal span of control within the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1114"> Residual Control versus Residual Income</seg>
<seg id="1115"> I have emphasized the connection between ownership and residual control rights.</seg>
<seg id="1116"> However, it is often argued that a key characteristic of ownership is that the owner of an asset is entitled to the residual income from that asset.</seg>
<seg id="1117"> I now consider the relationship between these two concepts.</seg>
<seg id="1118"> A problem that one faces when discussing the notion of residual income is that in many contexts it is not well defined.</seg>
<seg id="1119"> For example, if two partiesenter into a profit-sharing agreement, whereby party A receives log(¦Đ+1) and party B receives ¦Đ?log(¦Đ+1), where ¦Đ istotal profit, then who isthe residual income (or profit) claimant? The answer is that both partiesare.</seg>
<seg id="1120"> Given that profit-sharing contracts are not in principle costly to write if profitsare verifiable (and it is unclear how residual income is to be allocated if profitsare not verifiable), the conclusion is that residual income may not be a very robust or interesting theoretical concept. (For more on this, see Hart 1988.)</seg>
<seg id="1121"> In practice, complicated profit-sharing agreements are often not written, and some party (or set of parties) can be identified as the residual claimant for a particular asset.</seg>
<seg id="1122"> The question then arises, will this party also hold the residual control rights?</seg>
<seg id="1123"> The answer is, not necessarily: residual income and residual control do not have to be bundled together on a one-to-one basis.</seg>
<seg id="1124"> One example is given by publicly traded companies that have several classes of shares with different voting rights (so the ratio of income rights to control rights varies across investors)ˇŞsuch an arrangement is rare in the USA and UK but common in many other countries.</seg>
<seg id="1125"> A second example is given by joint ventures, where it sometimes happens that two parties share control 50 : 50 but have differential profit shares.</seg>
<seg id="1126"> A third example is given by any situation where a manager or worker is on a profit-based incentive or bonus scheme: the employee is in some sense a residual claimant, but may not have any voting rights.</seg>
<seg id="1127"> The above examples are somewhat special.</seg>
<seg id="1128"> I now present some theoretical arguments as to why in many cases one might expect the holder of residual control rights to have significant residual income rights, that is, why residual control and residual income should often go together.</seg>
<seg id="1129"> First, if residual income and residual control are separated, then this creates a hold-up problem.</seg>
<seg id="1130"> Assume that M1 has the control rights for asset a1 and M2 has most of the income rights (where income is verifiable). Then M1 will have little incentive to develop an idea for a profitable new use of a1 since most of the gains go to M2.</seg>
<seg id="1131"> Similarly, M2 has little incentive to develop an idea for operating a1 more efficiently since she must negotiate with M1 for the right to implement the idea.</seg>
<seg id="1132"> To put it simply, control rights and income rights are highly complementary and so, by Proposition 2(D), it makes sense to allocate them to the same person.</seg>
<seg id="1133"> Second, in some situations it may not be possible to measure (or verify) all aspects of an asset's return stream.</seg>
<seg id="1134"> For example, suppose return is composed of two parts: ˇ®short-termˇŻ income from current activitiesand ˇ®long-termˇŻ income, that is, changes in the asset's value.</seg>
<seg id="1135"> Arguably, (most of) the latter will accrue to whomever has control rights, possibly because this person can decide when or even whether to sell the asset.</seg>
<seg id="1136"> Now suppose the former returnˇŞshort-term incomeˇŞisallocated to a worker who operatesthe asset, aspart of a ˇ®high-poweredˇŻ incentive contract. (The worker doesnot have control rights.) Then the worker will maximize current output, without regard to the asset's long-term value; for example, he might not repair the asset, he might misuse it, etc. This may lead to a very inefficient outcome.</seg>
<seg id="1137"> It might be better to give the worker low-powered incentives, that is, to bundle the short-term income rightswith the control rights.</seg>
<seg id="1138"> Thisway, even though the worker may not work very hard, at least he will be prepared to balance his time between activities that increase current output and those that increase the resale value of the asset.</seg>
<seg id="1139"> Third, in some cases it may not even be feasible to separate residual income and residual control rights.</seg>
<seg id="1140"> Consider a widget producer, A, who entersinto an ex ante contract with B, whereby A agreesto hand over all the future profit from widget production to B. Then, whatever the contract says, A may be able to use his ex post power asthe owner to direct much of the profit from the widgets to himself.</seg>
<seg id="1141"> One way for A to do this is to refuse to sell any widgets until B agrees to give up a significant part of her profit share.</seg>
<seg id="1142"> Another possibility is for A to sell the widgets for a nominal amount in return for purchasers compensating A in some other way; for example, prospective buyers could be required to buy something else from A at an inflated price.</seg>
<seg id="1143"> In both cases, the outcome is that A receives much of the profit from widget sales by virtue of his possession of the residual control rights.</seg>
<seg id="1144"> Chapter 8 will provide another reason why residual income and residual control rights should be bundled together.</seg>
<seg id="1145"> It will be shown that unbundling residual income rights from residual control rights can lead to inefficienciesin the market for corporate control.</seg>
<seg id="1146"> In particular, unbundlingˇŞthat is, a departure from one share¨Cone voteˇŞmay allow a management team with high private benefitsbut low total value to win a take-over contest against a management team that haslow private benefits, but that creates higher value for shareholders overall.</seg>
<seg id="1147"> The above results can be used to understand an idea briefly mentioned in Chapter 1.</seg>
<seg id="1148"> I noted there that it isoften assumedˇŞparticularly in the industrial organization literatureˇŞthat profit-sharing is easier within a single firm than between independent firms.</seg>
<seg id="1149"> I observed that it is hard to make sense of this idea in a comprehensive contracting world, since the independent firmscould alwayswrite a profit-sharing contract.</seg>
<seg id="1150"> However, in an incomplete contracting world, those who possess residual rights of control may end up receiving the firm'sprofit, given their power to direct profit to themselves.</seg>
<seg id="1151"> As a result, if firm A buys firm B the owner-manager of A may receive both A and B'sprofit; that is, profit-sharing may occur.</seg>
<seg id="1152"> In contrast, when the firmsare separate, B'sprofit may be diverted to the owner-manager of B; that is, whatever any profit-sharing agreement may say, profit-sharing will not actually occur.</seg>
<seg id="1153"> The Effects of Reputation</seg>
<seg id="1154"> Nothing so far has been said about the effects of reputation.</seg>
<seg id="1155"> This is restrictive since it is often argued that reputation can overcome many of the problemsof opportunism identified in thisbook.</seg>
<seg id="1156"> For example, if one takesthe static model of Chapter 2 and repeatsit infinitely often, then the first-best can be achieved if the interest rate is not too high.</seg>
<seg id="1157"> One way to do thisisfor the partiesto agree in advance to co-operate, with thisoutcome being sustained by the threat that if one party deviates the other party will refuse to trade with it any more.</seg>
<seg id="1158"> There are two reasons why reputation has been put to one side, in spite of its obvious importance.</seg>
<seg id="1159"> First, as discussed in Chapter 1, in a first-best world firms don't matter at all, since the optimum can be sustained under any organizational form.</seg>
<seg id="1160"> Thus, to develop a theory of the firm, one must analyse a situation where the first-best cannot be guaranteed, i.e. where reputational forces are not strong enough to eliminate all problems of opportunism.</seg>
<seg id="1161"> As a first step, therefore, it does not seem unreasonable to consider the static model of Chapter 2, where reputation plays no role at all.</seg>
<seg id="1162"> Second, it isnot clear thatˇŞeven in an infinitely repeated relationshipˇŞreputation will eliminate all the problemsof opportunism that have been described.</seg>
<seg id="1163"> In some cases, although the trade is repeated, it is reasonable to suppose that the parties' relationship-specific investments are made only once.</seg>
<seg id="1164"> However, this means that the gains from breaching a co-operative agreement are sizeable even if a breach marks the end of the relationship.</seg>
<seg id="1165"> The point is that the deviating party getsa huge one-time gain from expropriating the other party'sspecific investment.</seg>
<seg id="1166"> Thus, it may be the case that the repeated version of the model exhibits problems of opportunism similar to those of the static model.</seg>
<seg id="1167"> In spite of these comments, I believe that the general issue of the influence of reputation on organizational form isa fascinating one.</seg>
<seg id="1168"> An interesting recent analysis is Halonen (1994) (see also Garvey 1991).</seg>
<seg id="1169"> Halonen considers the infinitely repeated version of the static model of Chapter 2, where the relationship-specific investment is made each period.</seg>
<seg id="1170"> She finds that for one range of parameters repetition makes joint ownership of assets optimal. (Joint ownership is never optimal in the static model of Chapter 2.) The reason is that joint ownership, by maximizing hold-up problems, minimizes the parties' payoffs if co-operation breaks down and therefore makes it easier to sustain cooperation.</seg>
<seg id="1171"> In contrast, for a second range of parameters she findsthat the basic resultsof the static model apply; that is, either non-integration or (some type of) integration is optimal.</seg>
<seg id="1172"> However, integration is less likely to be optimal in a repeated relationship than in the static case.</seg>
<seg id="1173"> Investments in Physical Capital</seg>
<seg id="1174"> The model of Chapter 2 supposes that all investments are investments in human capital.</seg>
<seg id="1175"> What this means is that, if M1 invests i, thiscreatesa return from operating a1 and a2 of R( i), but only if M1 isa party to the transaction.</seg>
<seg id="1176"> In effect, M1 is acquiring an asset-specific skill that is not transferable to others in his absence.</seg>
<seg id="1177"> In practice, the return from many investments is embodied, at least partially, in a piece of physical capital rather than in the human capital of the person making the investment.</seg>
<seg id="1178"> The main ideas of the property rights approach generalize to the case where investments are embodied in physical capital, but some of the details change.</seg>
<seg id="1179"> In particular, it is no longer clear that strictly complementary assets should be owned together or that joint ownership is never optimal.</seg>
<seg id="1180"> It will be useful to illustrate this point.</seg>
<seg id="1181"> Suppose there is a single asset a* and two agents M1 and M2 who can each increase the asset's value.</seg>
<seg id="1182"> For simplicity, take the investments to be discrete: M1 can make an investment that costs i and increases the value of a* by R; and M2 can make an investment that costs ? and increases the value of a* by . (Asin Chapter 2, increases in value and investment expenditures are observable but not verifiable.)</seg>
<seg id="1183"> Assume that each investment is embodied entirely in the physical capital, i.e. that once the investment is made neither M1 nor M2 is necessary to realize the value increase.</seg>
<seg id="1184"> Suppose also that RŁľi, so that both investments would be made in a first-best world.</seg>
<seg id="1185"> Consider a second-best world where M1 owns a* (that is, M1 enjoys the full value of the asset a*).</seg>
<seg id="1186"> Then M1 will certainly invest since he receives the full increase in value R for himself.</seg>
<seg id="1187"> However, M2 will not invest since any increase in the value of the asset goes to M1 and not to her. (Once M2 has made her investment M1 does not need M2 any more.) The opposite istrue if M2 ownsa*: now M2 investsbut M1 does not.</seg>
<seg id="1188"> Joint ownership may be an improvement here.</seg>
<seg id="1189"> Under joint ownershipˇŞwhere M1 and M2 each have veto power over the use of the assetˇŞany increase in value will be shared 50 : 50 under Nash bargaining (since agreement by both is necessary to realize the increase in value, e.g. through a sale).</seg>
<seg id="1190"> Therefore, M1 and M2 will both invest if and only if If R and are large, the above inequalitieswill be satisfied and so joint ownership will yield a more efficient outcome than single ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1191"> Similar examples can easily be constructed to show that strictly complementary assets should sometimes be separately owned.</seg>
<seg id="1192"> However, a word of caution isappropriate.There are other ways than joint ownership to provide agentswith incentives to invest in physical capital.</seg>
<seg id="1193"> In the above example the same outcome could be achieved with a stochastic ownership scheme, whereby M1 and M2 each own a* with probability 1/2.</seg>
<seg id="1194"> (Also, instead of separating ownership of complementary assets, one could allocate joint or stochastic ownership in an overall asset consisting of the complementary assets combined.) At this stage little is known in general about the relative advantages of joint ownership and stochastic ownership when there are many assets and many agents.</seg>
<seg id="1195"> Thus, it is not clear that the above discussion provides an explanation for joint ownership in practice (e.g. the fact that joint ventures are often owned 50:50 by the companies setting them up).</seg>
<seg id="1196"> Introduction</seg>
<seg id="1197"> Economists have a very well-established theory of market trading and are on the way to possessing a similarly well-developed theory of contractual transactions.</seg>
<seg id="1198"> The economic analysis of institutions, however, is in a much more rudimentary state.</seg>
<seg id="1199"> This book provides a framework for thinking about firms and other kinds of economic institutions.</seg>
<seg id="1200"> The basic idea is that firms arise in situations where people cannot write good contracts and where the allocation of power or control is therefore important.</seg>
<seg id="1201"> The book is divided into two parts.</seg>
<seg id="1202"> Part I is concerned with the boundaries of firms, Part II with the financial structure of firms.</seg>
<seg id="1203"> In this Introduction I want to sketch out some of the main themes.</seg>
<seg id="1204"> It is useful to start with a true story.</seg>
<seg id="1205"> Recently my wife and I entered into negotiations to buy a piece of land from a contractor, on which the contractor would build us a house.</seg>
<seg id="1206"> Our first impression of the contractor was favourable; we hope that he felt the same way about us.</seg>
<seg id="1207"> None the less, it was clear as we talked about how to structure the deal that each of us had legitimate concerns.</seg>
<seg id="1208"> To mention a few of them, my wife and I were worried that we might spend a lot of money and end up with a house we did not like, or that the house might not be finished on time, or that the contractor might quit in the middle of the project and we would have to hire someone else to complete the house.</seg>
<seg id="1209"> On the other hand, the contractor was worried that he would build a house designed according to our wishes and we would then not pay for it, or that we would dismiss the contractor in the middle of the project and hire someone else, or that we would choose very expensive kitchen and bathroom fixtures which would raise his costs substantially.</seg>
<seg id="1210"> In an ideal world, there would be a simple way to deal with many of these concerns.</seg>
<seg id="1211"> The three of us would write a binding contract that laid down each person's obligations in every conceivable eventuality and imposed large penalties if anybody failed to live up to them.</seg>
<seg id="1212"> For example, the contract would provide a description of the house down to the bathroom taps and light fixtures.</seg>
<seg id="1213"> It would specify how the price of the house would be altered if my wife and I made any changesˇŞthere would be one price for an extra bathroom, another for an extra fitted wardrobe, etc. There would be provisions for how the price of the house would change if the cost of raw materials rose during construction.</seg>
<seg id="1214"> A completion date would be specified, but extra days would be allowed if there were an unusually severe winter or if the foreman fell ill.</seg>
<seg id="1215"> And so on.</seg>
<seg id="1216"> Unfortunately, it is impossible to write a contract as detailed as this; it is simply too difficult to anticipate all the many thingsthat may happen.</seg>
<seg id="1217"> The contract we will write (at thispoint we have not yet agreed on a final contract!) will specify many aspects of the house, but many contingencies will be left out.</seg>
<seg id="1218"> As the contractor admitted rather disarmingly to us, he is learning how to write contracts as he goes along.</seg>
<seg id="1219"> The contract he wrote with his last client had many missing provisions; he hopes this one will have fewer.</seg>
<seg id="1220"> Given that we will write an incomplete contract, it is clear that revisions and renegotiations will take place.</seg>
<seg id="1221"> In fact, the contract is best seen as providing a suitable backdrop or starting point for such renegotiations rather than specifying the final outcome.</seg>
<seg id="1222"> Thus, in thinking about the contract, my wife and IˇŞand I believe the contractor tooˇŞhave found it useful to imagine worst-case scenarios.</seg>
<seg id="1223"> We are all looking for a contract that will ensure that, whatever happens, each side has some protection, both against opportunistic behaviour by the other party and against bad luck.</seg>
<seg id="1224"> For example, here are two contractsthat I do not think the three of uswill sign:</seg>
<seg id="1225"> Contract 1:</seg>
<seg id="1226"> My wife and I pay for the house and the land in advance.</seg>
<seg id="1227"> The contractor is then obliged to complete the house.</seg>
<seg id="1228"> He receives no further payment.</seg>
<seg id="1229"> We pay nothing in advance.</seg>
<seg id="1230"> However, we become the owner of the land immediately.</seg>
<seg id="1231"> We pay for the house and the land when the house is completed.</seg>
<seg id="1232"> It is pretty clear why these contracts are unattractive.</seg>
<seg id="1233"> With the first one, my wife and I would be concerned that the contractor would disappear after we had paid him the money for the house and land, and we would be left holding only the landˇŞor that, instead of literally disappearing, the contractor might work extremely slowly.</seg>
<seg id="1234"> With the second one, the contractor would be concerned that we would exclude him from the property that we had just become the owners of, and hire someone else to build the house. (In justification, we might claim that he was doing bad work.)</seg>
<seg id="1235"> Instead of these ˇ®extremeˇŻ contracts, it seems clear that we will end up writing something closer to the following contract (which is similar to contracts the contractor has used before):</seg>
<seg id="1236"> We pay some amount initially which corresponds roughly to the value of the land.</seg>
<seg id="1237"> We then become the owners of the land.</seg>
<seg id="1238"> After this we make a series of specified advances to the builder, which are timed to occur when certain parts of the house are finished. (For example, the contractor receives 20 per cent of the price of the house when the foundation is completed, 10 per cent when the chimney is built, and so on.) A final payment is made some time after the house is completed.</seg>
<seg id="1239"> The advantage of this kind of contract is that each side has some protection against worst-case scenarios; or, to put it another way, neither side can take too much advantage of the other.</seg>
<seg id="1240"> If the contractor disappears at any point, we could complete the project without incurring too much additional cost since we have paid an amount roughly commensurate with what we have received from him.</seg>
<seg id="1241"> On the other hand, if we sack the contractor at some point, he will not suffer too much since he has been paid for the work he has done.</seg>
<seg id="1242"> The house story is of course just one example of an economic relationship, but it has two features that I believe are very general.</seg>
<seg id="1243"> The first is that contracts are incomplete.</seg>
<seg id="1244"> The second is that, because of this, the ex post allocation of power (or control) matters.</seg>
<seg id="1245"> Here power refers roughly to the position of each party if the other party does not perform (e.g. if the other party behaves opportunistically).</seg>
<seg id="1246"> It was observed in the house example that contract 3 succeeded in sharing power reasonably between the contractor and my wife and me. In contrast, contract 1 gave the contractor too much power and contract 2 gave my wife and me too much power.</seg>
<seg id="1247"> In this book, I will argue that these two ideasˇŞcontractual incompleteness and powerˇŞcan be used to understand a number of economic institutions and arrangements.</seg>
<seg id="1248"> Before I develop this theme a little further, it is worth pointing out that power is not a standard feature of economic theory.</seg>
<seg id="1249"> For example, take the frameworks that economists use to analyse the behaviour of economic agents: general equilibrium theory, game theory, mechanism design or principal-agent theory, and transaction cost theory.</seg>
<seg id="1250"> In general equilibrium theory, it is supposed that trade takes place through anonymous competitive markets and that every agent abides by the terms of any transaction he or she enters into.</seg>
<seg id="1251"> In such a setting, power is irrelevant.</seg>
<seg id="1252"> In game theory, agents may have market power, i.e. the ability to affect price.</seg>
<seg id="1253"> However, market power is not the same as the notion of power used in this book.</seg>
<seg id="1254"> Market power captures the idea that the contractor can charge my wife and me a lot for our house because there are not many competing contractors around; it says nothing about how we allocate power within our relationship.</seg>
<seg id="1255"> In mechanism design or principal¨Cagent theory, it is supposed that it is costless to write a contract.</seg>
<seg id="1256"> An implication is that an optimal contract will be ˇ®comprehensiveˇŻ, in the sense that, like the idealized house contract, it will lay down each person's obligations in every conceivable eventuality and impose large penalties if anybody fails to live up to them.</seg>
<seg id="1257"> But here again power is irrelevant, since an optimal comprehensive contract will not be breached or renegotiated.</seg>
<seg id="1258"> Transaction cost theory comes closest to the framework presented here.</seg>
<seg id="1259"> However, although transaction cost theory puts a lot of emphasis on the costs of writing contracts, and the consequent contractual incompleteness, less attention is paid to the idea that power is important or that institutional arrangements are designed to allocate power among agents.</seg>
<seg id="1260"> In the remainder of this introduction, I will try to impart a flavour of how contractual incompleteness and power can be used to understand some important economic phenomena.</seg>
<seg id="1261"> I will touch on the contents of each chapter except for Chapter 1, which surveys the literature, and Chapter 4, which discusses the foundations of the theory of incomplete contracts.</seg>
<seg id="1262"> The Meaning of Ownership</seg>
<seg id="1263"> Economists have written a great deal about why property rights are important, and in particular why it matters whether a machine, say, is privately owned or is common property.</seg>
<seg id="1264"> However, they have been less successful in explaining why it matters who owns a piece of private property.</seg>
<seg id="1265"> To understand the difficulty, consider a situation where I want to use a machine initially owned by you.</seg>
<seg id="1266"> One possibility is for me to buy the machine from you; another possibility is for me to rent the machine from you.</seg>
<seg id="1267"> If contracting costsare zero, we can sign a rental agreement that isaseffective asa change in ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1268"> In particular, the rental contract can specify exactly what I can do with the machine, when I can have access to it, what happens if the machine breaks down, what rights you have to use the machine, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="1269"> Given this, however, it is unclear why changes in asset ownership ever need to take place.</seg>
<seg id="1270"> In a world of contracting costs, however, renting and owning are no longer the same.</seg>
<seg id="1271"> If contracts are incomplete, not all the uses of the machine will be specified in all possible eventualities.</seg>
<seg id="1272"> The question then arises: who chooses the unspecified uses? A reasonable view is that the owner of the machine has this right; that is, the owner has residual rights of control over the machine, or residual powers.</seg>
<seg id="1273"> For example, if the machine breaks down or requiresmodification and the contract issilent about this, the owner can decide how and when it isto be repaired or modified.</seg>
<seg id="1274"> It is now possible to understand why it might make sense for me to buy the machine from you rather than to rent it.</seg>
<seg id="1275"> If I own the machine, I will have more power in our economic relationship since I will have all the residual rights of control.</seg>
<seg id="1276"> To put it another way, if the machine breaksdown or needsto be modified, I can ensure that it is repaired or modified quickly, so that I can continue to use it productively.</seg>
<seg id="1277"> Knowing this, I will have a greater incentive to look after the machine, to learn to operate it, to acquire other machines that create a synergy with this machine, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="1278"> Chapters 2 and 3 develop a formal theory of asset ownership based on these ideas.</seg>
<seg id="1279"> The Boundaries of Firms</seg>
<seg id="1280"> A long-standing issue in organization theory concerns the determinants of the boundaries of firms.</seg>
<seg id="1281"> Why does it matter if a particular transaction is carried out inside a firm or through the market or via a long-term contract? To put it another way, given any two firmsA and B, what difference doesit make if the firms transact through an arms-length contract or merge and become a single firm?</seg>
<seg id="1282"> It has proved to be difficult to answer these questions using standard theory for the same reason that it is hard to explain why asset ownership matters.</seg>
<seg id="1283"> If contracting costs are zero, firms A and B can write a contract governing their relationship that specifiesthe obligationsof all partiesin all eventualities.</seg>
<seg id="1284"> Since the contract isall-inclusive, it isunclear what further aspects of their relationship could be controlled through a merger.</seg>
<seg id="1285"> This is true whether firmsA and B are in a vertical relationshipˇŞfirm A isbuying an input from firm BˇŞor in a horizontal or lateral relationship, e.g. where firms A and B sell complementary products and want to save on some duplicative production costs.</seg>
<seg id="1286"> Once one recognizesthat contractsare incomplete, however, it is possible to explain why a merger might be desirable.</seg>
<seg id="1287"> Consider the well-known example of Fisher Body, which for many years has supplied car bodies to General Motors.</seg>
<seg id="1288"> For a long time Fisher Body and GM were separate firms linked by a long-term contract.</seg>
<seg id="1289"> However, in the 1920s GM's demand for car bodies increased substantially.</seg>
<seg id="1290"> After Fisher Body refused to revise the formula for determining price, GM bought Fisher out.</seg>
<seg id="1291"> Why did GM and Fisher Body not simply write a better contract?</seg>
<seg id="1292"> Arguably, GM recognized that, however good a contract it wrote with Fisher Body, situations similar to the one it had just experienced might arise again; that is, contingencies might occur that no contract could allow for.</seg>
<seg id="1293"> GM wanted to be sure that next time around it would be in a stronger bargaining position; in particular, it would be able to insist on extra supplies, without having to pay a great deal for them.</seg>
<seg id="1294"> It is reasonable to suppose that ownership of Fisher Body would provide GM with this extra power by giving it residual control rights over Fisher Body's assets.</seg>
<seg id="1295"> At an extreme, GM could dismiss the managers of Fisher Body if they refused to accede to GM's requests.</seg>
<seg id="1296"> Of course, although the acquisition increased GM's power and made GM more secure in its relationship with Fisher Body, it arguably had the opposite effect on Fisher Body.</seg>
<seg id="1297"> That is, Fisher Body may have had more to worry about since the merger.</seg>
<seg id="1298"> For example, if Fisher Body's costs fall, GM is now in a stronger position to force a reduction in the (transfer) price of car bodies, hence reducing the return to Fisher managers.</seg>
<seg id="1299"> Anticipating this, Fisher managers may have less incentive to figure out how to reduce costs.</seg>
<seg id="1300"> Thus, there are both costs and benefitsfrom a merger.</seg>
<seg id="1301"> Chapters 2 and 3 develop a theory of the firm based on the idea that firm boundariesare chosen to allocate power optimally among the various parties to a transaction.</seg>
<seg id="1302"> I argue that power is a scarce resource that should never be wasted.</seg>
<seg id="1303"> One implication of the theory is that a merger between firms with highly complementary assets is value-enhancing, and a merger between firms with independent assets is value-reducing.</seg>
<seg id="1304"> The reason is as follows.</seg>
<seg id="1305"> If two highly complementary firmshave different owners, then neither owner has real power since neither can do anything without the other.</seg>
<seg id="1306"> It is then better to give all the power to one of the owners through a merger.</seg>
<seg id="1307"> On the other hand, if two firmswith independent assetsmerge, then the acquiring firm'sowner gainslittle useful power, since the acquired firm'sassetsdo not enhance hisactivities, but the acquired firm's owner loses useful power, since she no longer has authority over the assets she works with.</seg>
<seg id="1308"> In this case, it is better to divide the power between the ownersby keeping the firmsseparate.</seg>
<seg id="1309"> Financial Securities</seg>
<seg id="1310"> Debt</seg>
<seg id="1311"> Suppose you have an interesting idea for a business venture, but do not have the capital to finance it.</seg>
<seg id="1312"> You go to a bank to get a loan.</seg>
<seg id="1313"> In deciding whether to finance the project, the bank is very likely to consider not only the return stream from the project, but also the resale value of any assets you have or will acquire using the bank's funds; in other words, the bank will be interested in the potential collateral for the loan.</seg>
<seg id="1314"> In addition, the durability of your assets and how quickly the returnscome in are likely to determine the maturity structure of the loan.</seg>
<seg id="1315"> The bank will be more willing to lend long-term if the loan is supported by assets such as property or machines than if it is supported by inventory, and if the returnsarrive in the distant future rather than right away.</seg>
<seg id="1316"> These observations fit in well with the ideas emphasized in this book.</seg>
<seg id="1317"> Like the parties in the house transaction, the bank wants some protection against worst-case scenarios.</seg>
<seg id="1318"> If there is very little collateral underlying the loan, then the bank will worry that you will use its money unwisely or, in an extreme case, disappear with the money altogether.</seg>
<seg id="1319"> Similarly, if the collateral depreciates rapidly or the returns come in quickly, then the bank would be unhappy with a long-term loan since it would have little protection against your behaving opportunistically when the collateral was no longer worth much or after the project returnshad been realized (and ˇ®consumedˇŻ).</seg>
<seg id="1320"> Basically, the bank wants to ensure a rough balance between the value of the debt outstanding and the value remaining in the project, including the value of the collateral, at all times. (Similar considerations made contract 3 in the house example attractive: neither party wasin significant deficit or surplus with respect to the other party at any point in the transaction.) Chapter 5 builds a model of debt finance based on these ideas, and derives results about the kinds of project that will be financed.</seg>
<seg id="1321"> Equity</seg>
<seg id="1322"> Investors who finance business ventures sometimes take equity in the venture rather than debt.</seg>
<seg id="1323"> Equity, unlike debt, doesnot have a fixed set of repayments associated with it, with nonpayment triggering default.</seg>
<seg id="1324"> Rather, equity-holders receive dividendsif and when the firm chooses to pay them.</seg>
<seg id="1325"> This puts equity-holders potentially at the mercy of those running the firm, who may choose to use the firm'sprofits to pay salaries or to reinvest rather than to pay out dividends.</seg>
<seg id="1326"> Thus, equity-holders need some protection.</seg>
<seg id="1327"> Typically, they get it in the form of votes.</seg>
<seg id="1328"> If things become bad enough, equity-holdershave the power to remove those running the firm (the board of directors) and replace them with someone else.</seg>
<seg id="1329"> However, giving outside equity-holdersvoting power bringscostsaswell asbenefits.</seg>
<seg id="1330"> Equity-holders can use their power to take actions that ignore the (valid) interests of insiders.</seg>
<seg id="1331"> For example, they might close down an established, family-run business or force the business to terminate long-standing employees.</seg>
<seg id="1332"> The optimal allocation of power between insiders and outsiders is another of the topics covered in Chapter 5.</seg>
<seg id="1333"> Dispersed Power</seg>
<seg id="1334"> So far I have supposed that those with power wield it.</seg>
<seg id="1335"> That is, I have assumed that an owner will exercise her residual control rights over assets; e.g. an equity-holder will use her votes to replace a bad manager.</seg>
<seg id="1336"> However, if power is held by many people, then no one of them may have an incentive to be active in exercising this power.</seg>
<seg id="1337"> It is then important that there exist automatic mechanisms that will achieve what those with power are unable or unwilling to do by themselves.</seg>
<seg id="1338"> A leading example of dispersed power is the case of a public company with many, small shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="1339"> Shareholders cannot run the company themselves on a day-to-day basis and so they delegate power to a board of directors and to managers.</seg>
<seg id="1340"> This creates a free-rider problem: an individual shareholder does not have an incentive to monitor management, since the gains from improved management are enjoyed by all shareholders, whereas the costs are borne only by those who are active.</seg>
<seg id="1341"> Because of this free-rider problem, the managers of a public company have a fairly free hand to pursue their own goals: these might include empire-building or the enjoyment of perquisites.</seg>
<seg id="1342"> Chapters 6 and 8 explore two ˇ®automaticˇŻ mechanisms that can improve the performance of management: debt (in combination with bankruptcy) and take-overs.</seg>
<seg id="1343"> Debt imposes a hard budget constraint on managers.</seg>
<seg id="1344"> If a company has a significant amount of debt, management is faced with a simple choice: reduce slackˇŞthat is, cut back on empire-building and perquisitesˇŞor go bankrupt.</seg>
<seg id="1345"> If there isa significant chance that managerswill lose their jobsin bankruptcy, they are likely to choose the first option.</seg>
<seg id="1346"> Take-oversprovide a potential way to overcome collective action problemsamong shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="1347"> If a company isbadly managed, then there is an incentive for someone to acquire a large stake in the company, improve performance, and make a gain on the shares or votes purchased.</seg>
<seg id="1348"> The threat of such action can persuade management to act in the interest of shareholders.</seg>
<seg id="1349"> I derive some implications of these views of debt and take-overs.</seg>
<seg id="1350"> Chapter 6 shows that the view of debt as a constraining mechanism can explain the types of debt a company issues (how senior the debt is, whether it can be postponed).</seg>
<seg id="1351"> Chapter 8 shows that the possibility of take-overs can explain why many companies bundle votes and dividend claimstogetherˇŞthat is, why they adopt a one share¨Cone vote rule.</seg>
<seg id="1352"> One share¨Cone vote protectsshareholder property rights in the sense that it maximizes the chance that a control contest will be won by a management team that provideshigh value for shareholders, rather than high private benefitsfor itself.</seg>
<seg id="1353"> Of course, if a company takeson debt, then there isalwaysthe chance that it will go bankrupt.</seg>
<seg id="1354"> If contracting costs were zero, there would be no need for a formal bankruptcy procedure because every contract would specify what should happen if some party could not meet its debt obligations.</seg>
<seg id="1355"> In a world of incomplete contracts, however, there is a role for bankruptcy procedure.</seg>
<seg id="1356"> In Chapter 7 I argue that a bankruptcy procedure should have two main goals.</seg>
<seg id="1357"> The first is that a bankrupt company's assets should be placed in their highest-value use.</seg>
<seg id="1358"> The second is that bankruptcy should be accompanied by a loss of power for management, so as to ensure that management has the right incentive to avoid bankruptcy.</seg>
<seg id="1359"> Chapter 7 suggests a procedure that meets these goals, and at the same time avoids some of the inefficienciesof existing US and UK procedures.</seg>
<seg id="1360"> An Omitted Topic: Public Ownership</seg>
<seg id="1361"> The book is concerned with the optimal allocation of privately owned assets.</seg>
<seg id="1362"> A very important topic not considered concerns the optimal balance between public and private ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1363"> Which assets should be publicly owned and which should be privately owned? This issue has always been a central one in the economic and political debate, but it has attracted new attention in the last few years as major industries have been privatized in the West and the socialist regimes in Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union have dissolved.</seg>
<seg id="1364"> It is natural to analyse public choice versus private choice using the ideas of incomplete contracts and power.</seg>
<seg id="1365"> If contracting costs are zero, there is no difference between the optimal regulation of a private firm on the one hand, and nationalization or public ownership on the other.</seg>
<seg id="1366"> In both cases the government will write a ˇ®comprehensiveˇŻ contract with the firm or itsmanagersthat will anticipate all future contingencies.</seg>
<seg id="1367"> The contract will specify the manager's compensation scheme, how the price of the firm'soutput should change if costsfall, how the nature of the firm's product should change if there is a technological innovation or a shift in demand, etc.</seg>
<seg id="1368"> In contrast, in a world of incomplete contracts, public and private ownership are different, since in one case the government has residual control rightsover the firm's assets, while in the other case a private owner does. The public¨Cprivate case is not a simple extension of the pure private property rights model, however.</seg>
<seg id="1369"> At least two new questions arise.</seg>
<seg id="1370"> First, what isthe government'sobjective function? Much existing work viewsthe government asa monolith, but thisis unsatisfactory since, even more than in the case of a corporation, the government represents a collection of agents with conflicting goals: civil servants, politicians, and the citizens themselves.</seg>
<seg id="1371"> Second, what ensures that the government respects an agreed-on allocation of property rights? The government, unlike a private agent, can always change its mind: it can nationalize assets it has privatized or privatize assets it has nationalized.</seg>
<seg id="1372"> There is a small, but growing, literature that analyses public versus private ownership in incomplete contracting terms.</seg>
<seg id="1373"> However, much remains to be done.</seg>
<seg id="1374"> Developing a satisfactory theory, which deals among other things with the issue of the government'sobjective function and itscommitment to property rights, isa challenging but fascinating task for future research.</seg>
<seg id="1375"> Part I Understanding Firms</seg>
<seg id="1376"> The first part of the book, Chapters 1¨C4, isconcerned with the nature and extent of the firm, that is, with the determinantsof the boundariesof firms in a market economy.</seg>
<seg id="1377"> Chapter 1 contains a discussion of existing theories of the firm, including the neoclassical, principal¨Cagent, and transaction cost theories.</seg>
<seg id="1378"> While these theories have proved very useful for some purposes, I shall argue that they cannot by themselves explain the boundaries of firms (or the internal organization of firms).</seg>
<seg id="1379"> Chapters 2 and 3 describe the more recent incomplete contracting or ˇ®property rightsˇŻ approach, which can throw some light on firm boundaries.</seg>
<seg id="1380"> This theory can also explain the meaning and importance of asset ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1381"> Finally, Chapter 4 provides a discussion of the foundations of the incomplete contracting model used in Part I and, to some extent, throughout the book.</seg>
<seg id="1382"> 1 Established Theories of the Firm</seg>
<seg id="1383"> This chapter discusses some of the ways in which economists have looked at firms.</seg>
<seg id="1384"> I begin with the neoclassical theory of the firm, the standard approach found in all textbooks.</seg>
<seg id="1385"> I then move on to the principal¨Cagent and transaction cost theories.</seg>
<seg id="1386"> Neoclassical Theory</seg>
<seg id="1387"> Neoclassical theory, which has been developed over the last one hundred years or so, views the firm mainly in technological terms.</seg>
<seg id="1388"> The U-shape of the average cost curve is justified asfollows.</seg>
<seg id="1389"> There are some fixed costs of production (plant, machines, buildings) that must be incurred whatever the level of output.</seg>
<seg id="1390"> As output increases, variable costs increase, but fixed costs do not.</seg>
<seg id="1391"> Thus, there is a tendency for per-unit costs to fall.</seg>
<seg id="1392"> However, after a certain point further expansion becomes difficult because some inputs cannot be varied easily with the firm's scale.</seg>
<seg id="1393"> One of these is managerial talent.</seg>
<seg id="1394"> Asoutput rises, the manager eventually becomesoverloaded and hisproductivity falls.</seg>
<seg id="1395"> Asa consequence, the firm'saverage cost beginsto rise.</seg>
<seg id="1396"> How should one assess this theory of the firm? On the positive side, the theory is surely right to stress the role of technology in general, and returns to scale in particular, as important determinants of the size of firms(see e.g. Chandler 1990: 26¨C8).</seg>
<seg id="1397"> In addition, the theory has been very useful for analysing how the firm'soptimal production choice varies with input and output prices, for understanding the aggregate behaviour of an industry, and for studying the consequences of strategic interaction between firms once the assumption of perfect competition has been dropped (see e.g. Tirole 1988).</seg>
<seg id="1398"> At the same time, the theory has several serious weaknesses.</seg>
<seg id="1399"> First, it completely ignores incentive problems within the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1400"> The firm istreated asa perfectly efficient ˇ®black boxˇŻ, inside which everything operates perfectly smoothly and everybody doeswhat they are told.</seg>
<seg id="1401"> Even a cursory glance at any actual firm suggests that this is unrealistic.</seg>
<seg id="1402"> Second, the theory hasnothing to say about the internal organization of firmsˇŞtheir hierarchical structure, how decisions are delegated, who has authority.</seg>
<seg id="1403"> Third, and related, the theory does not satisfactorily pin down the bound-aries of the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1404"> Among other things, it is not clear why managerial talent is a fixed factor: why can't the managerial diseconomies that lie behind the upward-sloping portion of the average cost curve be avoided through the hiring of a second manager?</seg>
<seg id="1405"> It is useful to dwell on this last point since it lies at the heart of the first part of this book.</seg>
<seg id="1406"> Neoclassical theory is as much a theory of division or plant size as of firm size.</seg>
<seg id="1407"> This line of reasoning suggests that it is not enough to argue that a firm will not expand because its manager has special skills and additional managers are inferior.</seg>
<seg id="1408"> The real issue is why it makes sense for the additional managers to be employed outside this firm, rather than within a division or subsidiary operated by this firm.</seg>
<seg id="1409"> In other words, given the original firm and a second firm employing an alternative manager, why doesn't the first firm expandˇŞpossibly laterallyˇŞby merging with the second firm?</seg>
<seg id="1410"> To put it in stark terms (suggested originally by Coase 1937), neoclassical theory is consistent with there being one huge firm in the world, with every existing firm (General Electric, Exxon, Unilever, British Petroleum, . . . ) being a division of this firm.</seg>
<seg id="1411"> It is also consistent with every plant and division of an existing firm becoming a separate and independent firm.</seg>
<seg id="1412"> To distinguish between these possibilities, it is necessary to introduce factors not present in the neoclassical story.</seg>
<seg id="1413"> The Agency View</seg>
<seg id="1414"> As noted, neoclassical theory ignores all incentive problems within the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1415"> Over the last twenty years or so, a branch of the literatureˇŞprincipal¨Cagent theoryˇŞhasdeveloped which triesto rectify this.</seg>
<seg id="1416"> I shall argue that principal¨Cagent theory leadsto a richer and more realistic portrayal of firms but that it leaves unresolved the basic issue of the determinantsof firm boundaries.</seg>
<seg id="1417"> There is now a vast literature that analyses the form of the optimal incentive scheme under the above circumstances.</seg>
<seg id="1418"> Moreover, the basic principal¨Cagent problem described has been extended in a number of directions.</seg>
<seg id="1419"> Among other things, agency theorists have allowed for: repeated relationships, several agents, several principals, several dimensions of action for the agent, career concernsand reputation effects, and so on.</seg>
<seg id="1420"> Transaction Cost Theories</seg>
<seg id="1421"> The Distinction Between Comprehensive and Incomplete Contracts</seg>
<seg id="1422"> One important factor missing from the principal¨Cagent view isthe recognition that writing a (good) contract isitself costly.</seg>
<seg id="1423"> This is a theme that lies at the heart of the large transaction cost literature which started with Ronald Coase's famous 1937 paper and has been extensively developed by Oliver Williamson and others (see in particular Williamson 1975, 1985, and Klein et al. 1978).</seg>
<seg id="1424"> It is worth noting first that agency theory itself already incorporates some contracting costs.</seg>
<seg id="1425"> In the simple model considered above, it is supposed that managerial effort, e, cannot be made part of an enforceable contract since it is observed only by the agent.</seg>
<seg id="1426"> Another way of saying this is that the cost of putting e into the incentive contract isinfinite.</seg>
<seg id="1427"> However, thisisprobably not a very useful way of looking at things.</seg>
<seg id="1428"> Agency theory ascribesall contracting coststo the cost of observing variables.</seg>
<seg id="1429"> If a variable is observable by both parties, then the theory assumes that it can be contracted on costlessly.</seg>
<seg id="1430"> But this is not the same as supposing that it is costly to write a contract.</seg>
<seg id="1431"> This point can be made a little more sharply as follows.</seg>
<seg id="1432"> Although the optimal contract in a standard principal¨Cagent model will not be first-best (since it cannot be conditioned directly on variables like effort that are observed by only one party), it will be ˇ®comprehensiveˇŻ in the sense that it will specify all parties' obligations in all future states of the world, to the fullest extent possible.</seg>
<seg id="1433"> As a result, there will never be a need for the parties to revise or renegotiate the contract as the future unfolds. The reason is that, if the parties ever changed or added a contract clause, this change or addition could have been anticipated and built into the original contract.</seg>
<seg id="1434"> ESTABLISHED THEORIES OF THE FIRM</seg>
<seg id="1435"> In reality, contracts are not comprehensive and are revised and renegotiated all the time.</seg>
<seg id="1436"> According to the transaction cost literature, this is a consequence of three factors missing from the standard principal¨Cagent story.</seg>
<seg id="1437"> First, in a complex and highly unpredictable world, it ishard for people to think very far ahead and to plan for all the various contingenciesthat may arise.</seg>
<seg id="1438"> Second, even if individual planscan be made, it ishard for the contracting partiesto negotiate about these plans, not least because they have to find a common language to describe states of the world and actionswith respect to which prior experience may not provide much of a guide.</seg>
<seg id="1439"> Third, even if the partiescan plan and negotiate about the future, it may be very difficult for them to write their plansdown in such a way that, in the event of a dispute, an outside authorityˇŞa court, sayˇŞcan figure out what these plans mean and enforce them.</seg>
<seg id="1440"> In other words, the parties must be able to communicate not only with each other, but also with outsides who may have little knowledge about the environment in which the contracting partiesoperate.</seg>
<seg id="1441"> Asa result of these three contracting costs, the partieswill write a contract that isincomplete. That is, the contract will contain gaps and missing provisions.</seg>
<seg id="1442"> In particular, it will be silent about the parties' obligations in some states of the world and will specify these obligations only coarsely or ambiguously in other states of the world.</seg>
<seg id="1443"> For example, the contract might not specify what is to happen if the supplier's factory burns down, because this is not anticipated; or the contract might say that the supplier must always supply one widget, rather than a number of widgets that varies with the state of the world, because it is too costly to distinguish between different states of the world.</seg>
<seg id="1444"> The contract might also be short-term; that is, it might specify the parties' obligations only up to some date, T.</seg>
<seg id="1445"> It is useful to illustrate these points with the General Motors¨CFisher Body example from the Introduction.</seg>
<seg id="1446"> In a constant world, GM and Fisher might find it easy to write a long-term contract specifying the quantity, quality, and price of the car bodiesFisher suppliesto GM.</seg>
<seg id="1447"> For example, it might be optimal for them to agree that Fisher should supply 2,000 bodies, of a particular type, each day at a particular price for the foreseeable future.</seg>
<seg id="1448"> Now consider the case where the world is changing.</seg>
<seg id="1449"> The optimal number of car bodies, type of car bodies, and price may depend on a variety of factors, e.g. the demand for General Motors' output, Fisher Body's costs, actions of competitors, new regulationson car pollution, whether a trade agreement isreached with the Japanese, and innovations occurring in car and body production.</seg>
<seg id="1450"> It may be prohibitively expensive to write a contract that conditions quantity, quality, and price on all of the external factors just described.</seg>
<seg id="1451"> This is not just because some of the variables are privately observed, but also because, even if publicly observable, the variables are inherently hard to specify in advance in an unambiguous manner.</seg>
<seg id="1452"> For example, there may be no objective way of measuring the demand for cars, or the degree of innovation, or the extent of government regulation, or the actionsof competitors.</seg>
<seg id="1453"> Thus, a contract that triesto condition variableson these factors may not be enforceable by a court.</seg>
<seg id="1454"> In addition, even if it is possible for the parties to anticipate and contract on some of the factorsthat may be relevant for their relationship, there may be many othersthat the partiesdo not anticipate; for example, they may foresee the possibility of a new trade agreement, but not of new regulations on car pollution.</seg>
<seg id="1455"> Under these conditions, the parties are likely to write an incomplete contract.</seg>
<seg id="1456"> For example, the contract might be short-term.</seg>
<seg id="1457"> GM and Fisher may be able to look five yearsahead, but not much further.</seg>
<seg id="1458"> So they might write a contract specifying that Fisher should supply 2,000 bodies per day, of a particular type, at a particular price for the next five years.</seg>
<seg id="1459"> Both parties realize that towards the end of the five-year period they will have further common information about demand, costs, competitors' strategies, regulations, etc., and that they can then write a new contract for another five years; and so on.</seg>
<seg id="1460"> The Economic Implications of Contractual Incompleteness</seg>
<seg id="1461"> Asobserved, an incomplete contract will be revised and/or renegotiated asthe future unfolds.</seg>
<seg id="1462"> In fact, given that the partiescan fill in the gapsasthey go along, one may ask why contractual incompleteness matters. The reason is that the renegotiation process imposes several costs.</seg>
<seg id="1463"> Some of these are ex post costs, incurred at the renegotiation stage itself, and others are ex ante costs, incurred in anticipation of renegotiation.22</seg>
<seg id="1464"> First, the parties may engage in a great deal of haggling over the terms of the revised contract.</seg>
<seg id="1465"> Argument about division of surplus serves no overall productive purpose and, to the extent that it is time-consuming and wastes resources, it is inefficient.23</seg>
<seg id="1466"> Second, not only may the process of ex post bargaining be costly, but, to the extent that the parties have asymmetric information, they may fail to reach an efficient agreement.</seg>
<seg id="1467"> Suppose that at the recontracting stage Fisher Body knows the current cost of producing car bodies, but General Motors does not. (It knows only the probability distribution from which costs are drawn.) GM could ensure a supply of bodies by offering Fisher an attractive price (high enough to cover Fisher's costs with probability one).</seg>
<seg id="1468"> However, this is expensive, since GM is overpaying in states of the world where costs are low.</seg>
<seg id="1469"> GM may prefer to offer a low price, knowing that in high-cost states Fisher will not supply (even though the cost of bodies is less than their value to GM).</seg>
<seg id="1470"> In other words, profit-maximizing behaviour by GM leadsto absence of profitable trade with positive probability.</seg>
<seg id="1471"> It is worth noting at thispoint that neither of the above ex post costs would be significant if the partiescould easily switch to new trading partners at the renegotiation stage.</seg>
<seg id="1472"> Any attempt by Fisher (resp. GM) to haggle for an increased share of surplus would fail if GM (Fisher) could switch to an equally efficient alternative partner.</seg>
<seg id="1473"> Similarly, asymmetric information doesnot lead to ex post inefficiencies if, after a bargaining breakdown, the parties can (costlessly) start the process again with new identical partners; if GM's low-price offer is turned down, GM will either eventually find a low-cost supplier, or, if all suppliers have high costs, GM will learn this and raise its price.</seg>
<seg id="1474"> Thus, if the two costs described are high, it must be because there is something binding the partners together and making it difficult for them to switch at the recontracting stage.</seg>
<seg id="1475"> The leading candidate for that ˇ®somethingˇŻ isan ex ante relationship-specific investment, that is, a prior investment, which creates value if the parties' economic relationship extends over time, but does not if the parties split up.</seg>
<seg id="1476"> In the case of GM and Fisher, examples of relationship-specific investments might be GM's decision to locate a car assembly plant near Fisher's factory, Fisher's decision to locate its factory near GM's assembly plant, GM's decision to spend money on developing cars that rely on the bodies supplied by Fisher, and Fisher's decision to spend money figuring out how to reduce the cost of producing bodies for GM (but not for car manufacturersin general).</seg>
<seg id="1477"> Once the existence of relationship-specific investments is recognized, it becomes apparent that there can be a third cost of contractual incompleteness that may dwarf the haggling and ex post inefficiency costs discussed so far.</seg>
<seg id="1478"> Specifically, because contracts are incomplete, the parties may be deterred from making the relationship-specific investments that would be optimal in a ˇ®first-bestˇŻ world.</seg>
<seg id="1479"> Suppose it is efficient for Fisher to install machinery that enablesit to produce car bodies designed specifically for GM.</seg>
<seg id="1480"> In a comprehensive contracting world, the contract between GM and Fisher could be structured in such a way that Fisher would have an incentive to make the investment; one way to do this is to have a contract that fixesthe body price for the indefinite future, so that the gains from the investment accrue to Fisher.</seg>
<seg id="1481"> In an incomplete contracting world, however, such an arrangement may be impossible.</seg>
<seg id="1482"> Because of the difficulty of specifying quality and quantity very far in advance, both parties recognize that any long-term contract is incomplete and subject to renegotiation.</seg>
<seg id="1483"> Even if renegotiation proceeds smoothlyˇŞthat is, if problems due to haggling and asymmetric information do not ariseˇŞthe division of the gains from trade will depend on the ex post bargaining strengths of the parties rather than on what is specified in the initial contract or on economic efficiency.</seg>
<seg id="1484"> As a result, a party may be reluctant to invest because it fears expropriation by the other party at the recontracting stage, i.e. it fears that it will not cover its investment costs.</seg>
<seg id="1485"> For example, Fisher will worry that, once it has installed the special machinery, GM will use its bargaining power to set the price of car bodies close to Fisher's variable production costs, thus causing Fisher to make a loss on its initial fixed investment.</seg>
<seg id="1486"> Alternatively, GM may insist that Fisher's output satisfy very demanding quality criteria.</seg>
<seg id="1487"> Similarly, GM will worry that, once it has sunk the costs of developing a car that uses Fisher's bodies, Fisher will use its bargaining power to set the price of car bodies close to GM's variable production benefits, thus causing GM to make a loss on its initial fixed investment.</seg>
<seg id="1488"> Given each party's fear that the other party will ˇ®hold it upˇŻ at the renegotiation stage, the parties are likely to make investments that are relatively non-specific.</seg>
<seg id="1489"> For example, Fisher may decide to install general-purpose machinery that enablesit to supply a range of car manufacturers; thisway Fisher can play one manufacturer off against the other at the recontracting stage and achieve a higher price for its output.</seg>
<seg id="1490"> Similarly, GM may decide to develop a car that can use bodies produced by a number of different suppliers, rather than only Fisher bodies.</seg>
<seg id="1491"> Such decisions sacrifice some of the efficiency benefits of specialization, but, in a world of incomplete contracting, these efficiency losses are more than offset by the security that a general investment provides for each party.</seg>
<seg id="1492"> So far I have talked about the costs that plague the relationship between two independent, i.e. non-integrated, firms.</seg>
<seg id="1493"> The next question to ask is: how would these costs change if the two firmsmerged and became a single firm? Here transaction cost theory becomes somewhat vaguer.</seg>
<seg id="1494"> It is often suggested that haggling problems and hold-up behaviour are reduced in a single firm.</seg>
<seg id="1495"> However, the precise mechanism by which this happens is not usually spelled out.</seg>
<seg id="1496"> In the discussion of principal¨Cagent theory, I argued that it is unsatisfactory to assume that the informational structure changes directly as a result of a merger.</seg>
<seg id="1497"> In the same way, it is unsatisfactory to suppose that the agents automatically become less opportunistic. (Also, presumably opportunistic behaviour is not always reduced within the firm, since otherwise it would be optimal to carry out all economic activities within one huge firm.) If there islesshaggling and hold-up behaviour in a merged firm, it isimportant to know why.</seg>
<seg id="1498"> Transaction cost theory, as it stands, does not provide the answer.</seg>
<seg id="1499"> 2 The Property Rights Approach</seg>
<seg id="1500"> ALL the theories discussed in Chapter 1 suffer from the drawback that they do not explain what changes when two firmsmerge.</seg>
<seg id="1501"> I now describe a theoryˇŞthe property rightsapproachˇŞthat tries to address this question head-on.27 I divide the chapter into three parts.</seg>
<seg id="1502"> Section 1 provides a verbal description of the property rights approach.</seg>
<seg id="1503"> Section 2 develops a formal model under a very stylized set of assumptions.</seg>
<seg id="1504"> Finally, Section 3 discusses what light the theory can throw on actual organizational arrangements.</seg>
<seg id="1505"> A General Description</seg>
<seg id="1506"> Consider two firms, A and B, and imagine that firm A acquires firm B. Ask the following question: what exactly does firm A get for its money?</seg>
<seg id="1507"> At least in a legal sense, the answer seems straight-forward: firm A acquires, i.e. becomes the owner of, firm B's assets.</seg>
<seg id="1508"> Included in this category are firm B'smachines, inventories, buildings, land, patents, client lists, copyrights, etc.ˇŞthat is, all of firm B's physical or nonhuman assets.</seg>
<seg id="1509"> Excluded are the human assets of those people working for firm B; given the absence of slavery, the human capital of these workers belongs to them both before and after the acquisition.</seg>
<seg id="1510"> Why does ownership of physical or nonhuman assets matter? The answer is that ownership is a source of power when contracts are incomplete.</seg>
<seg id="1511"> To understand this, note that an incomplete contract will have gaps, missing provisions, or ambiguities, and so situations will occur in which some aspects of the uses of nonhuman assets are not specified.</seg>
<seg id="1512"> For example, a contract between General Motorsand Fisher Body might leave open certain aspectsof maintenance policy for Fisher machines, or might not specify the speed of Fisher's production line or the number of shifts per day, or whether GM's production process can be modified to accept Fisher's input more easily.</seg>
<seg id="1513"> Given that a contract will not specify all aspects of asset usage in every contingency, who has the right to decide about missing usages? According to the property rights approach, it is the owner of the asset in question who has this right.</seg>
<seg id="1514"> That is, the owner of an asset has residual control rights over that asset: the right to decide all usages of the asset in any way not inconsistent with a prior contract, custom, or law.</seg>
<seg id="1515"> In fact, possession of residual control rights is taken virtually to be the definition of ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1516"> This is in contrast to the more standard definition of ownership, whereby an owner possesses the residual income from an asset rather than its residual control rights.</seg>
<seg id="1517"> Note that this view of ownership seems to accord with common sense.</seg>
<seg id="1518"> For example, suppose I rent you my car for six monthsand during thisperiod you have an urge to install a CD player.</seg>
<seg id="1519"> It would generally be agreed that, if the contract is silent, you would have to ask my permission to do this; that is, the residual right to change the interior of the car would be mine asowner rather than yoursasrenter.</seg>
<seg id="1520"> Furthermore, even if the contract did make provisionsfor a CD player, there would be many other events and actions which would not be covered and with respect to which you would have to ask my permission.</seg>
<seg id="1521"> So, in the General Motors¨CFisher Body example, if the two companies are separate, GM has the right to decide whether to modify its production process, while Fisher can make the decisions about the speed of its own production line, number of shifts per day, or maintenance of its machines. (This is under the assumption that the contract is silent about these issues.) On the other hand, if GM acquires Fisher, then GM can make all the above decisions.</seg>
<seg id="1522"> Finally, if Fisher acquires GM, then all the decisions are in the hands of Fisher.</seg>
<seg id="1523"> To see the economic implications of different ownership arrangements, it is useful to focus on the third cost of contractual incompleteness described in Chapter 1, the distortion in relationship-specific investments.</seg>
<seg id="1524"> Suppose GM and Fisher have an initial contract that requires Fisher to supply GM with a certain number of car bodies every day.</seg>
<seg id="1525"> Imagine that demand for GM's cars now rises and GM requires additional car bodies.</seg>
<seg id="1526"> Suppose also that the initial contract is silent about this possibility, perhaps because of the difficulty of anticipating the circumstances of the demand increase.</seg>
<seg id="1527"> If Fisher is a separate company, GM must secure Fisher's permission to increase supply.</seg>
<seg id="1528"> That is, the status quo point in any contract renegotiation is where Fisher does not provide the extra bodies.</seg>
<seg id="1529"> GM does not have the right to go into Fisher's factory and set the production line to supply the extra bodies; Fisher, as owner, has this residual right of control.</seg>
<seg id="1530"> This is to be contrasted with the situation where Fisher is a subdivision or subsidiary of GM, so that GM owns Fisher's factory.</seg>
<seg id="1531"> In this case, if Fisher management refuses to supply the extra bodies, GM has the option to dismiss management and hire someone else to supervise the factory and supply extra bodies.</seg>
<seg id="1532"> (GM could even run Fisher itself on a temporary basis.) The status quo point in the contract renegotiation is therefore quite different.</seg>
<seg id="1533"> Formation of Assets</seg>
<seg id="1534"> Chapter 2 assumed that the assets were already in place.</seg>
<seg id="1535"> The analysis generalizes, however, to the case where they need to be constructed.</seg>
<seg id="1536"> It is useful to distinguish between two situations.</seg>
<seg id="1537"> In the first the construction costs are verifiable; e.g. the asset is purchased from a manufacturer at a (verifiable) price P.</seg>
<seg id="1538"> Under these conditions, the ownership decision can be separated from the construction decision.</seg>
<seg id="1539"> That is, suppose the model of Chapter 2 predicts that it is efficient for M1 to own a1 in the absence of construction. Then it will also be efficient for M1 to own a1 in the presence of constructionˇŞthe only issue is how the construction cost should be divided between M1 and M2 and this will depend on who gains more from the presence of a1 and on M1 and M2's relative date 0 bargaining positions.</seg>
<seg id="1540"> For example, if M1 gains greatly from a1, then M1 may pay most of P, whereas if M2 gains greatly from the economic relationship with M1ˇŞe.g. from her ability to hold M1 upˇŞthen M2 will pay most of P.</seg>
<seg id="1541"> The second case is when construction costs are themselves hard to verify.</seg>
<seg id="1542"> Under these conditions, construction is somewhat similar to the physical capital investments discussed in Section 6; in particular, because these construction costs are not verifiable, they cannot be shared. (If I agree to pay half the costs, you could spend my share on ˇ®inappropriateˇŻ construction.) Given this, the ownership and construction decisions become interlinked.</seg>
<seg id="1543"> Specifically, if I am going to own the asset, it may make sense for me also to build it, since if you build it I can hold you up at the completion stage.</seg>
<seg id="1544"> Integration, Information Dissemination and Co-Operation</seg>
<seg id="1545"> As noted in Chapter 1, it is sometimes argued that a benefit of carrying out activities within the firm is that information is exchanged more readily and parties are more willing to co-operate.</seg>
<seg id="1546"> In fact, some parts of the literature take the defining property of integration to be that informational asymmetries are removed within the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1547"> The incomplete contracting approach can provide some foundations for this idea.</seg>
<seg id="1548"> First, as observed in Section 2, an employer has more leverage over an employee than one independent contractor has over another, since the employer owns the assets the employee needs to be productive.</seg>
<seg id="1549"> Applying this idea to the case of information dissemination, one may conclude that an employee has a strong incentive to provide the information that an employer wants, if only so that the employee can establish himself as reliable, thereby increasing his future value to the employer (and hence possibly his wage).</seg>
<seg id="1550"> Second, Section 4 showed that employees' incentives within firms are likely to be low-powered, since employees do not have residual control rights.</seg>
<seg id="1551"> A disadvantage of this is that employees may have limited incentives to work hard or have good ideas because they do not receive the return from such activities.</seg>
<seg id="1552"> However, precisely because of this, they may be prepared to co-operate more.</seg>
<seg id="1553"> That is, someone with a low-powered incentive scheme may, say, be willing to help a fellow worker make an additional sale to a customer, or provide the information that makes such a sale possible, because she has little to lose.</seg>
<seg id="1554"> In contrast, someone on a higher-powered incentive scheme may not be prepared to help her fellow worker because she would prefer to make the sale herself.</seg>
<seg id="1555"> Each of these effects can go the other way, however.</seg>
<seg id="1556"> In some cases an employee may be less willing to supply information than an independent contractor, since, given that the employee has no control rights, his employer can use the information to take advantage of him (see e.g. Aghion and Tirole 1995).</seg>
<seg id="1557"> Similarly, two employees may compete rather than co-operate to the extent that only one of them will receive a promotion, and the employer as possessor of the residual control rights can decide which one it will be. (For a related phenomenon, see Milgrom 1988.)</seg>
<seg id="1558"> Thus, it is more accurate to say that integration changes the incentives of parties to reveal information and co-operate, rather than to say that it improves them.</seg>
<seg id="1559"> 4 A Discussion of the Foundations of the Incomplete Contracting Model</seg>
<seg id="1560"> IN this chapter I provide a discussion of the foundations of the incomplete contracting model used in Chapter 2.</seg>
<seg id="1561"> The discussion will be informal.</seg>
<seg id="1562"> I will not try to provide firm foundations for the modelˇŞthis is unfortunately too difficult at this stage.</seg>
<seg id="1563"> Instead, I will consider some natural ways to get round the incompleteness of contracts, and explain why in many cases they won't work.</seg>
<seg id="1564"> For a very interesting recent paper that adopts a more rigorous approach to some of the same issues, see Segal (1995).</seg>
<seg id="1565"> The chapter isdivided into three parts.</seg>
<seg id="1566"> First, the hold-up problem is considered in more detail.89 Next, a more general class of asset ownership structures than those in Chapter 2 is analysed.</seg>
<seg id="1567"> The chapter concludes with a consideration of how important the hold-up problem is for the property rights theory of the firm.</seg>
<seg id="1568"> Discussion of the Hold-Up Problem</seg>
<seg id="1569"> In Chapter 2 the existence of a hold-up problem was established under the assumption that the parties could not write a long-term contract.</seg>
<seg id="1570"> This section reexamines this assumption.</seg>
<seg id="1571"> It is useful to consider a simplified one-sided version of the investment story.</seg>
<seg id="1572"> Start with the case considered in Chapter 2 where the parties do not write a long-term contract at date 0.</seg>
<seg id="1573"> Instead, they wait until date 1 and bargain then about the type and price of the widget.</seg>
<seg id="1574"> The question is: can the parties write a date 0 contract to solve this hold-up problem? In two simple cases, the answer is yes.</seg>
<seg id="1575"> Case 1</seg>
<seg id="1576"> The Widget Type Can Be Described In Advance.Under these conditions it is easy for M1 and M2 to write a specific performance contract.</seg>
<seg id="1577"> Case 2.</seg>
<seg id="1578"> The Investment Can Be Verified.</seg>
<seg id="1579"> A second possibility is for the parties to contract on M1's date 0 investment rather than on the date 1 widget.</seg>
<seg id="1580"> The reason M1 does not invest in the absence of a contract is that 50 per cent of the gains from investment go to M2.</seg>
<seg id="1581"> M2 could compensate M1 for this by sharing in M1's investment costs.</seg>
<seg id="1582"> Cases 1 and 2 are fairly restrictive for two reasons.</seg>
<seg id="1583"> First, in practice, it may be difficult to specify the type and quality of input M1 requires in advance.</seg>
<seg id="1584"> The reason is that the nature of the input may depend on a number of factors occurring between dates0 and 1 (see below).</seg>
<seg id="1585"> Second, it may be hard to specify M1's investment obligations unambiguously in a contract.</seg>
<seg id="1586"> The investment M1 is meant to carry out may be hard to describe; it may be multi-dimensional and involve the expenditure of time and effort (say, in skill acquisition) as well as the expenditure of money (on physical capital).</seg>
<seg id="1587"> Given this, it may be hard for a court to determine whether M1 has fulfilled his contractual responsibilities.</seg>
<seg id="1588"> Note that, even if all expenditure is monetary, it is not enough for the court to look at M1's expenses since M1 could have spent the money in the wrong way.</seg>
<seg id="1589"> Note also that M2 cannot simply hand M1 a payment at date 0 and hope that M1 carries out his first-best investment.</seg>
<seg id="1590"> A lump-sum payment does not affect M1's investment incentive.</seg>
<seg id="1591"> It is useful to provide a (partial) formalization of the idea that the widget type cannot be specified in advance.</seg>
<seg id="1592"> Suppose that there are a very large number of equally likely states of the world S and a very large number of possible widgets, and in each state a different type of widget is required by M1.</seg>
<seg id="1593"> Ideally, the parties would write a contingent contract, stating which widget should be supplied in each state of the world.</seg>
<seg id="1594"> However, unless there is a shorthand for describing a type- s widget, this contract would have to consist of a list of S statements of the following form: in state 1, a widget with the following characteristics should be supplied . . . ; in state 2, a widget with the following (different) characteristics should be supplied . . . ; etc. If S isvery large and there is some cost of writing each statement (this could be just the cost of the paper used), then such a contract would be prohibitively expensive.</seg>
<seg id="1595"> Note also that, although the parties could reduce costs by indicating only what should happen in some states of the worldˇŞe.g. in the first 100 statesˇŞthere is little point in doing this if S is very large since the probability that one of these states will be relevant is negligible.</seg>
<seg id="1596"> The fact that the parties cannot write a contingent contract does not mean that they cannot achieve the first-best.</seg>
<seg id="1597"> Consider the following contract.</seg>
<seg id="1598"> The trouble with this solution to the hold-up problem is that it is extremely sensitive to the timing.95 Contract (A) works well if M1'srequest for M2 arrives just before production occurs.</seg>
<seg id="1599"> Suppose, however, that there is a period of time after M2 receivesM1'swidget proposal, but before production occurs. Then M2 can turn down M1'srequest, and renegotiate to supply the widget under better terms.</seg>
<seg id="1600"> The conclusion is that, if renegotiation is possible, a long-term contract does not solve the hold-up problem.</seg>
<seg id="1601"> In fact, the equilibrium level of investment under a long-term contract is just the same as it would be under no contract at all.</seg>
<seg id="1602"> Since renegotiation causes problems, it is natural to ask whether there is a way for the parties to commit not to renegotiate the original contract. (M2 would then have no incentive to decline M1'soffer.) Unfortunately, under the current legal system, it is not clear how M1 and M2 can do this.</seg>
<seg id="1603"> Suppose the parties write in their original contract that if they ever renegotiate M1 must pay M2 ˇę1 billion.</seg>
<seg id="1604"> Then the renegotiated contract can always include a clause that waives this penalty. (Moreover, under the current legal system the courts will enforce the renegotiated contract rather than the original contract.) The same is true if M1 and M2 agree to pay a third party ˇę1 billion if they renegotiate: the third party can agree to waive the penalty in the renegotiated contract. (She will never receive the billion pounds anyway.)</seg>
<seg id="1605"> Another possibility is that M2 could agree in the original contract that she will never make offers to M1 but will only respond to offers made by M1; in effect, she grants M1 100 per cent of the bargaining power in any contract renegotiation.</seg>
<seg id="1606"> This would clearly solve the hold-up problem in the one-sided investment case, but again it is hard to see how it could be enforced.</seg>
<seg id="1607"> Whatever the formal contract says, there is little to stop M2 from arranging an informal meeting with M1, and proposing a new contract that includes a clause waiving M2's promise not to speak.</seg>
<seg id="1608"> In this way M2 can recapture some of her bargaining power.</seg>
<seg id="1609"> I now briefly consider some other solutions to the hold-up problem, and some related ideas and literature.</seg>
<seg id="1610"> Revenue- or Cost-Sharing</seg>
<seg id="1611"> So far I have supposed R( i) and C* to be observable, but not verifiable.</seg>
<seg id="1612"> In fact, this assumption is not critical.</seg>
<seg id="1613"> Suppose R( i) and C* are verifiable. Then the parties can write a contract that makesM1'spayment to M2 depend on her costs or on M1's revenue. (That is, revenue- or cost-sharing becomes feasible.) However, such contracts do not solve the hold-up problem.</seg>
<seg id="1614"> To see why, let p be the price that M1 must pay if no trade occurs (that is, if revenues and costs both 0 equal zero).</seg>
<seg id="1615"> Call no trade at price p the ˇ®no-trade outcomeˇŻ.</seg>
<seg id="1616"> Then either party can trigger the no-trade outcome, with 0 the intention of renegotiating.</seg>
<seg id="1617"> M2 can do this by turning down M1'srequest; M1 can do this by asking for a ˇ®gold-plated widget which M2 cannot supply.</seg>
<seg id="1618"> Since either party can trigger the no-trade outcome, the logic used to derive (4.6) still holds.</seg>
<seg id="1619"> Hence the hold-up problem remains.</seg>
<seg id="1620"> The point is that revenue- / cost-sharing contracts give M1 and M2 each an option to trade under the existing contract but do not force them to trade under this contract.</seg>
<seg id="1621"> Third Parties</seg>
<seg id="1622"> So far I have focused on two-party contracts.</seg>
<seg id="1623"> Under certain assumptions, the presence of a third party can help.</seg>
<seg id="1624"> Suppose M1 and M2 agree that M1 will specify the kind of widget he wants at date 1, and M1 and M2 will then simultaneously send messages to the third party, T, about whether M1's request is appropriate (i.e. whether M1 has asked for a ˇ®regularˇŻ widget that costs M2 an amount C* to produce).</seg>
<seg id="1625"> If they both say that M1's request is appropriate, M2 must supply M1 with the widget and will receive C* from M1.</seg>
<seg id="1626"> If either says it is inappropriate, no trade occurs and M1 and M2 must each pay T a large fine.</seg>
<seg id="1627"> It is easy to see that this contract can achieve the first-best. (More precisely, there is one equilibrium of the contracting game (ˇ®truth-tellingˇŻ) that achieves the first-best.) However, this relies on T being honest.</seg>
<seg id="1628"> In particular, there is a great incentive for T to collude with M2 (or M1).</seg>
<seg id="1629"> M2 can announce that the widget is inappropriate even when it isn't, and M2 and T can share the large fine (perhaps having written a side-contract in advance for this purpose).</seg>
<seg id="1630"> In fact, it is not difficult to show that, if (perfect) collusion is possible, the introduction of a third party achieves nothing, since T will in effect merge with M1 or M2 and the two-party case again applies.</seg>

(I continue to rule out third parties on the grounds that they may collude with M1 or M2.) However, this is not the case.</seg>

<seg id="1637"> The Role of Bounded Rationality</seg>
<seg id="1638"> In formalizing the hold-up problem, I have assumed that the parties are unboundedly rational in the sense that they can calculate the consequences of any action they take. (They know R( i) and C* and can figure out (4.2).) At the same time, I have supposed that transaction costs cause contractual incompleteness.</seg>
<seg id="1639"> There is a tension in these positions, but they are not inconsistent.</seg>
<seg id="1640"> There is no contradiction in assuming on the one hand that the parties cannot write a contract that specifies widget type and investment in a sufficiently unambiguous manner that a court can enforce it, but assuming on the other hand that the parties can figure out the utility consequences of their inability to write an unambiguous contract.</seg>
<seg id="1641"> For example, in the house transaction described in the introduction, there are many contingencies that no contract can include. (See n. 3 of the Introduction for one of these.) But it does not follow from this that my wife and I cannot factor these contingencies into our expected utility; for instance, we might discount our utility by some amount to cover outcomes we cannot identify individually.</seg>
<seg id="1642"> In reality, a great deal of contractual incompleteness is undoubtedly linked to the inability of parties not only to contract very carefully about the future, but also to think very carefully about the utility consequences of their actions.</seg>
<seg id="1643"> It would therefore be highly desirable to relax the assumption that parties are unboundedly rational.</seg>
<seg id="1644"> Apart from making the analysis more realistic, such an approach would have other benefits too.</seg>
<seg id="1645"> First, it might permit the relaxation of the assumption that the parties cannot commit not to renegotiate their contract.</seg>
<seg id="1646"> In a world of bounded rationality, parties are unlikely to want to make such a commitment since they will wish to preserve their option to revise their contract as unanticipated events occur.</seg>
<seg id="1647"> Second, for similar reasons, a bounded rationality approach might allow the relaxation of the assumption that incorruptible third parties cannot be found to improve on two-party contracts.</seg>
<seg id="1648"> Third parties are useful to sustain message games in which M1 and M2 must pay a large penalty (to the third party) unless they agree on the appropriateness of input.</seg>
<seg id="1649"> However, such games will be less attractive in a world of bounded rationality where the parties may not agree either ex ante or ex post on what qualifies as ˇ®appropriateˇŻ input.</seg>
<seg id="1650"> It is worth noting that one can go only so far in dropping the assumption of rationality.</seg>
<seg id="1651"> If parties are too irrational, they may not realize that an investment today will be expropriated by an opportunistic trading partner tomorrow.</seg>
<seg id="1652"> As a result, a party may invest efficiently even when it is suboptimal for him to do so; i.e. the hold-up problem may disappear! In other words, the hold-up problemˇŞas well (probably) as the theory of asset ownership in Chapter 2ˇŞseems to rely on a minimal degree of foresight about the future utility consequences of current actions.</seg>
<seg id="1653"> The Role of Asymmetric Information (Or Lack Thereof)</seg>
<seg id="1654"> Asymmetric information has played a very limited role in the analysis of the hold-up problem (and plays a very limited role in the analysis in this book more generally).</seg>
<seg id="1655"> Variables such as R (i), C*, and i were supposed to be observable but not verifiable.</seg>
<seg id="1656"> I should make it clear that asymmetric information was not deemphasized because it was judged to be unimportant; rather, this was done because the analysisˇŞparticularly the analysis of renegotiationˇŞis much more tractable under the assumption of symmetric information.</seg>
<seg id="1657"> Also, from a purely economic point of view it is most natural to study the hold-up problem in the context of symmetric information.</seg>
<seg id="1658"> The hold-up problem is most acute when M2 observes M1's investment (or observes the returns from this investment) and can exploit M1's eagerness for the widget to extract a high price.</seg>
<seg id="1659"> A hold-up problem can also arise if M2 is unsure about whether M1 has invested, but it is less extreme (see Tirole 1986 a).</seg>
<seg id="1660"> There is one place, however, where asymmetric information might be very useful.</seg>
<seg id="1661"> Suppose that M2's cost C* is private information. Consider a contract that states that M1 will specify a widget type (and widget price) at date 1, and M2 can either agree to M1's suggestion or decline it.</seg>
<seg id="1662"> In the case of symmetric information, this contract achieves the first-best if M1 has all the bargaining power.</seg>
<seg id="1663"> With asymmetric information, however, the first-best is not achieved since M1 may offer a price below M2'scostsand M2 may decline it.</seg>
<seg id="1664"> But this means that M2 may pretend that her costs are high even when they are not in order to force M1 to pay more.</seg>
<seg id="1665"> Asa result, the hold-up problem may reappear. (I say ˇ®mayˇŻ because, as far as I know, this case has not been analysed in the literature.) In other words, asymmetric information may help to justify the analysis of Chapter 2.</seg>
<seg id="1666"> Related Literature</seg>
<seg id="1667"> There are a number of recent articles in the literature showing how the hold-up problem can be solved when contracts are incomplete.</seg>
<seg id="1668"> Perhaps the most notable contribution is by Aghion et al. (1994b) (henceforth ADR).</seg>
<seg id="1669"> ADR show that under certain conditions the first-best can be achieved even when M2 and M1 both invest.</seg>
<seg id="1670"> However, they make two assumptions that I do not make.</seg>
<seg id="1671"> First, they suppose that M1 and M2 trade a standard widget at date 1, whose characteristics are already known at date 0.</seg>
<seg id="1672"> This means that the parties can write a specific performance contract, whereby M2 agrees to deliver a suitable number of widgets to M1 for a price, and this can serve as a starting point for renegotiation.</seg>
<seg id="1673"> ADR suppose that M1 and M2's benefit and cost functions are uncertain ex ante and so the optimal number of widgets to trade varies with the state of the world.</seg>
<seg id="1674"> This means that the parties will almost always renegotiate to a number of widgets different from.</seg>
<seg id="1675"> However, even though renegotiation will take place, an appropriate choice of will ensure that one of the partiesˇŞM2, sayˇŞhas the right ex ante incentive to invest. Thus, one side of the hold-up problem is solved.</seg>
<seg id="1676"> In contrast, in our model there are no standard widgets and so in effect =0.</seg>
<seg id="1677"> This means that neither party's investment pays off in the absence of renegotiation, and hence neither M1 nor M2 can be given the right ex ante incentives through a specific performance contract.</seg>
<seg id="1678"> Second, ADR show that efficient investment by the other partyˇŞM1, sayˇŞcan be achieved by designing the date 1 bargaining game appropriately.</seg>
<seg id="1679"> ADR consider an alternating offer bargaining game.</seg>
<seg id="1680"> To understand how their game works in the model of this chapter, imagine that date 1 is divided into two parts, ˇ®the beginning' and 'the endˇŻ.</seg>
<seg id="1681"> Suppose that, at the beginning of date 1, M1 makes an offer to M2.</seg>
<seg id="1682"> M2 has three choices: to accept M1's offer (in which case the bargaining game is over); to reject M1's offer (in which case the bargaining game continues); or to select an outside option, which corresponds to no trade (in which case trade never occurs).</seg>
<seg id="1683"> If the bargaining game continues, then at the end of date 1 M2 makes an offer, which M1 either accepts or rejects.</seg>
<seg id="1684"> This ends the bargaining.</seg>
<seg id="1685"> There is no discounting.</seg>
<seg id="1686"> Given the above timing, the following ex ante contract providesM1 with all the bargaining power.</seg>
<seg id="1687"> Set the no-trade price to be zero if no trade is triggered at the beginning of date 1 through M2 exercising her outside option.</seg>
<seg id="1688"> However, set the no-trade price to be - D, where D is a large positive number, if M2 first rejects M1's offer and then has her own offer rejected.</seg>
<seg id="1689"> D is essentially a large damage payment that M2 must make to M1 if bargaining continues to the end of date 1, and then breaks down.</seg>
<seg id="1690"> To see why this gives M1 all the bargaining power, note that M2 will always prefer to exercise her outside option, worth zero, rather than continue with the bargaining, given the large damage payment D. It follows that M1 need only offer M2 an amount p= C* to persuade her to accept his first offer at the beginning of date 1.</seg>
<seg id="1691"> The ADR solution is ingenious, but it depends on some strong assumptions.</seg>
<seg id="1692"> In reality, it is hard to find anything corresponding to the outside option of no trade (for ever).</seg>
<seg id="1693"> Who enforces this outcome, i.e. who ensures that the bargaining game ends at this point and that M2 cannot make any further offers? Another interpretation of the ADR procedure is that, instead of there being an outside option, the damage payment is made to depend on how many offersM2 makes.</seg>
<seg id="1694"> That is, M2 pays no damages if she turns down M1'soffer and makes no further offer, and she pays D if she does make another offer and it is turned down.</seg>
<seg id="1695"> However, this version supposes that, in the event of a dispute, the courts can determine how many offers M2 has made.</seg>
<seg id="1696"> This may not be reasonable if M2 can make secret offers.</seg>
<seg id="1697"> That is, having turned down M1's offer, M2 could come to M1 on the sly and propose that M1 pay her R( i) for the widget and waive the damage payment D. M1 may as well accept this offer since he will not receive D anyway. (There will be no proof that M2 made the second offer if it is rejected.)</seg>
<seg id="1698"> The conclusion is that the ADR procedure is likely to be valid only in particular situations.</seg>
<seg id="1699"> Under more general conditions, the hold-up problem reappears and the analysis of Chapter 2 becomes relevant.</seg>
<seg id="1700"> Discussion of the Property Rights Model of Chapter 2</seg>
<seg id="1701"> Given a justification for the hold-up problem, it is not difficult to provide some (tentative) support for the model of Chapter 2.</seg>
<seg id="1702"> Assume as in Chapter 2 that M1 requires an input from M2 and also that each party can make an investment that affects the benefits and costs of this input. (M1 chooses i and M2 chooses e. ) Suppose also that the marginal returns from investment depend on how the nonhuman assets a1 and a2 are used.</seg>
<seg id="1703"> Moreover, there is a huge number of potential, hard-to-describe uses of these assets, only one of which, say, will be relevant in a particular state of the world.</seg>
<seg id="1704"> (It is supposed that each use is equally likely to be relevant.) Then a contract (of finite length) that attempts to specify particular uses will, with probability close to 1, specify only irrelevant ones, and hence what matters is who has residual rights of controlˇŞthe right to choose asset uses not specified in the contract.</seg>
<seg id="1705"> Given a particular ownership allocation, the parties will bargain over the gains from trade, as in Chapter 2.</seg>
<seg id="1706"> The logic of the present chapter can be used to show that the parties cannot gain from writing a long-term contract that specifies a no-trade price p0 and a trade price p1.</seg>
<seg id="1707"> Other possibilities are stochastic ownership arrangements and option-to-own contracts, or more generally ownership arrangements that are contingent on messages sent by the parties between dates 0 and 1.</seg>
<seg id="1708"> Stochastic ownership is helpful because it can act as a smoothing device.</seg>
<seg id="1709"> It is clear from the exampleˇŞas well as from the discussion of stochastic schemesˇŞthat the analysis of ownership structures in Chapter 2 is not complete.</seg>
<seg id="1710"> In particular, Chapter 2 derives the optimality of non-integration, type 1 integration, and type 2 integration relative to each other, but not relative to all possible arrangements.</seg>
<seg id="1711"> At the same time, the main ideas behind the analysisˇŞif not all the detailsˇŞare likely to be robust.</seg>
<seg id="1712"> First, the more sophisticated schemes that are stochastic or depend on messages are still examples of ownership.</seg>
<seg id="1713"> In an important sense, the main lesson of Chapter 2 is that the allocation of scarce ownership rightsˇŞthat is, the allocation of residual rights of controlˇŞmatters when contracts are incomplete.</seg>
<seg id="1714"> This continues to be true when stochastic and message-dependent ownership structures are allowed for.</seg>
<seg id="1715"> Second, it seems probable that Proposition 2 will generalize to the case of stochastic or message-dependent ownership structures: that is, it will still be the case that, if only one party's investment matters, or only one party's investment decision is elastic, then that party should own both assets; that if assets are strictly complementary then they should be under common ownership; and that if assets are independent then they should be separately owned.</seg>
<seg id="1716"> Thus, while more sophisticated ownership arrangements may be an interesting embellishment to the analysis of Chapter 2, they do not undermine the message of that analysis.</seg>
<seg id="1717"> More on the Role of the Hold-Up Problem</seg>
<seg id="1718"> I have stressed that there are two crucial ingredients of the property rights approach: incomplete contracts, and residual rights of control over nonhuman assets.</seg>
<seg id="1719"> In the model of Chapter 2, the first ingredient was important because it led to a hold-up problem, given the non-contractibility of relationship-specific investments, while the second was important because it implied that changes in ownership could affect the severity of this hold-up problem.</seg>
<seg id="1720"> It is worth pointing out that, although the hold-up problem is a useful vehicle for developing the property rights approach, it is not an essential part of the approach.</seg>
<seg id="1721"> That is, even in the absence of a hold-up problem, asset ownership would still generally matter.</seg>
<seg id="1722"> What is required for a theory of asset ownership is that there is some inefficiency in the economic relationship, which the allocation of residual control rights can influence: the inefficiency could be an ex post one rather than an ex ante one.</seg>
<seg id="1723"> It is worth briefly mentioning some other ways to generate inefficiency.</seg>
<seg id="1724"> 1.One obvious approach is to suppose that there is asymmetric information at date 1 (plus incomplete contracts at date 0). (See the discussion in Ch. 1, ˇě3.) For example, M1 may know the value to him of the widget supplied by M2, whereasM2 may not; or M2 may know her production costs, while M1 may not.</seg>
<seg id="1725"> Under these conditions, ownership matters since it determines the outcome if bargaining breaks down, i.e. the parties' no-trade payoffs.</seg>
<seg id="1726"> Furthermore, the no-trade payoffs matter, since with positive probability bargaining will break down given the asymmetry</seg>
<seg id="1727"> 2. An even simplerˇŞalthough more controversialˇŞway to generate ex post inefficiency is to suppose that there is no (explicit) asymmetric information, but that there is a positive probability that (for unexplained reasons) M1 and M2 will simply 'not get along at date 1'.</seg>
<seg id="1728"> Assume that in this case the economic relationship breaks down irretrievably. (The parties no longer talk to each other and trade elsewhere.) Under these conditions, ownership again matters since it determines who can take what assets from the relationship if breakdown occurs.</seg>
<seg id="1729"> 3. A related approach is to suppose that the parties, rather than being opportunistic, simply have different views about the returns from various asset usages and hence disagree about how the assets should be employed; moreover, bargaining does not resolve these differences.</seg>
<seg id="1730"> Such an approach probably requires a departure from common knowledge, since otherwise the parties would go on talking until agreement was reached.</seg>
<seg id="1731"> However, a role is again provided for asset ownership since whoever has the right to determine an asset's use will exercise this right if disagreement persists (which it will some of the time).</seg>
<seg id="1732"> All these approaches seem worth exploring in future work.</seg>
<seg id="1733"> The end result may be a theory of integration that is richer and more realistic than the one described in Chapter 2, even if it may be 'messier' and somewhat less tractable.</seg>
