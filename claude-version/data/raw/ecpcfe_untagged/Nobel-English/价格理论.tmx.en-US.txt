<seg id="7">MEANING OF ECONOMICS: ECONOMIC THEORY Economics is the science of how a particular society solves its economic problems.</seg>
<seg id="8">An economic problem exists whenever scarce means are used to satisfy alternative ends.</seg>
<seg id="9">If the means are not scarce, there is no problem at all; there is Nirvana.</seg>
<seg id="10">If the means are scarce but there is only a single end, the problem of how to use the means is a technological problem.</seg>
<seg id="11">No value judgments enter into its solution;</seg>
<seg id="12">only knowledge of physical and technical relationships.</seg>
<seg id="13">For example, suppose given amounts of iron, labor, ere. are available and are to be used to build an engine of maximum horsepower.</seg>
<seg id="14">This is a purely technical problem that requires knowledge solely of engineering and of physical science.</seg>
<seg id="15">Alternatively, let the objective be to build the "best" engine, where the concept of best involves not only horsepower, but also weight, size, etc. There is no longer a single end.</seg>
<seg id="16">No amount of purely physical and technical knowledge can yield a solution, since such knowledge cannot tell you how much power it is "worth" sacrificing to save a certain amount of weight.</seg>
<seg id="17">This is an economic problem, involving value judgments.</seg>
<seg id="18">This concept of an economic problem is a very general one and goes beyond matters ordinarily thought of as belonging to economics.</seg>
<seg id="19">For example, according to this conception an individual is dealing with an economic problem when he decides how to allocate his leisure time between alternative uses.</seg>
<seg id="20">Indeed, strictly speaking there is hardly any problem that is purely technological.</seg>
<seg id="21">Even in the case cited above, the engineer building the engine will have alternative ends, thinking about other things, making his work pleasant, etc., and these will affect his decision about how hard to work on the stated technological problem.</seg>
<seg id="22">This concept of an economic problem is also broad in the sense that it covers equally the problems in a Robinson Crusoe economy, in a backward agricultural economy, or in a modern industrial society.</seg>
<seg id="23">Economics, by our definition, is not concerned with all economic problems.</seg>
<seg id="24">It is a social science, and is therefore concerned primarily with those economic problems whose solution involves the cooperation and interaction of different individuals.</seg>
<seg id="25">It is concerned with problems involving a single individual only insofar as the individual's behavior has implications for or effects upon other individuals.</seg>
<seg id="26">Furthermore, it is concerned not with the economic problem in the abstract, but with how a particular society solves its economic problems.</seg>
<seg id="27">Formally, the economic problem is the same for a Robinson Crusoe economy, a backward agricultural economy, a modem industrial society organized on a communistic basis, and a modern industrial society organized on a capitalistic basis.</seg>
<seg id="28">Butˇ¤ these different societies use different institutional arrangements to solve their economic problems.</seg>
<seg id="29">Thus there is need for a different economics - or a different chapter in economics - for each kind of society.</seg>
<seg id="30">There turns out, in fact, to be much that is common to the various chapters, but this cannot be required in advance;</seg>
<seg id="31">it is, rather, one of the conclusions of economic science.</seg>
<seg id="32">Our definition of economics can be viewed as something of a compromise between a completely general definition of the economic problem and an opposing desire for concreteness of application.</seg>
<seg id="33">Differences in the institutional arrangements that are used to solve economic problems can be illustrated by reference to our own society.</seg>
<seg id="34">One may think of our society as consisting of four sectors, each using a conceptually different arrangement: the house- hold sector, the sector consisting of non-profit institutions, and the market sector.</seg>
<seg id="35">A very substantial fraction, probably more than half, of all resources, broadly defined, are used in the household sphere.</seg>
<seg id="36">But for the most part these uses of resources raise no social problem, at least not for economics.</seg>
<seg id="37">For the rest, the bulk of resources are used in the market sphere.</seg>
<seg id="38">These notes will concentrate mostly on the market sector.</seg>
<seg id="39">The notion of free in the tennfree- enterprise" should be interpreted as the freedom to set up an enterprise rather than the freedom to do anything one wishes with his enterprise including preventing others from setting up enterprises.</seg>
<seg id="40">Economics is sometimes divided into two parts: positive economics and nonnative economics.</seg>
<seg id="41">The former deals with how the economic problem is solve the latter deals with how the economic problem should be solved.</seg>
<seg id="42">For example, the effects of price or rent control on the distribution of income are problems of positive economics.</seg>
<seg id="43">On the other hand, the desirability of these effects on income distribution is a problem of normative economics.</seg>
<seg id="44">Economic theory, like all theory, may be thought of in two ways.</seg>
<seg id="45">It may be thought of as a language or filing system, or it may be thought of as a set of substantive empirical propositions.</seg>
<seg id="46">With respect to theory in the first meaning, the relevant question to be asked is usefulness and not rightness or wrongness.</seg>
<seg id="47">The proposition that price is deter mined by the interaction of demand and supply forces is merely an attempt to set up a useful filing system within which there can easily be placed under the headings "demand" or "supply" anyone of the forces affecting price.</seg>
<seg id="48">The usefulness of this filing system will in turn depend on the substantive fact whether a listing of the forces operating on demand contains few elements in common with a listing of the forces operating on supply.</seg>
<seg id="49">Economic theory as a set of substantive propositions contains propositions which are, in principle, capable of being tested because they attempt to be predictive.</seg>
<seg id="50">The definition of a demand curve is theory as language.</seg>
<seg id="51">However, the statement that the demand curve slopes downward to the right is theory as a substantive empirical proposition.</seg>
<seg id="52">It has empirically observable consequences, whereas the definition of a demand curve does not.</seg>
<seg id="53">Theory as language coincides with Marshall's engine of analysis.</seg>
<seg id="54">His objective, as well as that of any other investigator, is to construct a language that will yield as many substantive propositions as possible.</seg>
